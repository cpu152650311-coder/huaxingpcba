HTS and HS Codes: Getting Classification Right

The single most expensive mistake in PCB importing is misclassification. A wrong HS code triggers incorrect duty rates, potential penalties, and delayed clearance. Bare PCBs and assembled PCBA fall under different tariff headings with significantly different duty treatment.

1

Bare PCB — HS 8534.00

Unpopulated printed circuit boards (no components mounted) fall under HS 8534.00. In the US, the 10-digit HTS code is 8534.00.0020 for rigid PCBs with ≤4 layers, or 8534.00.0040 for multilayer (>4 layers). These are covered under Section 301 List 3, attracting an additional 25% ad valorem duty on top of the base MFN rate. The EU uses CN code 8534 00 11 (≤4 layers) and 8534 00 19 (>4 layers) with a base rate of 0% — meaning EU buyers face significantly lower tariff costs than US buyers for bare PCBs.

2

Assembled PCBA — HS 8473.30 or 8537.10

Once components are mounted, the classification shifts. PCBAs with a dedicated function (e.g., a motor controller board) often fall under the end-product's tariff heading. General-purpose assembled boards typically use HS 8473.30 (parts for automatic data processing machines) or HS 8537.10 (electrical control panels). US HTS codes: 8473.30.1180 (ADP parts) or 8537.10.9170 (control panels ≤1,000V). The Section 301 additional duty varies by list — List 1 covers 8473 at 25%, while 8537 falls under List 3 also at 25%. Always confirm with your customs broker. See our PCB assembly process guide for how assembled boards differ from bare PCBs.

3

Flex and Rigid-Flex PCBs — Same Chapter, Different Subheading

Flexible and rigid-flex PCBs also fall under HS 8534, but with different subheadings: 8534.00.0080 in the US for flexible PCBs. The same Section 301 duties apply. For detailed flex PCB specifications, see our flex PCB design guide and rigid-flex manufacturing overview.

Key Takeaway: EU importers pay 0% base duty on bare PCBs (HS 8534). US importers face 0% MFN base + 25% Section 301 additional duty = 25% effective rate. PCBA tariffs depend heavily on the end-function classification — get a binding ruling from CBP before large shipments.

EU Import Duty Structure for PCBs

European Union importers benefit from significantly lower tariff rates on Chinese PCBs compared to their US counterparts. The EU's Common Customs Tariff applies uniformly across all 27 member states:

EU buyers should focus less on duties and more on VAT optimization. Import VAT (19% in Germany, 21% in Netherlands, 20% in France, 22% in Italy) is recoverable for VAT-registered businesses through the reverse-charge mechanism, but it affects cash flow. Working with a supplier who provides proper commercial invoices and proof of export is essential for smooth VAT recovery. The Landed Cost Formula: What You Actually Pay

Unit price FOB Shenzhen tells only part of the story. Landed cost — the total delivered cost per board at your warehouse door — is what matters for budgeting and margin calculation. Here's the formula:

Landed Cost = (Unit Price × Quantity) + Freight + Insurance + Customs Duties + Brokerage Fees + Port/Destination Charges

1

Freight — Air vs Sea: The 5× Cost Gap

Air freight from Shenzhen to US West Coast runs $3.50–$6.50/kg for PCB shipments (2026 rates). Sea freight LCL (less-than-container-load) from Shenzhen to Los Angeles costs approximately $0.80–$1.50/kg but takes 18–25 days vs 5–7 days air. For a 100kg PCB shipment, air freight adds $350–$650; sea freight adds $80–$150. For urgent prototypes, quick-turn PCB manufacturing with air freight is the standard. For production runs, sea freight LCL with consolidation typically optimizes cost.

2

Customs Brokerage — Fixed Fee, Variable Value

US customs brokerage fees range from $75–$200 per entry for standard PCB shipments. Complex classifications (PCBA with multiple HTS codes) may incur higher fees. Bond charges (single-entry bond ≈ 0.5% of value or $50 minimum) add to this. EU brokerage fees vary by country but typically €80–€200 per entry.

3

Insurance — Small Cost, Big Protection

Marine cargo insurance costs approximately 0.3–0.5% of CIF value. On a $25,000 PCB shipment, that's $75–$125 — negligible compared to the risk of damage or loss. Always insure. Most freight forwarders include basic coverage, but verify the per-kg liability limit covers high-value PCBA.

4

Unit FOB price: $12.00/board. Quantity: 1,000 pcs. Total FOB: $12,000.
Air freight (80kg): +$440 (at $5.50/kg)
Insurance (0.4%): +$50

For sea freight (80kg at $1.20/kg): Total landed $15,096 → $15.10/board (25.8% over FOB).

Cost Optimization Strategies for PCB Importers

1

Consolidate Shipments to Hit Volume Thresholds

Combine multiple PCB orders into one larger shipment. Freight costs per kg drop sharply at the 100kg and 300kg air freight breakpoints. If you order PCBs quarterly, consider placing 3 months of demand in one shipment — the freight savings often outweigh the carrying cost of inventory. For insights on ordering strategy, read our PCB price negotiation guide.

2

Leverage the De Minimis Threshold for Samples

US Section 321 ($800 de minimis) and EU €150 low-value consignment relief allow duty-free entry for small shipments. Structure prototype orders into separate shipments staying under these thresholds. For 5–10 prototype PCBs at typical unit prices of $30–$80, each order falls comfortably within de minimis. This is why our prototype vs production comparison recommends treating prototypes as a separate logistics event.

3

Use Correct Incoterms

Specifying FOB Shenzhen (Incoterms 2020) means the supplier handles export clearance and delivers to the carrier you nominate — you control freight and insurance. DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) shifts all import responsibility to the supplier but typically comes with a 10–15% premium. For first-time buyers, DDP simplifies the process; for repeat purchasers, FOB + your own freight forwarder typically saves 8–12%. Ask your supplier for both options and compare. See our PCB quote comparison guide for how to evaluate cost proposals that include logistics.

4

Verify Supplier's Export Documentation

Incorrect commercial invoices are the #1 cause of customs delays. Your supplier should provide: commercial invoice with accurate HS codes and country of origin, packing list with weights and dimensions, bill of lading or air waybill, and any required certificates (RoHS, REACH, UL). Our PCB supplier audit checklist includes export documentation capability as a scored criterion.

Section 301 Outlook and Risk Management

The Section 301 tariffs on Chinese PCBs have been in place since 2018–2019 and remain at 25% in 2026. While the USTR periodically reviews exclusions, PCBs and electronic assemblies have not benefited from significant relief. The strategic implication: budget for the 25% additional duty as a permanent cost, not a temporary surcharge.

For buyers concerned about tariff exposure, consider these mitigations: (1) explore whether your assembled PCBA qualifies under a different HTS code with lower Section 301 rates through a formal CBP ruling; (2) for very high-value production, evaluate partial assembly in China with final configuration in a third country — though this typically only makes sense above $500K annual spend; (3) work with your PCB supplier to optimize panelization (fewer boards per panel = more efficient freight utilization). See our panelization guide for board-layout strategies that reduce shipping volume.

Summary: Budget 20–35% Above FOB for Landed PCB Cost

For US buyers, expect your landed cost to be 25–35% above the FOB unit price once duties, freight, and fees are included. EU buyers face a more favorable 5–15% landed cost premium (mainly freight + VAT cash flow). The fastest way to reduce this gap is consolidation — larger, less frequent shipments with air freight breakpoint optimization.
