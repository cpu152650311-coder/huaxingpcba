Why PCB Quotes Vary by 2-3× for the Same Board

Same Gerber files. Same stack-up. Same quantity. And yet quotes range from $1.95 to $4.85 per board. Here's why — and it's rarely about one factory being "cheaper."

1

Different Panel Utilization Rates

One factory nests your 45×38mm board into a standard 300×250mm panel and fits 28 units. Another uses a 250×200mm panel and fits only 15 units. Same panel cost — but 87% more boards per panel. That alone explains a 40-50% unit price difference. Always ask: "How many boards per panel, and what panel size are you using?"

2

NRE Charges: Buried or Separated?

Some suppliers embed NRE (tooling, stencil, programming) into the unit price. Others show it as a separate line item. A quote at $1.95/unit with $850 NRE vs $2.85/unit with $0 NRE — at 500 units, the "cheaper" option actually costs $1,825 vs $1,425. Always ask for a line-item NRE breakdown. Our PCB cost factors guide covers how NRE amortization works across volume tiers.

3

Testing Coverage: 100% vs Sample-Only

Flying probe testing on 100% of boards costs roughly $0.15-0.40/board depending on test point count. A 4-board sample test cuts that to $0.02/board — but leaves 99.2% of your boards untested. If your application can't tolerate a single open or short (medical, automotive, aerospace), that $0.38 "savings" is a liability. See our PCB testing methods comparison for when each approach makes sense.

4

Material Substitutions

Your stack-up specifies ITEQ IT-180A (Tg 180°C). One quote uses it. Another substitutes Shengyi S1000-2 (Tg 180°C equivalent but ~12% cheaper). Functionally equivalent for most applications — but if your IPC Class 3 requirements demand traceable material certs, the substitution may not be acceptable. Check if the quote states the actual laminate brand and grade.

Key Takeaway: The bottom-line number on a PCB quote is meaningless without the line-item breakdown. Always request a detailed BOM-level quote that separates: bare PCB cost, NRE, testing, surface finish surcharge, and shipping terms. If a supplier won't provide this — that's your first red flag.

5 Hidden Fees That Quietly Inflate Your PCB Quote

These line items rarely appear in the initial quote. They show up in the final invoice — or worse, after production has started. Here's what to ask about upfront:

1

Panel Utilization Penalty

If your board dimensions leave more than 15-20% wasted panel area, some factories add a "low utilization surcharge" of $15-50 per panel. A 38×22mm board on a 300×250mm panel wastes 89% — expect a penalty. Ask: "What panel size are you using, and is there a utilization surcharge for my board dimensions?"

2

MOQ Traps — "Free" Engineering with Strings

A quote that includes "free DFM review" and "free engineering support" often comes with an unwritten condition: the MOQ is 500-1,000 units, and dropping below triggers retroactive NRE charges of $200-400. Always confirm: "If we order 100 units for the first run, does the NRE price change?"

3

Lead Time Premiums

Standard lead time for 4-6 layer boards from Shenzhen is 5-8 working days. Quotes under 3 days typically carry a 30-50% premium. If you need 24-hour quick-turn, expect to pay 2-3× the standard price. See our quick-turn PCB guide for realistic timelines and costs.

4

Via Filling & Special Process Surcharges

Via-in-pad with copper filling? That's an extra $0.08-0.15/via. Blind vias? Add $0.50-2.00/board per lamination cycle. Controlled depth drilling (backdrill)? $0.15-0.30 per hole. If your design uses these — and the quote doesn't mention them — you're either getting a pleasant surprise or the factory didn't read your stack-up. Our via technology guide explains which via types trigger surcharges.

5

Certification & Compliance Documentation

UL listing, RoHS compliance certificates, ISO 9001 traceability reports — standard documentation is usually free. But full material certification (CMC) with batch-level traceability, or first-article inspection reports (FAIR) per AS9102 for aerospace applications, can add $50-200 per order. If your industry requires it, confirm it's included in the quote.

Red Flag: If a quote is significantly cheaper than all others and the seller says "everything is included" without specifying what "everything" covers — ask for the line-item breakdown in writing. A 40% price gap between the cheapest and the average almost always means something is excluded.

5 Red Flags in a PCB Quote

Before you accept any quote, scan for these warning signs. Any one of them should prompt a follow-up question. Two or more — get a second opinion.

1

"All-inclusive" without a breakdown

A quote that bundles everything into one number sounds convenient — but it's impossible to compare against other suppliers. You can't tell if they're including 100% testing or sample testing, ENIG or HASL imitation. Ask for the breakdown. If they refuse, walk away.

2

No mention of laminate brand or grade

"FR-4" is not a material specification — it's a category. Within FR-4, there are dozens of grades ranging from $3/sheet to $35/sheet. A quote that says only "FR-4, Tg 140" is ambiguous. Demand the brand and grade. Our PCB materials guide explains the real performance differences.

3

Price valid for "today only"

PCB commodity prices (copper, laminate, gold for ENIG) fluctuate, but day-to-day changes are negligible. A "this price expires in 24 hours" tactic is a sales pressure technique, not a reflection of market reality. Quotes should be valid for 7-14 days minimum.

4

Refuses to provide certifications or factory audit access

Any legitimate manufacturer can provide ISO 9001, IATF 16949, or UL certificates within 24 hours. A reluctance to share certifications — or outright refusal — is the single strongest red flag. Our supplier audit checklist covers the 10 things every buyer should verify before placing an order.

5

Shipping terms are vague

"Shipping: buyer's responsibility" or "FOB Shenzhen" without specifying the carrier, insurance, and delivery timeline leaves you exposed. Customs duties, import taxes, and freight forwarding costs can add 15-30% to your landed cost if not accounted for upfront. Always confirm: DDP (delivered duty paid) vs FOB vs EXW.

The 5-Minute Quote Review Checklist

Before you place an order, run every PCB quote through this checklist. If you can't answer a question from the quote document, pick up the phone or send an email — because that missing information is where the money leaks.

✓

NRE listed separately? Amortized correctly for my order quantity?

Divide NRE by your actual order quantity (not the quote's assumed volume). If NRE ÷ qty > $0.50/board, ask if it can be amortized over multiple orders.

✓

Testing: 100% or sample? Which method?

For volume above 500 units, flying probe on 100% is standard. For 5,000+ units, fixture testing becomes cost-effective. Confirm the method and coverage.

✓

Laminate brand and grade specified? Tg value confirmed?

Acceptable: ITEQ IT-180A, Shengyi S1000-2, Isola 370HR, Rogers 4350B. Unacceptable: "FR-4" or "standard laminate."

✓

Shipping: DDP, FOB, or EXW? Carrier and timeline stated?

DDP (delivered duty paid) is safest — the supplier handles customs and you pay one landed price. FOB leaves you responsible for freight, insurance, and import clearance.

✓

Certifications current? Factory name matches the cert?

Cross-check the factory name on the quote against the name on the ISO certificate. Audit firms have found cases where a trading company quotes under one name but manufactures under another with expired certifications. Our certifications guide explains what to verify.
