Shipping is the invisible link in every PCB procurement decision — and it affects your total landed cost, production timeline, and product quality in ways that are easy to overlook until something goes wrong. A shipment of perfectly fabricated PCBs that arrives damaged, delayed by three weeks, or held up in customs because of missing documentation is not a savings — it is a liability. Shipping is not just logistics; it is part of your quality chain.

At Huaxing, we ship to 30+ countries from our Shenzhen facility, using every major freight method available. We have sent prototypes via DHL that arrived in Silicon Valley in 48 hours, production batches via sea freight that cleared Rotterdam customs without a single query, and rail freight to Germany that beat air freight on total cost by 60%. The method you choose should match your order size, urgency, destination, and budget — and this guide will walk you through every factor that drives that decision.

Whether this is your first order from a Chinese PCB manufacturer or your hundredth, understanding the shipping landscape will save you money, reduce stress, and prevent the kind of surprises that derail production schedules.

Incoterms for PCB Buyers

Incoterms (International Commercial Terms) define who pays for what and where responsibility transfers from seller to buyer. Choosing the wrong Incoterm for your PCB order can cost you hundreds in unexpected destination charges — or leave you scrambling to arrange freight from a port you have never dealt with. These are the five Incoterms most relevant to PCB procurement.

EXW — Ex Works

You handle everything from our factory door. Under EXW, Huaxing makes the goods available at our Shenzhen facility, packed and ready for collection. You (or your freight forwarder) arrange and pay for export clearance, main carriage, import clearance, and final delivery. It is the cheapest Incoterm in terms of our quoted price — but the most work for you and the riskiest if you are unfamiliar with Chinese export procedures.

EXW makes sense for experienced buyers who already have a freight forwarder relationship, a consolidated shipping account, and familiarity with Chinese export documentation. For first-time buyers importing from China, EXW is not recommended — the administrative burden and risk of errors outweigh the modest cost savings. Huaxing will always help load your forwarder's truck at no extra charge, but the export declaration, customs clearance, and all onward logistics are your responsibility.

FOB — Free On Board

We handle export clearance and deliver to Shenzhen or Hong Kong port. You handle ocean freight and everything beyond. FOB is the most common Incoterm for sea freight PCB orders. Huaxing is responsible for all costs and risks until the goods are loaded onto the vessel at the named port of shipment (typically Yantian, Shekou, or Hong Kong). After that, the buyer manages the ocean freight, marine insurance, destination port charges, import clearance, and inland delivery.

FOB splits responsibility cleanly at the vessel's rail. It gives you control over the choice of shipping line and the ability to use your own freight forwarder for the ocean leg, while we handle the Chinese-side complexity (customs export declaration, port fees, loading charges). This is the recommended Incoterm for most production-volume sea freight orders — you get competitive ocean rates through your forwarder while we manage origin-side logistics.

CIF — Cost, Insurance & Freight

We pay freight and insurance to your destination port. You handle import clearance and duties. Under CIF, Huaxing arranges and pays for the main carriage and marine insurance to the named destination port (e.g., CIF Rotterdam, CIF Los Angeles). Risk transfers to the buyer once the goods cross the ship's rail at origin — meaning if cargo is damaged during the voyage, the buyer claims against the insurance policy that Huaxing purchased on your behalf.

CIF is attractive for buyers who want a predictable landed cost to their destination port without managing freight logistics. However, be aware that CIF gives you less control over the choice of carrier and routing. You also need to budget for destination-side costs that CIF does not cover: terminal handling charges (THC), customs brokerage fees, import duties/VAT, and inland trucking from the port to your facility. These can add 15-25% to the CIF price depending on the destination country.

DDP — Delivered Duty Paid

We handle EVERYTHING including import duties at destination. DDP is the most comprehensive — and most expensive — Incoterm. Huaxing is responsible for export clearance, main carriage, import clearance, all duties and taxes at destination, and final delivery to your door. You receive the goods at your facility, period. There are no hidden fees, no customs broker phone calls, and no last-mile logistics to arrange.

DDP is strongly recommended for first-time buyers importing from China. The premium over CIF or FOB is typically 10-20%, but it eliminates the risk of customs holds, unexpected duty assessments, and the administrative burden of navigating import procedures in your country. For small to mid-sized orders (under ~$5,000), the convenience premium of DDP is almost always worth it. For large production orders where every percentage point of cost matters, you may prefer to manage the destination side yourself through FOB or CIF and save the DDP markup. Huaxing offers DDP through our freight partners for most destinations worldwide.

DAP — Delivered At Place

We handle everything except import duties and taxes. DAP is DDP minus the destination duties and import VAT. Huaxing arranges export clearance, main carriage, import customs clearance, and delivery to your named place — but any import duties, taxes, or customs fees assessed at the destination border are your responsibility and payable upon arrival.

DAP offers a good balance of convenience and cost. You get door-to-door logistics managed by us, but you pay duties directly (which means you can reclaim VAT/GST as a business expense in most jurisdictions — something you cannot do under DDP because the duties are paid in our name). For VAT-registered businesses in the EU, UK, Australia, and similar jurisdictions, DAP is often preferable to DDP because you can recover the VAT on your next tax filing. For non-business buyers or entities that cannot reclaim import VAT, DDP may be simpler.

Quick Decision Guide: First-time buyer importing from China? Choose DDP (hassle-free, all-inclusive). Experienced buyer with a freight forwarder? Choose FOB (control over ocean freight). Need predictable cost to your port? Choose CIF. VAT-registered business wanting door-to-door? Choose DAP. Know exactly what you are doing with Chinese exports? Then EXW is an option.

How We Package PCBs for International Transit

International shipping subjects packages to vibration, compression, temperature swings, and rough handling that can destroy poorly packaged PCBs. A cracked BGA joint, a delaminated pad, or moisture-damaged board may not be visible on arrival — but it will fail in the field. Our packaging protocol is designed to ensure boards survive a multi-week journey through multiple cargo handlers, climate zones, and customs inspections.

1

Vacuum-Sealed Moisture Barrier Bags (MBB) with Desiccant + HIC Card

Every bare PCB and assembled PCBA is sealed in a moisture barrier bag meeting IPC/JEDEC J-STD-033 standards. Each bag includes a fresh desiccant pack and a Humidity Indicator Card (HIC) so you can verify moisture exposure on receipt. This is critical for boards stored before soldering — moisture absorption during transit can cause delamination and popcorning during reflow.

2

Anti-Static Bubble Wrap for ESD-Sensitive Assemblies

All PCBA assemblies are wrapped in pink anti-static (Type B) bubble wrap, with conductive (Type D) bags used for high-sensitivity components such as MOSFETs, MCUs, and RF modules. This protects against both physical shock and electrostatic discharge during transit and handling.

3

Rigid Cardboard Boxes with Corner Protection

Orders are packed in double-wall corrugated cardboard boxes rated for 200+ PSI burst strength. All corners are reinforced with edge protectors. Boxes are sized to the order — we do not use oversized boxes with loose fill. Void spaces are filled with crumpled kraft paper or foam inserts to prevent shifting.

4

Foam-in-Place for Heavy or Bulky Orders

For orders exceeding 15 kg or containing heavy components (transformers, heat sinks, large connectors), we use expanding polyurethane foam-in-place packaging. The foam conforms exactly to the contents, distributing impact forces evenly and preventing internal movement during transit.

5

Palletized and Shrink-Wrapped for Freight Shipments

All air freight, sea freight, and rail shipments are palletized on heat-treated ISPM 15 compliant wood or plastic pallets, secured with strapping, and wrapped in heavy-gauge polyethylene stretch film. Each pallet is labeled with its contents and weight for easy identification at freight terminals.

6

Each Package Labeled with Critical Information

Every box and pallet carries a durable, weatherproof label showing: your Purchase Order number, Huaxing part number, quantity enclosed, gross/net weight, handling instructions (ESD-sensitive, keep dry, this side up), and destination address in both English and local language where applicable.

Customs Documentation Checklist

Customs clearance delays are the single most common cause of shipping delays for international PCB orders — and nearly all of them are preventable with correct documentation. Each shipment must include a complete, accurate document packet. Missing or inconsistent information is the primary reason shipments are held for inspection or returned.

01

Commercial Invoice

The foundational document for any international shipment. Must include: seller and buyer full legal names and addresses, detailed description of goods ("Printed Circuit Boards" or "Printed Circuit Board Assemblies" — not "PCB" or "electronics"), quantity, unit value, total value, currency, country of origin (China), and HS code. For PCBs, the primary HS code is 8534.00 (Printed Circuits). For PCBA, the most common codes are 8537.10 (Boards/panels for electric control, under 1,000V) or 8542.31 (Electronic integrated circuits — processors and controllers, for assembled boards with programmed MCUs). The invoice must be signed and dated.

02

Packing List

A detailed breakdown of what is in each box or pallet: number of packages, type of packaging, dimensions (L x W x H in cm), gross weight (kg), net weight (kg), quantity per package, and total quantity. The packing list must match the commercial invoice exactly — discrepancies between the two documents are a common trigger for customs holds. For multi-item orders, list each line item separately with its own quantities and package references.

03

Air Waybill (AWB) / Bill of Lading (B/L)

For courier and air freight: an Air Waybill issued by the carrier (DHL, FedEx, or freight forwarder). For sea freight: a Bill of Lading — either a Master B/L (issued by the shipping line) or a House B/L (issued by a forwarder consolidating multiple shipments). The AWB or B/L serves as a receipt for the goods, evidence of the contract of carriage, and — for B/Ls — a document of title. Always confirm that the consignee name and address on the AWB/B/L match the commercial invoice exactly.

04

Certificate of Origin

A Certificate of Origin (COO) verifies that the goods were manufactured in China. It may be required by your country's customs authority to determine applicable duty rates. If your country is part of a Generalized System of Preferences (GSP) program — which grants reduced or zero-duty treatment to imports from developing countries — you will need Form A (GSP Certificate of Origin). Huaxing can provide both standard COO and Form A for all shipments. Many of our customers in the EU, UK, Canada, and Australia use Form A to reduce PCB import duties to 0%.

05

UL / ISO Certificates (as requested by customs)

Some destination countries — notably in the Middle East, South America, and parts of Southeast Asia — require UL or ISO certificates to be included with the shipping documentation to verify that the imported goods meet safety and quality standards. While not universally required, having these documents ready prevents delays if customs requests them. Huaxing includes copies of our UL (E354321) and ISO 9001 certificates in every shipment packet.

06

RoHS / REACH Compliance Statement

For all EU-bound shipments, a signed RoHS compliance declaration (confirming that the goods meet RoHS 3 requirements — all 10 restricted substances below threshold) and a REACH SVHC compliance statement are included in the documentation packet. For customers in other regions (UK, Australia, Korea, California), we include the applicable regional environmental compliance statements upon request. Non-EU countries with similar regulations increasingly expect these documents.

Critical Note on HS Codes: HS codes determine your duty rate. Using the wrong code can result in overpayment or, worse, a customs penalty for misclassification. For bare PCBs, 8534.00 is correct in virtually all jurisdictions. For assembled PCBA, the correct code depends on the board's function — 8537.10 covers most control/distribution boards; 8542.31 applies to boards whose primary function is data processing (e.g., single-board computers, microcontroller modules). If you are unsure which HS code applies to your assembly, consult a customs broker or ask us — we ship to 30+ countries and can advise based on your destination.

Duties, Taxes, and Landed Cost

Import duties and taxes are the most variable component of your total landed cost, and the hardest to estimate without understanding your destination country's tariff schedule. Here is what you need to know to budget accurately.

Typical Duty Rates for PCBs

PCB import duties are generally low compared to consumer electronics — most countries classify PCBs as intermediate goods and apply favorable rates. In our experience shipping to 30+ countries:

  • Bare PCBs (HS 8534.00): 0% in most countries, including the US, EU member states, UK, Japan, Korea, Australia, and Canada. Some countries apply 0-3%. This is because bare PCBs are component parts, not finished goods.
  • Assembled PCBA (HS 8537.10): 0-5% depending on the country. US is typically 0-2.5%. EU is 0-3.7% (depending on whether the board falls under a specific tariff suspension). Some developing countries with protective tariffs may apply higher rates — always check with your customs broker.
  • Assembled PCBA with processors (HS 8542.31): 0-3% in most countries. The US and EU generally apply zero duty to integrated circuits and processor boards under the Information Technology Agreement (ITA).

VAT / GST Recovery

In addition to duty, most countries apply Value Added Tax (VAT) or Goods and Services Tax (GST) on imported goods, calculated on the CIF value plus duty. Rates range from 5% (Canada GST, though provinces add their own) to 27% (Hungary). Common rates: EU ~19-25%, UK 20%, Australia 10%, Singapore 9%.

If you are a VAT/GST-registered business, you can typically recover import VAT through your periodic VAT return — the import VAT is treated as input tax and offset against your output tax liability. This means import VAT is a cash flow item, not a permanent cost, for registered businesses. Under DDP, however, duties and VAT are paid in the exporter's name, which can complicate recovery. This is another reason DAP is often preferable to DDP for VAT-registered entities.

Calculating Landed Cost

Your total landed cost follows this formula:

Landed Cost = Product Cost + Freight + Insurance + Customs Duty + Import VAT/GST + Brokerage Fees + Inland Delivery

For most PCB orders, the product cost and freight dominate. Duty is typically the smallest line item (0-5%). Brokerage fees — charged by the customs broker for filing the entry — are often overlooked but can range from $25-$75 for small shipments to $150-$300 for consolidated freight. When comparing quotes, always ask whether brokerage is included in the freight price.

Tracking and Insurance

Tracking Your Shipment

All express courier shipments (DHL, FedEx, UPS, TNT) come with full end-to-end tracking — you receive a tracking number when the shipment leaves our facility, and you can follow it through every scan point from Shenzhen to your door. These systems update in near real-time, with alerts available by email or SMS at key milestones (departure from origin, customs clearance, out for delivery).

For air freight and sea freight, tracking is at the waybill or container level — you will receive the master AWB number or container number, which you can use to track the shipment on the carrier's website. Updates are less granular than express tracking (typically port/terminal departures and arrivals, not every scan), but sufficient to know where your cargo is and whether it is on schedule.

Rail freight (China-Europe) provides waybill-level tracking through the rail operator's system. Most major forwarders now offer GPS-enabled tracking for rail shipments along the Yiwu-Duisburg and Zhengzhou-Hamburg corridors, giving you visibility into your cargo's position across the route.

When to Buy Additional Insurance

All express courier shipments include basic carrier liability coverage — DHL includes coverage up to $100, FedEx up to $100 (or $9.07/lb for US shipments, whichever is greater), UPS includes coverage up to $100. This is not true insurance — it is carrier liability under the terms of the Warsaw/Montreal Convention (air) or Hague-Visby Rules (sea), and it is subject to strict liability limits and exclusions. If your shipment is worth significantly more than the carrier liability limit, you need separate cargo insurance.

We recommend purchasing additional cargo insurance for any order valued over $5,000. Cargo insurance typically costs 0.3-0.5% of the declared value for air freight and 0.2-0.4% for sea freight — for a $10,000 shipment, you are looking at roughly $30-$50 for full coverage including general average, theft, pilferage, non-delivery, and water damage. Huaxing can arrange cargo insurance through our freight partners for any shipment upon request, or you can purchase it independently through a marine insurance broker.

Important: Carrier liability does NOT cover "consequential damages" — if a delayed shipment causes your production line to stop, the carrier is not liable for your lost production time, regardless of the declared value. For time-critical orders, the only protection is a buffer in your schedule. Plan for the worst-case transit time, not the best-case.

Practical Shipping Tips from 15+ Years of Export Experience

These are the things we have learned from shipping thousands of PCB orders to 30+ countries — the small decisions that make the difference between a smooth delivery and an expensive problem.

  • Consolidate when you can. If you order prototype PCBs every 2-3 weeks, ask us to hold and ship monthly. The savings from consolidating four 2-kg shipments into one 8-kg shipment can be 40-60%. Express courier rates are highly regressive — the per-kg cost drops sharply as weight increases, especially at the 5 kg, 10 kg, and 20 kg breakpoints.
  • Declare the correct value. Under-declaring the commercial invoice value to reduce duty is customs fraud in every jurisdiction and can result in seizure of the goods, fines, and blacklisting of your company and ours. Over-declaring (to get higher carrier liability coverage) costs you more in duty and raises flags with customs. Declare the transaction value — what you actually paid.
  • Check your country's de minimis threshold. Many countries exempt low-value shipments from duty and tax. The US de minimis (Section 321) threshold is $800 — shipments valued under $800 enter duty-free and tax-free. The EU threshold is EUR 150 for duty exemption (but VAT applies above EUR 0 for B2C shipments under the IOSS scheme). Australia's threshold is AUD 1,000. If your order is near a threshold, it may be worth splitting shipments.
  • Provide your tax ID for DDP/DAP shipments. If you are a business importing under DDP or DAP, providing your local VAT/GST/Tax ID number can simplify customs clearance and prevent delays at the border. Without it, the shipment may be treated as a personal import with different (often higher) duty treatment.
  • Plan for holidays. Chinese New Year (late January to mid-February) causes a 2-3 week logistics bottleneck as factories and freight forwarders shut down. The weeks before and after Christmas/New Year see heavy demand that drives up air freight rates. Plan production orders around these periods.
