Payment is the final step in any PCB procurement decision — and for international buyers sourcing from China, it's often the one that causes the most anxiety. How do you send money to a factory 10,000 km away and be confident you'll receive what you paid for?

This guide covers every major payment method available to PCB buyers, from standard T/T wire transfers to bank-guaranteed Letters of Credit. We explain the mechanics, costs, risks, and — most importantly — how to protect yourself. At Huaxing PCBA, we've processed payments from 30+ countries across North America, Europe, Middle East, and Asia-Pacific, and we understand what buyers need to feel secure.

Key Principle: The right payment method balances three factors — cost (bank fees vs. percentage-based), speed (instant vs. 3-7 day setup), and risk (buyer protection vs. direct transfer with no recourse). No single method is best for every situation — the optimal choice depends on your order size, relationship with the supplier, and risk tolerance.

Payment Methods: Deep Dive

Each payment method has distinct mechanics, fee structures, and risk profiles. Understanding these differences is essential before you commit funds to an international PCB order.

T/T — Telegraphic Transfer (Wire Transfer)

The industry standard for B2B PCB orders. T/T is a direct bank-to-bank transfer using the SWIFT network. It's the most cost-effective method for production orders and the default payment method for the vast majority of PCB manufacturers in China.

How it works: The supplier issues a Pro Forma Invoice (PI) with their bank details. You instruct your bank to transfer funds to the supplier's account. The transfer typically takes 1-3 business days and incurs a flat fee of $15-40 per transfer (your bank may charge outgoing wire fees; the supplier's bank may charge incoming fees — clarify who pays what in advance).

Typical PCB terms: The most common structure is 30/70 — 30% deposit to start production, 70% balance before shipment after you've seen inspection photos or video. For established relationships, 50/50 is also common. For samples under $1,000, 100% advance is standard.

SWIFT/BIC codes identify the receiving bank. Always double-check the account name matches the company name on the PI exactly — if the PI says "Huaxing Technology Co., Ltd." but the bank account is under an individual's name, that's a red flag.

Best for: Orders above $1,000. Any production order. The flat-fee structure makes T/T dramatically cheaper than percentage-based methods for amounts over $2,000.

L/C — Letter of Credit

The safest option for large orders. An L/C is a bank guarantee — your bank promises to pay the supplier only when they present documents proving the goods were shipped exactly as specified. The bank, not the buyer, verifies compliance.

How it works: You apply for an L/C at your bank (typically requires a credit line or cash collateral). The L/C specifies exactly what documents the supplier must present: commercial invoice, bill of lading, packing list, certificate of origin, inspection certificate. The supplier ships the goods and presents these documents to their bank. If the documents match the L/C terms exactly (even a typo can cause rejection), the bank releases payment.

Cost: 1-3% of the L/C value in bank fees (issuance fee, advising fee, negotiation fee, amendment fee if terms change). For a $100,000 order, expect $1,000-3,000 in total bank fees. Most cost-effective above $50,000.

Irrevocable vs. Revocable: Always use an Irrevocable L/C — it cannot be modified or cancelled without all parties' consent. Revocable L/Cs offer no real protection and are rarely used in practice.

Best for: First-time orders above $50,000. Orders with new, unverified suppliers. High-value custom PCB orders where the cost of non-delivery is severe. Buyers who want maximum bank-grade protection.

PayPal

Fast and convenient for small amounts, but the percentage-based fee structure makes it uneconomical for production orders. PayPal charges 3.5-4.4% + a fixed fee per transaction for cross-border payments — on a $5,000 order, that's $175-220 in fees alone.

Buyer Protection: PayPal's Buyer Protection covers items not received or significantly not as described. Disputes must be filed within 180 days. For PCB buyers, this provides meaningful recourse for non-delivery but limited protection for quality disputes (what constitutes "significantly not as described" for a PCB with subtle soldering defects?).

Limitations: Many Chinese PCB factories do not accept PayPal for production orders — it's common for samples and small prototype runs but uncommon for $5K+ orders. PayPal's currency conversion rates are typically 2.5-4% worse than mid-market rates, adding hidden cost.

Best for: Samples, prototypes, and orders under $2,000. Testing a new supplier before scaling to larger T/T orders. Buyers who prioritize convenience and speed over cost.

Alibaba Trade Assurance

Escrow-like protection through Alibaba's platform. Payment is held by Alibaba and only released to the supplier when you confirm delivery. If there's a dispute (quality, delay, non-delivery), Alibaba mediates and can refund your payment.

How it works: The supplier must have an Alibaba listing for your order. You place the order through Alibaba.com and pay via T/T or credit card to Alibaba's escrow account. Alibaba holds the funds. The supplier manufactures and ships. You confirm receipt — or file a dispute. Funds are released only after confirmation or dispute resolution.

Fees: The supplier typically pays 2-5% to Alibaba, which is usually built into the pricing. There's no direct fee to the buyer, but you may pay slightly higher unit prices compared to direct T/T.

Limitations: Not all custom PCB orders can be processed through Trade Assurance — the supplier needs an applicable Alibaba listing. The dispute resolution process can take weeks and Alibaba's mediation tends to favor documented evidence (inspection reports, third-party test data, shipping records).

Best for: First-time buyers building trust with a new Chinese PCB supplier. Orders from $500 to $30,000. Buyers who want escrow protection without the complexity of an L/C.

Western Union / MoneyGram

Fast but risky. Funds are available for pickup within minutes at agent locations worldwide. Once picked up, the money is gone — there is no buyer protection, no dispute mechanism, and no recourse for non-delivery or quality issues.

When to use: Only for small sample fees (<$500) with a supplier you've already verified and trust. Some PCB factories ask for sample shipping costs via Western Union because it's faster than T/T for small amounts. This can be legitimate, but it's also a common fraud vector.

Red flag: Any supplier pushing Western Union for production orders (>$1,000) should be treated with extreme skepticism. Legitimate factories overwhelmingly prefer T/T or L/C for production payments.

Best for: Very small sample shipping fees only (<$500). Nothing else.

Payment Terms: Understanding the Structures

The payment method is how money moves. The payment term is when money moves. PCB transactions commonly use a split structure where payment happens in stages tied to production milestones.

30/70 — Deposit / Balance Before Shipment

The most common structure in the PCB industry. You pay 30% upfront to initiate production (covers material costs and reserves capacity). The remaining 70% is paid before shipment, after the factory provides inspection photos, video, or — for larger orders — a third-party inspection report. This structure balances risk: the supplier has enough deposit to cover materials if you cancel; you retain leverage until the goods are verified.

What to expect before paying the balance: Photos of finished boards, close-ups of critical features (vias, edge connectors, silkscreen legibility), E-test or flying probe report, and for PCBA — AOI report and functional test results.

50/50 — Equal Split

Used for higher-risk custom designs or when the supplier's material cost is higher (e.g., Rogers laminates, heavy copper, exotic surface finishes). The larger deposit gives the supplier more working capital for expensive raw materials. 50/50 is also common for repeat orders where trust has been established — it simplifies cash flow for both parties.

100% Advance

Full payment before production begins. Standard for samples and orders under $1,000 — the administrative cost of tracking split payments exceeds the risk for small amounts. For production orders above $2,000, 100% advance is unusual and should only be agreed with long-established, trusted suppliers. If a new supplier demands 100% upfront on a $5,000+ order, treat it as a red flag.

Net 30 / Net 60 — Credit Terms

Payment after shipment — 30 or 60 days from the invoice date. This is credit extended by the supplier and is rare for first-time orders. Net terms are only available to buyers with an established relationship and proven payment history, typically after 6-12 months of consistent orders with on-time T/T payments. If a factory offers Net 30 on your first order without knowing you, ask why — legitimate factories are conservative with credit.

How to Protect Yourself: 6-Step Checklist

International payments carry inherent risk. These six steps form a systematic due diligence process that protects you regardless of which payment method you choose.

1

Always Require a Pro Forma Invoice with Company Stamp

A proper PI should include: company name and registered address, bank account details (exactly matching the company name), detailed line items with quantities and unit prices, payment terms, delivery terms (Incoterm), and estimated ship date. The company stamp (chop) carries legal weight in China. If a supplier hesitates to provide a stamped PI, walk away.

2

Verify the Bank Account Name Matches the Company Name

The beneficiary name on the bank account must match the company name on the PI. If the PI says "Shenzhen Huaxing Technology Co., Ltd." but the bank account is under "Mr. Zhang Wei," that's a serious red flag — you're sending money to an individual, not the company. Legitimate factories use corporate bank accounts. Always verify this before sending a wire.

3

Start Small — Test the Supplier Before Scaling

For a new supplier, place a sample order ($500-2,000) first. Evaluate: communication quality, adherence to promised delivery dates, packaging quality, and — most importantly — PCB quality. A supplier that performs well on a small order is more likely (though not guaranteed) to perform well on production volumes. Use this test to build confidence before committing $10K+.

4

Request Inspection Evidence Before Releasing Balance

Before sending the 70% balance payment, request: high-resolution photos of finished boards (both sides, close-up of fine-pitch areas), E-test/flying probe report showing 100% electrical test pass, AOI report for assembled boards, and for critical orders — video showing the boards being packed. Legitimate factories provide these as standard practice. A factory that refuses to share inspection evidence before payment is hiding something.

5

Use L/C or Trade Assurance for First Orders Above $10,000

The added cost (1-3% for L/C, 2-5% for Trade Assurance) is insurance — not a fee. On a $20,000 first order, $400-600 in bank fees or platform commissions buys you verified delivery. For repeat orders with a supplier you trust, switch to T/T for lower costs. This is standard practice among experienced international PCB buyers.

6

Keep Every Communication and Payment Record

Save: all email correspondence (especially specification confirmations and delivery promises), the Pro Forma Invoice, bank transfer receipts with transaction reference numbers, inspection photos and reports, and shipping documents. In the event of a dispute — whether through your bank, Alibaba, PayPal, or legal channels — documentation determines the outcome. Screenshots of WhatsApp conversations are not sufficient — insist on email for key specifications and commitments.

Currency Considerations

Most Chinese PCB manufacturers quote in USD. This is standard practice and generally works well for both parties. However, currency movements and intermediary bank practices can affect your final cost.

USD pricing: Nearly universal among Shenzhen PCB factories. Stable, predictable, and avoids the complexity of RMB international settlement. If you're in the Eurozone, UK, or Australia, be aware that your bank or an intermediary (correspondent) bank will convert your local currency to USD, typically at a rate 0.5-2% worse than the mid-market rate. This hidden cost is in addition to the wire fee.

Euro (EUR) and GBP: Some larger factories accept EUR or GBP transfers to local European accounts to reduce buyer currency conversion costs. Huaxing PCBA can accept EUR payments to our European partner account — ask your sales representative if this option is available for your order.

Wise (TransferWise) and similar services: For orders under $5,000, services like Wise, OFX, or Airwallex can offer better exchange rates than traditional banks — sometimes 0.5-1% above mid-market vs. 2-4% at banks. However, not all PCB factories accept payments from these platforms (they may show as third-party transfers). Confirm before using.

Practical tip: On a €10,000 order quoted at $10,800 USD, a 2% hidden currency conversion markup costs you ~€200. Asking "can you invoice in EUR?" or using a low-cost transfer service can save €100-200 per order. Over 10 orders a year, that's real money.

Red Flags: When to Stop and Verify

These warning signs don't automatically mean fraud — but they do mean you should slow down and verify before sending money.

100% Upfront on Production Orders

A supplier demanding full payment before production on a $3,000+ order. Legitimate factories expect split payments (30/70 or 50/50). Full advance on production orders is unusual and shifts all risk to the buyer.

Bank Account in a Different Country

The factory is in Shenzhen but their bank account is in Hong Kong, Turkey, or Eastern Europe — with a different company name. While Hong Kong accounts are common for legitimate Shenzhen exporters, the account name must still match. Offshore accounts under different entities are a fraud vector.

Personal Account Name on PI

The Pro Forma Invoice shows a company name but the bank beneficiary is an individual. Legitimate factories use corporate accounts. An individual account means you have no recourse against the company — you sent money to a person, not a business.

Western Union for >$1,000

Insisting on Western Union or MoneyGram for amounts over $500-1,000. These methods offer zero buyer protection and are favored by scammers for exactly that reason. Legitimate production orders should use T/T or L/C.

No Stamped Pro Forma Invoice

Reluctance or refusal to provide a Pro Forma Invoice with the company's official stamp (chop). The company stamp carries legal significance in China. A supplier who won't stamp a PI may not want a paper trail.
