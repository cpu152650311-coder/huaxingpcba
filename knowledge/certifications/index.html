PCB Certifications.
What They Mean & Why They Matter.

Understanding certification requirements is essential for qualifying PCB suppliers. Each mark — UL, ISO, IATF, RoHS — represents a different layer of quality assurance. This guide explains what every certification actually means, how to verify it, and which ones matter for your industry.

Home / Knowledge Base / Certifications Guide

When you source PCBs from a manufacturer you have never visited, certifications are the closest thing you have to a factory audit. A certificate hanging on a wall does not guarantee perfect boards every time — but the absence of the right certifications is a reliable indicator that a supplier has not invested in the systems required to produce consistent, compliant products.

Certifications in PCB manufacturing fall into three broad categories: safety certifications (UL — proving the board itself will not catch fire or fail dangerously), quality management certifications (ISO 9001, IATF 16949, ISO 13485 — proving the factory has documented, repeatable processes), and environmental/regulatory certifications (RoHS, REACH, ISO 14001 — proving the materials and processes meet regional chemical and waste-handling laws). A credible supplier will hold certifications across all three categories. A supplier with only one or two may be operating below the standard your product requires.

This guide walks through the seven most important certifications in PCB and PCBA manufacturing. For each one, we cover what it actually verifies, what it does not verify, how to check it yourself, and the red flags that should make you pause before placing an order.

Certification Deep-Dive

Each certification serves a distinct purpose. Understanding the differences — especially between certifications that sound similar — is what separates a thorough supplier evaluation from a box-checking exercise.

UL Certification (UL E354321)

UL (Underwriters Laboratories) is the most recognized safety certification body in North America. For PCBs, the relevant standard is UL 796 (Standard for Printed Wiring Boards), which tests for electrical fire hazards, maximum operating temperature, and material flammability under UL 94 (Standard for Tests for Flammability of Plastic Materials). A UL-certified PCB has been tested to ensure it will not become an ignition source or propagate a fire under specified electrical and thermal conditions.

There is an important distinction between UL Recognized (marked with a backward "UR") and UL Listed. UL Recognized applies to components intended for use within a larger end-product — PCBs almost always fall into this category. UL Listed applies to complete, stand-alone end-products (a power supply, a toaster). If a supplier tells you their PCBs are "UL Listed," they are either misinformed or misleading you. What you want is UL Recognized under the UL 796 (ZPMV2) category.

Every UL-certified manufacturer is assigned a unique E-number (UL file number) that you can look up in the UL Product iQ database. This is one of the few certifications you can verify yourself, instantly, without relying on the supplier to provide documentation. Simply enter the file number and confirm that (a) the company name matches, (b) the certification is active, and (c) the scope includes printed wiring boards (category ZPMV2).

Huaxing's UL file number is E354321. You can verify it at productiq.ul.com — it is active, current, and covers rigid and rigid-flex printed wiring boards.

ISO 9001:2015 — Quality Management Systems

ISO 9001:2015 is the foundational quality management standard, and for a PCB manufacturer, it is the minimum bar you should accept. It certifies that the organization has documented, repeatable processes for design, production, inspection, and customer feedback — and that management reviews these processes regularly and drives continuous improvement.

What ISO 9001 covers: documented process control, internal auditing, management review, corrective action procedures, customer satisfaction monitoring, calibration of measurement equipment, and traceability of non-conforming products. A factory with ISO 9001 has systems in place to catch defects before they reach the customer and to prevent recurrence when defects do occur.

What ISO 9001 does not cover: product-specific performance requirements. It certifies the process, not the product. A PCB from an ISO 9001 factory can still fail electrically or mechanically — the standard ensures only that when it fails, the factory has a procedure for finding out why and fixing it. Nor does ISO 9001 address industry-specific requirements like automotive defect prevention (that is IATF 16949) or medical device risk management (that is ISO 13485).

A PCB manufacturer without ISO 9001 is a red flag. The certification is not expensive or difficult to obtain relative to industry revenue — a factory operating without it is either very new (untested) or unwilling to submit to external audit (concerning). Even if your product does not require ISO 9001 compliance, the certification signals that the factory has basic quality infrastructure in place.

IATF 16949 — Automotive Quality Management

IATF 16949 is the automotive industry's quality management standard, developed by the International Automotive Task Force (IATF) in conjunction with ISO. It is built on ISO 9001 but adds automotive-specific requirements that are substantially stricter. If ISO 9001 asks "do you have a process?", IATF 16949 asks "can you prove that process prevents defects before they happen?"

Key automotive-specific requirements include: defect prevention over detection (failure mode and effects analysis, or FMEA, is mandatory for every production process), full material traceability from raw laminate to finished board, statistical process control (SPC) on critical manufacturing parameters, and the Production Part Approval Process (PPAP) — a formal 18-element submission package that proves a supplier can produce consistent parts at rate before the first production order ships. IATF 16949 also requires a certified internal auditor program and mandatory annual layout inspections.

IATF 16949 is mandatory for Tier 1 automotive suppliers and increasingly expected of Tier 2 and Tier 3 suppliers. If you are shipping PCBs into an automotive supply chain — even indirectly — your manufacturer should hold this certification. Note that IATF 16949 certification is expensive and audit-intensive; a factory with it has demonstrated serious commitment to quality.

ISO 13485 — Medical Device Quality Management

ISO 13485 is the quality management standard specific to medical device manufacturing, including components like PCBs that go into medical devices. It is fundamentally about risk management — every process, from raw material receiving through final inspection, must be evaluated for its potential impact on patient safety and device efficacy.

Key medical-specific requirements include: full traceability at the batch and component level (you must be able to trace any PCB back to the specific laminate lot, solder paste batch, and reflow oven profile used to produce it), validated processes (any process where the output cannot be fully verified by subsequent inspection must be validated in advance — this typically includes soldering, cleaning, and conformal coating), cleanroom requirements (medical PCBs often require controlled-environment manufacturing to prevent particulate contamination), and documented risk management per ISO 14971 throughout the product realization cycle.

If your PCBs go into a medical device that requires FDA 510(k) clearance, CE marking under the EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR), or similar regulatory approval, your contract manufacturer should hold ISO 13485. The certification demonstrates that the factory understands and operates within a regulated medical quality framework — not just a general manufacturing one.

ISO 14001 — Environmental Management Systems

ISO 14001 certifies that a manufacturer has an environmental management system in place — documented policies and procedures for managing waste, controlling chemical disposal, reducing energy consumption, and complying with applicable environmental regulations. For PCB manufacturing, which involves copper etching chemicals, solder fluxes, plating solutions, and solvent-based cleaning agents, environmental management is not abstract — it is a daily operational requirement.

ISO 14001 is relevant to PCB procurement for two reasons. First, RoHS and REACH compliance — while ISO 14001 does not itself certify RoHS/REACH compliance, a factory with ISO 14001 has the environmental monitoring infrastructure to track restricted substances through its supply chain. Second, regulatory risk — a PCB factory that lacks environmental controls is at higher risk of regulatory shutdown, which can disrupt your supply. This is especially relevant when sourcing from regions with evolving environmental enforcement.

The standard also requires life-cycle thinking — considering environmental impacts from raw material acquisition through end-of-life disposal. For you as a buyer, this means a certified supplier is more likely to have evaluated the environmental footprint of their laminate suppliers, plating chemistries, and packaging materials.

RoHS — Restriction of Hazardous Substances

RoHS (Restriction of Hazardous Substances) is an EU directive (2011/65/EU, amended by 2015/863) that restricts the use of six hazardous substances in electrical and electronic equipment: lead (Pb, max 0.1% in homogeneous material), mercury (Hg, max 0.1%), cadmium (Cd, max 0.01%), hexavalent chromium (Cr6+, max 0.1%), polybrominated biphenyls (PBB, max 0.1%), and polybrominated diphenyl ethers (PBDE, max 0.1%). For PCB manufacturing, the most impactful restriction is lead — it is what drove the industry-wide transition from tin-lead (SnPb) solder to lead-free alternatives (SAC305, SN100C, etc.).

RoHS 2 (2011/65/EU) expanded the scope to include all electrical/electronic equipment (not just specific categories), added CE marking requirements, and required manufacturers to maintain technical documentation demonstrating compliance for 10 years. RoHS 3 (2015/863) added four additional phthalate plasticizers to the restricted list: DEHP, BBP, DBP, and DIBP. If your product ships to the EU, you need to comply with RoHS 3.

How to verify RoHS compliance: a supplier should be able to provide material composition test reports from an accredited third-party lab (SGS, Intertek, TUV) for each restricted substance, not just a generic "RoHS compliant" statement on a datasheet. These reports should be dated within the last 12-24 months. You can also request XRF (X-ray fluorescence) screening results for incoming materials — many PCB manufacturers run XRF on laminate and solder paste lots as part of incoming inspection.

REACH — Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation, and Restriction of Chemicals

REACH (EC 1907/2006) is a European Union regulation concerning the production and use of chemical substances. It is significantly broader than RoHS. While RoHS restricts six (now ten) specific substances in electrical/electronic equipment, REACH covers all chemicals used in manufacturing — including solvents, plating solutions, cleaning agents, adhesives, and conformal coatings — across all industries, not just electronics.

The REACH regulation operates through two key mechanisms. First, the SVHC Candidate List (Substances of Very High Concern), maintained by the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA), identifies chemicals that are carcinogenic, mutagenic, toxic to reproduction, persistent/bioaccumulative, or of equivalent concern. As of 2026, this list contains over 240 substances and is updated twice yearly. If a product contains an SVHC above 0.1% w/w, the manufacturer has a duty to communicate this to downstream customers and, upon request, to consumers within 45 days. Second, the Authorisation List (Annex XIV) — substances that cannot be placed on the EU market after a "sunset date" unless a specific use has been authorised.

For PCB procurement, REACH compliance means your manufacturer must be able to declare that their products do not contain SVHCs above the 0.1% threshold, and must have documented communication procedures with their chemical suppliers to stay current with ECHA updates. A supplier who provides a one-time REACH declaration from five years ago has not understood the regulation — REACH compliance is an ongoing, active process.

How to Verify Certifications

A certificate on a website is worth nothing until you have verified it independently. Every certification can be faked, and in PCB sourcing — particularly when dealing with smaller brokers and trading companies — fraudulent certificates are more common than most buyers assume. Use this five-step verification checklist before qualifying any new supplier.

1

Look up the UL file number at the UL Product iQ database

Go to productiq.ul.com and enter the supplier's E-number. Confirm that the company name on file matches, the certification status is "Active," and the scope includes "Printed Wiring Boards" (category ZPMV2). This is the single most reliable verification you can do — UL's database is updated in real-time, and quarterly factory inspections mean lapsed certifications are caught quickly.

2

Request a copy of the ISO/IATF certificate and verify the certifying body

A legitimate ISO or IATF certificate will display the name and accreditation mark of the certification body (CB) that issued it. Credible CBs include TUV (TUV Rheinland, TUV SUD, TUV Nord), SGS, BSI (British Standards Institution), DNV, Bureau Veritas, and Intertek. These are all IAF (International Accreditation Forum) signatories. A certificate from an unknown or unfamiliar CB — especially one you cannot find on the IAF member list — should be treated as unverified. For IATF 16949 specifically, you can look up the certificate on the IATF Global Oversight database.

3

Check certificate validity dates

Every ISO-based certificate has an issue date and an expiry date (typically three years from issue, with annual surveillance audits in between). Confirm the date range is current. Also check the original certification date — a factory certified in 2024 is very different from one that has held continuous certification since 2010. Long certification history demonstrates institutional commitment to quality, not just a one-time effort to win a contract.

4

Verify the certificate scope matches what you are buying

Every ISO certificate includes a scope statement describing the activities covered. Read it carefully. If you are buying PCB fabrication and assembly and the scope says "plastic injection molding," "metal stamping," or "sales of electronic components," the certificate is for a different business line and does not apply to the PCBs you are purchasing. Similarly, if the scope says "PCB design and sales" but not "manufacturing," the factory may be outsourcing production to another facility — which means the quality systems you think you are buying into do not cover your actual manufacturer.

5

Request a factory audit report if the order value justifies it

For orders above approximately $10,000, or for any order going into a regulated industry (automotive, medical, aerospace), request a copy of the most recent third-party factory audit report. This is typically a QMS audit, a social compliance audit (such as SMETA or SA8000), or a customer-specific audit conducted by one of the supplier's existing clients. A factory that cannot or will not share any audit report — even with sensitive details redacted — may have something to hide. Note that this is different from the certificate itself; the audit report contains the actual findings, non-conformances, and corrective actions.

Red Flags When Reviewing Supplier Certifications

Even with valid documentation, certain patterns should raise concern. These warning signs do not automatically disqualify a supplier, but they should prompt deeper investigation before you commit to an order.

!

Expired Certificates

A supplier that displays an expired certificate — or sends you one when you ask for current documentation — is either disorganized (the certification has genuinely lapsed) or hoping you will not check the date. Either way, this is not a supplier you want managing your production quality. Always cross-check the dates printed on the certificate against the certifying body's online database where available.

!

Certificates from Unknown or Obscure Certifying Bodies

There are hundreds of accredited certification bodies worldwide, but the credible ones in manufacturing — TUV, SGS, BSI, DNV, Bureau Veritas, Intertek — are widely known. A certificate issued by a certification body you have never heard of, especially one without IAF (International Accreditation Forum) signatory status or with a website that looks insubstantial, may be from a "certification mill" that issues certificates without meaningful audits.

!

Scope Mismatch

If the certificate scope says "plastic injection molding," "cable assembly," or "trading of electronic components" but you are buying PCBs, the certification does not cover your product. This happens more often than you might expect — trading companies sometimes present a manufacturer's certificate as their own, or show a certification for one factory when your order will be produced at a different, uncertified facility. Always verify that the company name and production address on the certificate match the entity you are contracting with.

!

Reluctance to Share Certificate Numbers

Every UL file number, ISO certificate number, and IATF certificate number is public information — there is no confidentiality concern in sharing them. If a supplier hesitates, claims the number is "proprietary," or provides only a cropped image of the certificate without the number visible, they are almost certainly hiding something. A legitimate, certified manufacturer will provide their certificate numbers proactively, often displaying them on their website and in their email signature.
