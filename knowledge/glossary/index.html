Understanding PCB and PCBA terminology is fundamental to successful procurement, engineering collaboration, and quality assurance. Whether you are reviewing a fabrication drawing from your supplier, evaluating a datasheet for a new laminate, or communicating design requirements to your assembly partner, precise terminology eliminates ambiguity and prevents costly misunderstandings.

This glossary compiles over 500 terms spanning the full lifecycle of printed circuit boards — from material selection and stackup design through fabrication, assembly, testing, and reliability. Each definition is written for the practicing engineer or procurement professional: concise, technically accurate, and grounded in real-world manufacturing. A

Annular Ring

The copper ring that surrounds a drilled hole on a PCB, measured as the radial distance from the edge of the hole to the edge of the pad. A minimum annular ring of 0.125–0.15 mm is typically required for Class 2 boards to ensure reliable through-hole connections even when the drill wanders slightly from the pad centre.

AOI (Automated Optical Inspection)

A machine-vision-based inspection method that scans PCB assemblies for defects such as missing components, solder bridges, tombstoning, and misaligned parts by comparing captured images against a golden board or CAD data. AOI systems operate at high speed and are typically positioned after solder paste printing, after component placement, and after reflow soldering in SMT lines.

Aspect Ratio

The ratio of a PCB's thickness to the diameter of its smallest drilled hole. A 2.0 mm thick board with a 0.25 mm drill has an aspect ratio of 8:1. Aspect ratios above 10:1 challenge the ability of electroplating solutions to deposit copper uniformly inside the hole barrel, making plating quality harder to guarantee.

Assembly Drawing

A mechanical drawing that shows the physical placement, orientation, and part-number references for every component on a PCB. Assembly drawings include board outline dimensions, fiducial locations, tooling-hole positions, and special assembly notes such as press-fit or hand-solder requirements.

Anti-Pad

A clearance area in a power or ground plane around a plated through-hole where copper is removed to prevent an unwanted electrical connection between the hole and the plane. The anti-pad diameter must be large enough to maintain the required voltage clearance while small enough to preserve plane integrity for return-current paths.

Artwork

The photolithographic film or digital image that defines the copper pattern for each layer of a PCB. Historically produced as silver-halide film on mylar, artwork is now almost exclusively digital, rendered from Gerber or ODB++ data directly onto photoresist by laser direct imaging (LDI) systems.

Annular Ring Breakout

A manufacturing defect in which the drilled hole breaches the edge of its target pad, reducing or eliminating the annular ring on one side. IPC-6012 defines breakout limits by class: Class 2 allows breakout up to 90 degrees from the pad periphery provided minimum external annular ring is maintained, while Class 3 generally prohibits any breakout.

Active Component

An electronic component that requires an external power source to operate and can control the flow of current — transistors, ICs, diodes, and optoelectronic devices are all active. Active components can amplify, switch, or rectify signals, distinguishing them from passive components such as resistors and capacitors.

Additive Process

A PCB fabrication method in which copper is selectively deposited onto a bare substrate to build up circuit traces, rather than etching away unwanted copper from a fully clad laminate (subtractive process). Semi-additive processing (SAP) and modified semi-additive processing (mSAP) are the dominant techniques for producing sub-25-micron trace/space geometries in advanced HDI and IC-substrate boards.

Acid Trap

A geometric feature in a PCB layout — typically an acute angle at the junction of two traces or a trace entering a pad at a sharp angle — that can trap etchant chemistry and cause over-etching. Modern DFM rules flag and eliminate acid traps by requiring obtuse angles at trace junctions and smooth pad-entry transitions.

Air Gap

The physical separation between two conductors at different potentials, measured through air rather than through the PCB substrate. Creepage requirements in safety standards such as IPC-2221 and IEC 60950-1 mandate minimum air-gap distances as a function of working voltage, pollution degree, and altitude.

Alkaline Etching

A PCB etching process that uses an alkaline (ammoniacal) copper chloride solution to dissolve exposed copper, leaving behind the circuit pattern protected by an etch resist. Alkaline etching provides high etch rates and excellent sidewall definition and is the dominant etching chemistry for outer-layer patterning in volume PCB manufacturing.

Analog Circuit

An electronic circuit that processes continuously variable signals — voltages or currents that can assume any value within a range — as opposed to the discrete high/low levels of digital circuits. Analog PCB design demands careful attention to grounding, shielding, and component placement to minimise noise, crosstalk, and thermal drift.

Array / Panel Array

A configuration of multiple identical PCB images stepped and repeated within a single production panel to maximise material utilisation and assembly throughput. Arrays include breakaway rails, tooling holes, fiducials, and often break-routing or V-scoring to facilitate depanelisation after assembly.

ATE (Automatic Test Equipment)

Computer-controlled hardware and software systems that perform electrical tests on PCBs or assembled boards to verify functionality, measure parametric values, and detect manufacturing faults. ATE systems range from simple bed-of-nails in-circuit testers to complex functional test stations with programmable power supplies, signal generators, and measurement instruments.

Au / Gold

The chemical symbol for gold, used extensively in PCB surface finishes for its excellent corrosion resistance, solderability preservation, and reliable wire-bonding characteristics. Gold finishes on PCBs fall into two broad categories: immersion gold (thin, ~0.05–0.125 µm, over electroless nickel as ENIG) and hard electrolytic gold (thick, ~0.5–5.0 µm, for edge connectors and high-wear contacts).

AWG (American Wire Gauge)

A standardised logarithmic scale for specifying the diameter of round, solid, non-ferrous electrical wire. AWG numbers run inversely to diameter — a higher AWG number denotes a thinner wire. In PCB design AWG is often referenced when sizing discrete wires for hand-soldered connections, jumper wires, or transformer windings.

Axial Lead

A through-hole component lead configuration in which two wire leads emerge from opposite ends of a cylindrical component body along the same axis, as seen in many resistors, diodes, and capacitors. Axial-leaded components are manually formed and inserted into PCB holes spaced to match the body length plus lead bend radii.

Altium

A leading commercial PCB design software suite (Altium Designer) that integrates schematic capture, PCB layout, 3D mechanical integration, signal integrity simulation, and manufacturing-output generation in a unified environment. Altium's native file format and extensive component-library ecosystem make it one of the most widely used EDA tools in professional PCB design.

Adhesive

In PCB assembly, a thermosetting epoxy or acrylic compound used to secure surface-mount components to the board before wave soldering, preventing displacement during the soldering process. Adhesives are dispensed as dots between component pads and cured thermally or by UV exposure before the board enters the wave.

Anode

The electrode in an electroplating cell at which oxidation occurs — in PCB copper plating, the soluble copper anode dissolves into the electrolyte to replenish copper ions that are deposited onto the PCB cathode. In tantalum capacitors and diodes, the anode is the positive terminal during normal operation.

AQL (Acceptable Quality Level)

The maximum percentage of defective units in a production lot that can be considered satisfactory as a process average. AQL sampling plans, defined in ISO 2859 / ANSI ASQ Z1.4, specify sample sizes and accept/reject criteria for incoming inspection and final quality audits in PCB manufacturing.

Anti-Tarnish

A protective organic coating applied to copper surfaces on PCBs to retard oxidation and preserve solderability during storage between fabrication and assembly. Anti-tarnish treatments are often water-based organic films that are displacable by flux during soldering; OSP (Organic Solderability Preservative) is the most common example.

Alumina (Al2O3)

Aluminium oxide ceramic used as a substrate material for thick-film hybrid circuits and high-power LED modules. Alumina offers high thermal conductivity (~24–30 W/mK), excellent electrical insulation, and mechanical rigidity, making it suitable for applications where FR-4 cannot dissipate sufficient heat.

Annular Ring Minimum

The smallest allowable annular ring specified by the applicable performance class and design rules. IPC-6012 Class 2 permits a minimum external annular ring of 0.05 mm with 90-degree breakout allowed; Class 3 typically requires 0.125 mm minimum with no breakout for high-reliability applications.

Autoplacer

An EDA tool feature that automatically places components on a PCB layout based on netlist connectivity, design rules, and placement constraints. Autoplacers use optimisation algorithms (simulated annealing, force-directed placement) to minimise total connection length and reduce routing congestion, though they rarely match the quality of skilled manual placement for complex designs and typically serve as a starting point that is then refined by the PCB designer.

Autorouter

A PCB design tool feature that automatically generates copper trace routing between component pins based on the netlist and design rules. Modern autorouters use grid-based, shape-based, or topological routing algorithms; while they can effectively route simpler sections of a board (memory buses, parallel interfaces), high-speed, impedance-controlled, and analogue sections still require manual routing for optimal signal integrity.

Annular Ring Calculator

A design-rule tool or spreadsheet that computes the required pad diameter for a given finished hole size and minimum annular ring specification. For a 0.3 mm finished hole with a 0.125 mm minimum annular ring, the minimum pad diameter is 0.3 + 2 x 0.125 = 0.55 mm — though fabricators typically add additional allowance for drill wander and registration tolerance.

Anisotropic Conductive Film (ACF)

An adhesive film containing conductive particles that provide electrical conduction in the Z-axis only (through the film thickness) while maintaining electrical isolation in the X-Y plane. ACF is used for bonding flex circuits to glass displays (LCD/OLED) and for attaching fine-pitch components where conventional soldering is impractical, with bonding achieved by heat and pressure.

Abrasion

Mechanical wear of a PCB surface caused by rubbing, scraping, or repeated contact — typically at connector mating surfaces, test points, or areas subject to handling. Abrasion removes surface finishes and can expose underlying copper, leading to oxidation and solderability loss; gold-plated edge connectors are specifically designed and tested for abrasion resistance over hundreds or thousands of mating cycles.

A-Side / B-Side

The two sides of a double-sided or multilayer PCB: the A-side (primary side, component side, top side) is typically the side with the most or the largest components and is processed first during SMT assembly; the B-side (secondary side, solder side, bottom side) is the opposite side. In mixed-technology boards, the B-side typically carries the through-hole components inserted after SMT processing of both sides is complete.

B

BGA (Ball Grid Array)

A surface-mount IC package in which the interconnection to the PCB is made through an array of solder balls on the underside of the package rather than through peripheral leads. BGA packages enable high pin counts in compact footprints, offer lower lead inductance than QFP alternatives, and demand precise reflow-profile control to achieve reliable collapse and wetting of every ball in the array.

Blind Via

A plated hole that connects one or more outer layers to one or more inner layers of a multilayer PCB without passing through the entire board thickness. Blind vias free up routing real estate on layers below the via termination and are a key enabling feature of HDI (High-Density Interconnect) designs; they are typically formed by laser drilling through the outer copper foil into a buried copper pad.

Buried Via

A plated hole that connects two or more inner layers of a multilayer PCB without extending to either outer surface. Buried vias are formed during the lamination of sub-assemblies (cores) and are invisible on the finished board; they allow high routing density without consuming real estate on outer layers.

Board Thickness

The total thickness of a finished PCB including all copper layers, laminate cores, prepreg sheets, solder mask, and surface finish. Standard board thicknesses are 0.8 mm, 1.0 mm, 1.6 mm, and 2.0 mm, though custom thicknesses from 0.2 mm to over 6.0 mm are achievable. Board thickness directly affects impedance control, via aspect ratio, and mechanical fit in enclosures.

Bow and Twist

Two types of board flatness deviation: bow is a cylindrical curvature where all four corners lie in the same plane but the centre is displaced, while twist is a diagonal deformation where one corner is out of plane relative to the other three. IPC-6012 limits bow and twist to 0.75% for surface-mount boards and 1.5% for through-hole boards to ensure reliable assembly processing.

BOM (Bill of Materials)

A structured list of every component required to assemble a PCB, including manufacturer part numbers, quantities, reference designators, package types, and often alternate/approved sources. The BOM is the central procurement document for PCB assembly — its accuracy directly determines whether the correct parts are sourced, kitted, and loaded onto pick-and-place machines.

Bed of Nails

A test fixture containing an array of spring-loaded pogo pins arranged to contact specific test points on a PCB when the board is pressed against the fixture. Bed-of-nails fixtures enable in-circuit testing (ICT) by providing simultaneous electrical access to hundreds of nets, and are custom-built for each PCB design.

Boundary Scan (JTAG)

A test methodology defined in IEEE 1149.1 that uses dedicated test logic built into ICs to control and observe signals at device pins without needing physical test probes on every net. Boundary scan enables testing of interconnects between BGA and other inaccessible packages, and is widely used for prototyping, production test, and in-system programming.

Back Drilling

A post-fabrication process in which a drill bit larger than the original plated through-hole is used to remove the unused portion of a via barrel (the stub) from the back side of the board. Back drilling reduces signal reflections caused by via stubs in high-speed digital designs — particularly important above 5 Gbps where stub resonances can close the eye diagram.

Bare Board

A fabricated but unpopulated PCB — the raw board with copper traces, pads, plated holes, solder mask, and surface finish, but with no components attached. Bare-board testing (continuity and isolation) is performed before assembly to verify that the fabricated board matches the netlist and contains no shorts or opens.

Base Copper

The copper foil that is bonded to the laminate during the core-manufacturing process before any circuit patterning. Base copper thickness is specified in ounces (oz) — 0.5 oz (18 um), 1 oz (35 um), and 2 oz (70 um) being the most common — and determines the starting conductor thickness before plating adds additional copper.

Base Material

The insulating substrate onto which copper foil is bonded to form a laminate. Common base materials include FR-4 (woven glass/epoxy), polyimide (for flex and high-temperature applications), CEM-1/CEM-3 (composite epoxy), PTFE (for RF/microwave), and metal cores (aluminium or copper for thermal management).

Black Pad

A reliability defect specific to ENIG (Electroless Nickel Immersion Gold) surface finishes in which excessive galvanic corrosion during the immersion-gold plating step attacks the nickel layer, creating a phosphorus-rich, brittle interface. Solder joints to black-pad surfaces exhibit brittle fracture at the Ni-Sn intermetallic layer and can fail mechanically under thermal cycling or mechanical shock.

Black Oxide

A chemical conversion coating applied to inner-layer copper surfaces before lamination to improve bond strength between the copper and prepreg resin. Black oxide produces a dark, micro-roughened copper surface; alternative oxide processes (brown oxide, red oxide) provide similar adhesion enhancement with different process characteristics.

Blister

A localised separation between layers of a PCB laminate or between the copper foil and the substrate, appearing as a raised bubble on the board surface. Blisters are caused by trapped moisture volatilising during soldering temperatures and represent a latent defect from inadequate lamination or storage conditions; IPC-6012 considers blistering a rejectable defect.

Bonding

The process of joining layers of a multilayer PCB under heat and pressure so that the prepreg resin flows, cures, and creates a monolithic structure. In wire-bonded assemblies, bonding also refers to the ultrasonic or thermosonic attachment of fine gold or aluminium wires between a semiconductor die and the substrate pads.

Breakaway Tab

A small connecting strip of PCB material left between the board outline and the panel frame after routing, designed to be snapped or cut after assembly to separate individual boards from the panel. Breakaway tabs typically include perforations (mouse bites) to reduce the force required for depanelisation.

B-Stage

The partially cured intermediate state of a thermosetting resin (such as the epoxy in prepreg) in which the material is solid but still fusible and capable of further curing. Prepreg is supplied and stored in B-stage; during lamination, heat and pressure melt the B-stage resin, cause it to flow, and then fully polymerise it to the C-stage (fully cured) state.

Burr

A raised edge or rough ridge of copper that forms on the exit side of a drilled hole when the drill bit is dull or drilling parameters are sub-optimal. Burrs must be removed by mechanical brushing or chemical processes because they can pierce prepreg during lamination or create solder-mask defects on outer layers.

Bus Bar

A thick copper conductor on a PCB or in a power distribution system designed to carry high currents with minimal voltage drop and resistive heating. On PCBs, bus bars may be implemented as wide traces with heavy copper plating, as external copper bars soldered or bolted to the board, or as dedicated inner-layer planes.

Bypass Capacitor

A capacitor placed between a power rail and ground near an active device to provide a low-impedance path for high-frequency noise and transient current demands. Effective bypassing (decoupling) is a critical aspect of PDN design, with capacitor values, package sizes, and placement distances chosen to minimise loop inductance over the frequency range of interest.

Bevel

A chamfered edge applied to the contact fingers of a gold-plated edge connector to facilitate insertion into a mating socket and reduce wear on the gold plating. The bevel angle is typically 20 to 45 degrees and is produced by mechanical milling or routing after the gold plating step.

Ball Shear

A destructive mechanical test in which a shear tool pushes a solder ball laterally off a BGA pad while measuring the force required to cause failure. Ball shear testing per JEDEC JESD22-B117A verifies the mechanical integrity of the solder-ball-to-pad interface and can reveal brittle failures associated with black pad or improper reflow.

BTU (Board Thermal Unit)

A measure of the heat energy absorbed or released by a PCB assembly during reflow soldering, determined by board mass, specific heat capacity of the materials, conveyor speed, and oven zone temperatures. Reflow profiles are designed around the BTU requirements of a specific assembly to ensure that all solder joints reach the required peak temperature without overheating sensitive components.

Bleeding

The unwanted migration of resin, ink, or marking fluid beyond its intended boundary during curing. Solder-mask bleeding onto a pad reduces the solderable area and can cause solderability defects; legend-ink bleeding obscures part markings and reference designators. Proper cure profiles and ink-viscosity control minimise bleeding.

Bare-Board Testing

The electrical testing of an unpopulated PCB to verify that there are no unintended open circuits (missing continuity where it should exist) or short circuits (unwanted continuity between nets that should be isolated). Bare-board testing is the final fabrication quality gate and is performed on 100% of boards for all but the most cost-sensitive, low-complexity single-sided designs.

B-stage Prepreg Handling

The specific storage and handling requirements for prepreg materials to maintain their B-stage properties before lamination. Prepreg must be stored refrigerated or at controlled room temperature and humidity, has a defined shelf life (typically 3-6 months), and must be handled with clean gloves to prevent contamination that would compromise inter-layer bond strength.

Bonding Film

A thin layer of thermoplastic or thermosetting adhesive film used in flex and rigid-flex PCB construction to bond polyimide coverlay to the circuit layer, or to bond multiple flex layers together. Bonding films are supplied in sheet form, cut to shape, and laminated under heat and pressure to create a void-free bond between layers.

Breakdown Voltage

The voltage at which an insulating material (laminate, solder mask, or conformal coating) fails and allows a destructive current to flow through it. PCB laminate breakdown voltage is typically in the kilovolt range for standard thicknesses, but the practical operating voltage limit is determined by creepage and clearance requirements well below the breakdown threshold, per IPC-2221 design standards.

Bulk Resistance

The DC resistance of a conductor or material measured through its volume (as opposed to surface resistance, which is measured along its surface). For PCB traces, bulk resistance is determined by the trace's cross-sectional area (width x thickness), length, and the resistivity of copper (~1.72e-8 ohm-m for pure copper at 20 degC), and determines the DC voltage drop along the trace.

Bow (IPC Definition)

Per IPC-TM-650, bow is the deviation from flatness of a PCB characterised by a cylindrical or spherical curvature such that all four corners lie in the same plane. The percentage of bow is calculated as the maximum vertical displacement divided by the length of the curved edge, multiplied by 100. Bow and twist are measured together on a flat granite surface plate with feeler gauges or by automated laser profilometry.

Body Size (Component)

The physical length, width, and height dimensions of a component package, specified in the component datasheet and represented in the PCB footprint by the courtyard outline. Body size determines the minimum component-to-component spacing on the PCB and the required clearance for automated placement nozzles and inspection access; it is a primary input to DFM analysis for component placement density.

C

CAF (Conductive Anodic Filament)

A failure mechanism in PCBs in which a conductive copper-salt filament grows along the glass-resin interface between two conductors at different potentials in the presence of moisture and ionic contamination. CAF growth can create a short circuit between adjacent plated through-holes or traces and is accelerated by high humidity, high voltage gradients, and poor glass-to-resin bonding.

CTE (Coefficient of Thermal Expansion)

A material property that quantifies how much a material expands per degree of temperature increase, typically expressed in ppm/degC. The CTE mismatch between the copper plating (~17 ppm/degC) and the laminate in the Z-axis (~50-70 ppm/degC for FR-4 above Tg) is the primary driver of plated-through-hole fatigue failure during thermal cycling.

Class 2 / Class 3

Two of the three IPC performance classifications for electronic products. Class 2 (Dedicated Service) covers products where extended life and uninterrupted service are desired but not critical. Class 3 (High Reliability) covers products where continued performance is critical, equipment downtime cannot be tolerated, and the end-use environment may be harsh — medical devices, aerospace, and military equipment typically require Class 3.

Core

A fully cured laminate sheet with copper foil bonded to one or both sides, used as the starting building block for multilayer PCB construction. Cores are processed with inner-layer imaging and etching, then stacked with prepreg sheets and laminated together to form the complete multilayer structure.

Copper Weight (oz)

A measure of copper foil thickness expressed as the weight of copper per square foot. One ounce copper (1 oz) equals approximately 35 um (1.4 mil) thickness. Common copper weights range from 0.25 oz (9 um) for fine-pitch HDI to 6 oz (210 um) or more for high-current power electronics; the copper weight directly determines the current-carrying capacity of traces.

Counterbore

A cylindrical flat-bottomed recess machined into a PCB to accommodate the head of a screw or standoff so that it sits flush with or below the board surface. Counterbores are produced by a secondary mechanical drilling operation using a larger-diameter drill or end mill, and are specified with diameter, depth, and whether they are plated or non-plated.

Countersink

A conical recess machined into a PCB to accept a flat-head screw so that the screw head sits flush with the board surface. Countersinks are specified by the screw size, the cone angle (typically 82 or 90 degrees), and whether the hole is plated or non-plated.

Test Coupon

A small, dedicated test structure fabricated on the same panel as the production PCBs and used for destructive and non-destructive quality testing. Coupons typically contain patterns for measuring plated-hole copper thickness, solderability, impedance, and peel strength, and are often retained by the manufacturer as objective evidence of conformance.

CEM-1

A composite epoxy material consisting of a paper core with a woven glass surface layer, used as a low-cost alternative to FR-4 for single-sided and simple double-sided PCBs. CEM-1 has lower mechanical strength and poorer electrical properties than FR-4 and is not suitable for multilayer or plated-through-hole designs.

CEM-3

A composite epoxy material with a glass-mat core and woven glass outer layers, offering performance closer to FR-4 than CEM-1 but still at a cost advantage. CEM-3 supports plated through-holes and can be used in simple multilayer boards, though its electrical and thermal properties remain inferior to those of FR-4.

Chamfer

A bevelled edge (see Bevel) applied to gold-plated connector fingers to facilitate insertion. The chamfer angle and smoothness directly affect the insertion force and the number of mating cycles the connector can withstand before the gold plating wears through to the underlying nickel.

Characteristic Impedance (Z0)

The ratio of voltage to current for a signal propagating along a transmission line, determined by the geometry of the trace, the dielectric constant of the surrounding material, and the distance to the reference plane. In PCB design, controlled-impedance traces are designed to a target Z0 (typically 50 ohms single-ended or 100 ohms differential) to minimise signal reflections and ensure signal integrity.

Chemical Etching

The process of removing unwanted copper from a PCB panel by dissolving it in a chemical solution — typically acidic cupric chloride for inner layers and alkaline ammoniacal copper chloride for outer layers — while the desired circuit pattern is protected by an etch resist. Etching uniformity across the panel and etch-factor control are critical for achieving target trace widths.

Chip-On-Board (COB)

An assembly technology in which a bare semiconductor die is attached directly to the PCB substrate with conductive or non-conductive adhesive and electrically connected via fine wire bonds. The die and wire bonds are then encapsulated with a glob of epoxy for protection. COB reduces packaging cost and footprint but requires cleanroom-level assembly conditions.

Cleanroom

A controlled environment with regulated particulate contamination, temperature, and humidity used for critical PCB fabrication and assembly processes. Photolithography, dry-film lamination, and semiconductor assembly require Class 10,000 (ISO 7) or better cleanroom conditions to prevent yield loss from particle-induced defects.

CNC Drilling

Computer Numerical Control drilling — the automated mechanical drilling process that creates holes in PCB panels using high-speed spindles (up to 200,000 RPM) and solid carbide drill bits as small as 0.1 mm diameter. CNC drilling machines read Excellon-format drill files that specify the X-Y coordinates, tool sizes, and drilling sequence for every hole in the board.

Conformal Coating

A thin protective polymer film applied to an assembled PCB to protect it from moisture, dust, chemicals, and temperature extremes. Common conformal-coating materials include acrylic, silicone, polyurethane, and parylene, applied by spraying, brushing, dipping, or vapour deposition; coating thickness is typically 25–250 um.

Controlled Impedance

The practice of designing PCB transmission-line geometries and selecting laminate materials such that the characteristic impedance of each signal trace falls within a tight tolerance (typically +/-10% or +/-5%) of a target value. Controlled-impedance designs require close coordination between the PCB designer and fabricator to account for etch factor, dielectric thickness tolerance, and Dk variation.

Copper Foil

Thin sheets of copper produced by electrodeposition (ED foil) or rolling (RA/rolled-annealed foil) that are bonded to laminate substrates to form the conductive layers of a PCB. ED foil is the standard for rigid PCBs; RA foil offers smoother surface profiles and better flex endurance and is preferred for flexible circuits and high-frequency applications.

Cross-Section

A destructive analysis technique in which a PCB sample is cut, potted in epoxy, polished, and examined under a microscope to evaluate the internal structure — copper thickness in holes, intermetallic compound formation, laminate voids, and layer-to-layer registration. Cross-sectioning is the definitive method for verifying PCB manufacturing quality and is a standard requirement for first-article inspection.

Crosshatching

A copper plane pattern consisting of a grid of intersecting lines rather than a solid fill, used on flex PCBs and in some rigid-flex designs to maintain flexibility while providing a ground reference. Crosshatching reduces the bending stiffness of a copper plane and can be tuned by adjusting the hatch pitch and line width.

CTI (Comparative Tracking Index)

A measure of a laminate material's resistance to electrical tracking — the formation of a conductive carbon path along the surface when exposed to moisture and voltage. CTI values range from PLC 0 (>600V) to PLC 5 (<100V); higher CTI materials are required for high-voltage designs and are mandated by safety standards such as IEC 62368-1.

C-Stage

The fully cured, final state of a thermosetting resin in which it is infusible, insoluble, and cannot be re-softened by heating. The laminate in a finished PCB is in the C-stage; prepreg transitions from B-stage to C-stage during the lamination cycle.

Coverlay

A thin polyimide film coated with a layer of adhesive, used in flexible PCBs as the equivalent of solder mask to insulate and protect the outer-layer copper traces. Coverlay is applied by lamination rather than printing and offers superior flexibility and chemical resistance compared to photoimageable solder mask on flex substrates.

Capacitive Coupling

The transfer of electrical energy between two conductors through their mutual capacitance — a primary mechanism of crosstalk in PCB design. Capacitive coupling is proportional to the parallel length of adjacent traces, inversely proportional to their separation distance, and increases with signal edge rate; it is managed by increasing trace spacing, inserting guard traces, and routing sensitive signals between reference planes.

Copper Plating Thickness

The thickness of electroplated copper on the walls of a plated through-hole, measured at the centre of the hole barrel by cross-section or non-destructive methods. IPC-6012 specifies a minimum of 20 um average and 18 um minimum for Class 2, and 25 um average and 20 um minimum for Class 3; inadequate plating thickness reduces current-carrying capacity and reliability under thermal cycling.

Capillary Action

The movement of a liquid within narrow spaces — such as the gap between a BGA component and the PCB surface, or the barrel of a plated through-hole — driven by surface tension forces. Capillary action draws molten solder up through plated holes (providing hole fill in through-hole solder joints), draws underfill epoxy under flip-chip and BGA components, and can draw flux residues into areas that cannot be cleaned.

Crazing

A network of fine cracks in the resin of a PCB laminate, typically caused by thermal shock, mechanical stress, or chemical attack. Crazing degrades the laminate's dielectric properties and provides pathways for moisture ingress and CAF growth; it is a rejectable defect when it occurs in areas between conductors or at hole walls per IPC-6012 criteria.

Conductor Spacing

See Line Spacing and Spacing (Clearance). The edge-to-edge distance between adjacent copper features on the same PCB layer. Defined as a minimum in the design rules, conductor spacing is verified during DRC and is one of the primary DFM parameters communicated to the fabricator at the quoting stage.

Copper Pour

A filled polygon of copper on a PCB layer, typically connected to a power or ground net, that provides current-carrying capacity, thermal spreading, and EMI shielding. Copper pours are defined in the EDA tool by drawing the pour outline and specifying the net connection and the clearance to other features; they may be solid (full fill) or hatched (crosshatched) depending on the application.

D

DRC (Design Rule Check)

An automated verification process in PCB design software that checks the layout against a set of user-defined and fab-defined rules governing minimum trace width, minimum spacing, hole-to-copper clearance, annular ring, and dozens of other geometric constraints. Running DRC before releasing Gerber files to the fabricator catches errors that would otherwise manifest as manufacturing defects or non-functional boards.

DFM (Design for Manufacturability)

A design methodology and review process that ensures a PCB can be fabricated and assembled reliably, cost-effectively, and at high yield. DFM analysis covers trace/space adequacy, via-in-pad implications, solder-mask clearance, component-to-edge distance, fiducial placement, and panel utilisation — with the goal of eliminating features that drive up cost or reduce yield before the design reaches production.

DFT (Design for Test)

A design practice that ensures a PCB assembly can be effectively and efficiently tested during production. DFT provisions include accessible test points or test vias on every net, sufficient clearance around probe targets, dedicated test pads for ICT fixtures, and inclusion of boundary-scan chains where physical probing is impractical.

Datum

A precisely defined reference point, line, or plane from which all dimensional measurements on a PCB are referenced. The board datum is typically established by fiducial marks or tooling holes and is used by fabrication CAM systems, AOI machines, and pick-and-place equipment to align the board coordinate system with the physical panel.

Deburr

A mechanical process that removes the raised copper burrs formed on the exit side of drilled holes using abrasive brushes, non-woven pads, or micro-etching. Deburring is performed immediately after drilling and is essential because residual burrs can puncture prepreg during lamination or cause solder-mask coverage defects.

Delamination

The separation of layers within the PCB laminate — either between the copper foil and the substrate or between layers of prepreg — caused by thermal stress, moisture absorption, or poor lamination bonding. Delamination is a catastrophic defect: it compromises electrical insulation and mechanical integrity, and IPC-6012 defines any delamination exceeding 2% of the board area as rejectable.

Desmear

A chemical process that removes the thin layer of resin smear deposited on the inner copper surfaces of drilled holes. Desmear — typically a permanganate etch following a plasma or chemical swell step — exposes clean copper for reliable electroless copper deposition and is critical for achieving void-free plated through-hole barrels.

Differential Pair

Two parallel PCB traces routed together at a constant spacing, carrying equal-amplitude, opposite-polarity signals. Differential pairs are the physical implementation of differential signalling and are characterised by their differential impedance (typically 90 or 100 ohms); they offer superior noise immunity and lower EMI compared to single-ended signals and are used extensively in high-speed serial interfaces such as USB, PCIe, and LVDS.

Digital Circuit

An electronic circuit that processes binary signals — voltages that represent logic 0 and logic 1 states with well-defined thresholds. Digital PCB design must manage simultaneous switching noise, ground bounce, and signal-integrity effects that intensify as edge rates decrease, even at relatively low clock frequencies.

DIP (Dual Inline Package)

A through-hole IC package with two parallel rows of pins extending from the long sides of a rectangular plastic or ceramic body. DIP packages were the dominant IC format throughout the 1970s and 1980s; today they are largely supplanted by surface-mount packages but remain common in prototyping, breadboarding, and through-hole legacy designs.

Double-Sided PCB

A PCB with conductive copper patterns on both the top and bottom surfaces of the substrate, connected by plated through-holes. Double-sided boards represent the simplest form of plated-through-hole construction and provide approximately twice the routing density of single-sided boards for a given board area.

Drill File (Excellon)

A CNC machine-control file — typically in Excellon format — that specifies the X-Y coordinates, tool numbers (corresponding to drill diameters), and drilling sequence for every hole on a PCB. The Excellon file is one of the essential manufacturing outputs generated by PCB design software and is read directly by the CNC drilling machine controller.

Dry Film

A photopolymer resist material supplied as a thin film that is laminated onto copper-clad laminate by heat and pressure. The dry film is exposed to UV light through an artwork mask, and the unexposed (or exposed, depending on the resist type) areas are developed away, leaving the resist pattern that protects copper during etching or defines areas for pattern plating.

Dk (Dielectric Constant)

A measure of a material's ability to store electrical energy in an electric field, representing the ratio of the permittivity of the material to the permittivity of free space. Dk is a primary parameter for impedance control and signal-speed calculation: lower Dk materials (3.0-3.5 for PTFE, ~4.0-4.6 for standard FR-4) enable faster signal propagation and are preferred for high-speed and RF applications.

Df (Dissipation Factor)

Also called loss tangent (tan d), Df quantifies the dielectric loss in a laminate material — the fraction of signal energy that is converted to heat as the signal propagates along a transmission line. Lower Df values (0.002-0.005 for low-loss laminates vs. 0.015-0.025 for standard FR-4) are critical for high-frequency and high-speed digital designs to minimise insertion loss over long trace lengths.

DPMO (Defects Per Million Opportunities)

A Six Sigma metric that quantifies process quality by counting the number of defects relative to the number of opportunities for a defect to occur, normalised to one million. In PCB assembly, DPMO might be measured as solder-joint defects per million joints placed; world-class SMT lines operate at fewer than 50 DPMO.

Dog-Bone

A BGA escape-routing pattern in which a short trace extends from the BGA pad to a via placed between four adjacent pads, creating a shape that resembles a dog bone. Dog-bone fanout is the standard routing strategy for through-hole via BGA escape where via-in-pad is not used.

Daisy Chain

A test structure in which multiple components or connections are wired in series so that a single continuity measurement can verify the integrity of the entire chain. Daisy-chain patterns on test coupons are used to verify plated-through-hole reliability during thermal cycling; daisy-chain nets in BGA designs enable production testing of solder-joint integrity under the package.

Datum Plane

A theoretically perfect planar surface established as a reference for dimensional measurements. On a PCB, multiple datum planes — typically the primary plane (board surface) and secondary and tertiary planes defined by tooling holes or board edges — create a complete coordinate reference system for inspection and assembly alignment.

Dross

The oxide and contaminant skin that forms on the surface of molten solder in a wave-soldering pot as the solder reacts with atmospheric oxygen. Dross must be periodically skimmed from the wave surface to prevent it from being deposited onto PCBs; excessive dross formation increases solder consumption and maintenance frequency.

Dendrite / Dendritic Growth

A tree-like or fern-like conductive filament that grows between two conductors at different potentials in the presence of moisture and ionic contamination, eventually bridging the gap and causing a short circuit. Dendritic growth is a form of electrochemical migration most commonly observed with silver (silver migration) but also affects copper and tin; it is detected by SIR testing and prevented through cleaning, conformal coating, and adequate conductor spacing.

Dewetting

A soldering defect in which molten solder initially wets and coats a surface, then recedes, leaving behind an irregular, thin solder coating with exposed areas of the base metal. Dewetting is distinguished from non-wetting (in which the solder never wets the surface at all) and is caused by excessive time at soldering temperature, inadequate flux, or contaminated surfaces.

Dimensional Stability

The ability of a laminate material to maintain its X-Y dimensions during the thermal and chemical processing of PCB fabrication. Dimensional changes — shrinkage or expansion — during lamination and subsequent processes cause misregistration between layers; laminates with good dimensional stability minimise this movement and are essential for high-layer-count and HDI boards with tight registration tolerances.

Dummy Pad

A non-functional copper pad added to a PCB layer — typically on inner layers — to balance copper density across the panel for uniform electroplating or to provide additional bonding area during lamination. Dummy pads are electrically isolated from active circuitry and are often used when a layer would otherwise have large areas of bare laminate surrounded by dense circuitry.

Dk/Df Variation

The change in a laminate's dielectric constant and dissipation factor with frequency, temperature, and moisture content. Dk typically decreases and Df increases with rising frequency and temperature; these variations must be accounted for in wideband RF designs and in high-speed digital links where the signal spectrum spans several decades of frequency, requiring the designer to simulate at the extremes of material behaviour rather than at a single nominal value.

Dual-Side Assembly

A PCB assembly with surface-mount components on both the top and bottom sides of the board, requiring two solder-paste printing and reflow cycles (one for each side). Dual-side assembly doubles the component density per board area but complicates the assembly process: the first-side components must be secured during the second reflow cycle by the surface tension of the solidified solder, and heavy components on the bottom side may require adhesive bonding to prevent them from falling off during the second reflow.

E

ENIG (Electroless Nickel Immersion Gold)

A two-layer surface finish consisting of an electroless nickel layer (3-7 um) providing a diffusion barrier and hard solderable surface, capped with a thin immersion gold layer (0.05-0.125 um) that protects the nickel from oxidation. ENIG is the most widely used high-reliability surface finish because it offers excellent planarity for fine-pitch SMT, good shelf life, multiple reflow capability, and compatibility with both solder and aluminium wire bonding — though it requires strict bath control to avoid black-pad defects.

ENEPIG (Electroless Nickel Electroless Palladium Immersion Gold)

An enhanced surface finish that adds an electroless palladium layer (0.05-0.1 um) between the nickel and immersion gold layers. The palladium layer acts as an additional barrier that prevents nickel corrosion during gold deposition, essentially eliminating the black-pad risk of ENIG, and provides a surface compatible with both solder, gold-wire, and copper-wire bonding — making it the preferred finish for mixed-assembly technologies.

Etching

The chemical process of selectively removing unwanted copper from a PCB panel to leave behind the desired circuit pattern (see Chemical Etching). In PCB fabrication, etching is performed on inner layers (after develop/etch/strip) and outer layers (after pattern plating), and the precision of the etching process directly determines the final trace width and spacing.

Etch Factor

The ratio of the depth of etch to the amount of lateral undercut below the resist edge, describing the cross-sectional shape of an etched trace. A higher etch factor indicates a more vertical sidewall; typical etch factors for production PCB etching are 1:1 to 3:1, meaning that for each micron of copper removed vertically, 0.3-1.0 micron is removed laterally (undercut). Controlled impedance designs must account for etch factor to achieve target trace widths.

Edge Connector

A row of gold-plated contact pads (fingers) along one edge of a PCB that mates with a corresponding socket connector. Edge connectors are fabricated with hard electrolytic gold over nickel plating to withstand hundreds to thousands of insertion cycles, and the PCB edge is typically bevelled to reduce insertion force and plating wear.

Electroless Copper

A thin layer of copper (0.5-1.5 um) deposited onto the hole walls of a PCB through an autocatalytic chemical reduction reaction rather than electrolytic plating. Electroless copper provides the initial conductive layer that enables subsequent electrolytic copper plating to build up the full plated-through-hole barrel thickness; without it, the non-conductive hole walls could not be electroplated.

Electromigration

The transport of metal ions through a conductor or across an insulating medium under the influence of an electric field and elevated temperature. In PCBs, electromigration typically refers to dendritic growth between adjacent conductors under DC bias in the presence of moisture and ionic contamination, leading to current leakage and eventual short circuits (see also CAF and Silver Migration).

Electroplating

The electrochemical process of depositing a metal coating onto a conductive surface by passing direct current through an electrolyte solution. In PCB manufacturing, copper electroplating thickens the electroless copper seed layer on hole walls to the required 20-25 um barrel thickness; nickel and gold electroplating provide hard-wearing edge-connector finishes and solderable surface finishes.

Embedded Capacitance

A technology in which distributed capacitance is created within the PCB stackup by using ultra-thin laminate layers with high dielectric constant between power and ground planes. Embedded capacitance reduces the number of discrete decoupling capacitors required on the board surface, lowers PDN impedance, and saves assembly cost and board real estate in high-density designs.

EMI / EMC

Electromagnetic Interference (EMI) is unwanted electromagnetic energy emitted from or conducted onto a PCB that can disrupt the operation of nearby equipment. Electromagnetic Compatibility (EMC) is the broader discipline of ensuring that a product neither emits excessive EMI nor is susceptible to interference from external sources. PCB-level EMC design involves stackup planning for return-path continuity, proper grounding, filtering, and shielding.

End Cap

The metallised termination at each end of a chip-style SMD component (resistor, capacitor, inductor) that provides the solderable surface. End caps typically consist of a fired silver or copper base layer, a nickel barrier layer to prevent leaching, and a tin or tin-lead outer layer for solderability.

ENTEK / OSP

Organic Solderability Preservative — a thin, transparent organic coating applied to copper pads to prevent oxidation and preserve solderability during storage. OSP is a low-cost, environmentally friendly surface finish that is displaced by flux during soldering; it provides a very flat surface ideal for fine-pitch SMT but has a limited shelf life (typically 6-12 months) and is not suitable for multiple reflow cycles without reapplication.

Epoxy

A thermosetting polymer resin system that forms the matrix of FR-4 and other glass-reinforced PCB laminates. Epoxy resins provide good mechanical strength, chemical resistance, and electrical insulation, and are cross-linked (cured) during the lamination cycle. The epoxy system — including resin, hardener, and fillers — largely determines the laminate's Tg, CTE, and moisture absorption characteristics.

E-Test (Electrical Test)

The bare-board test process that verifies every PCB net for continuity (no unintended opens) and every pair of nets for isolation (no unintended shorts) against the design netlist. E-test can be performed with flying-probe testers (no fixture cost, slower) or bed-of-nails fixtures (high fixture cost, fast throughput); both methods apply voltages and measure resistances at every test point on the board.

Excellon Format

The industry-standard CNC file format for specifying PCB drilling and routing operations. An Excellon file contains tool definitions (drill sizes), X-Y coordinates for each hole, and optional routing paths; modern Excellon 2 format supports slots, blind and buried vias, and back-drilling definitions alongside the traditional circular drill commands.

Exposure

The process step in which a UV light source illuminates a photopolymer resist (dry film or liquid photoimageable solder mask) through a photomask or via laser direct imaging to define the pattern that will be developed. Proper exposure energy and collimation are critical for achieving vertical sidewalls and accurate feature dimensions, particularly for fine-pitch designs.

Eutectic Solder

A solder alloy that transitions directly from solid to liquid at a single, well-defined temperature with no pasty phase. The traditional Sn63Pb37 eutectic solder melts at exactly 183 degC; the most common lead-free eutectic, Sn96.5Ag3.0Cu0.5 (SAC305), melts at approximately 217-220 degC, though it is near-eutectic rather than truly eutectic.

Eyelet

A small metal tube inserted into a non-plated PCB hole and flared or rolled over at both ends to create a mechanical through-hole connection. Eyelets are used in low-cost single-sided boards, as a repair technique for damaged plated holes, and in applications where a robust mechanical connection is required in addition to electrical continuity.

Edge Clearance

The minimum distance between any copper feature (trace, pad, plane) and the board outline edge. Standard edge clearance is typically 0.25-0.50 mm for internal copper and larger for outer-layer copper to prevent exposure during routing or V-scoring; insufficient edge clearance risks copper exposure, corrosion ingress, and short circuits at the board edge.

Epoxy Smear

A thin layer of epoxy resin that is mechanically smeared across the surface of inner-layer copper pads during the drilling process. Epoxy smear must be removed by desmear chemistry before electroless copper deposition; if not removed, it prevents the plated copper from making a reliable electrical and mechanical connection to the inner-layer pad.

ESD (Electrostatic Discharge)

The sudden flow of electricity between two objects at different electrostatic potentials, caused by direct contact or dielectric breakdown. ESD can catastrophically damage or latently degrade semiconductor devices on a PCB; ESD-protected areas (EPAs) with grounded work surfaces, wrist straps, footwear, and ionisers are mandatory throughout PCB assembly, and ESD-protective packaging is used for storing and transporting assemblies.

Edge Plating

Copper plating applied to the vertical edges of a PCB, typically on the board outline or on the walls of internal cut-outs, to create an electrical connection from the surface to the edge or to provide an EMI shielding surface. Edge plating is an additional process beyond standard PCB fabrication and is specified on the fabrication drawing when required for grounding, shielding, or edge-connector applications.

Etchback

A controlled chemical process that removes a small amount of dielectric material from the walls of a drilled hole, recessing the laminate below the level of the inner-layer copper pads. Positive etchback — where the copper pads protrude slightly into the hole — ensures that the electroless and electroplated copper physically interlocks with the inner-layer pads, creating a more reliable three-point connection than a simple butt joint.

Embedded Passives

Resistors, capacitors, or inductors formed within the PCB stackup — as planar patterns on internal layers (planar capacitors between power and ground planes, spiral inductors on signal layers) or as thick-film resistive material printed and cured on inner layers — rather than as discrete surface-mount components. Embedded passives free up surface real estate, reduce parasitic inductance, and improve reliability by eliminating solder joints, though they increase PCB fabrication complexity and limit the ability to tune values after the board is manufactured.

Electrochemical Migration (ECM)

An umbrella term for metal-ion transport across an insulating surface under DC bias and humidity, encompassing silver migration, copper dendritic growth, CAF, and tin whisker formation. ECM is a primary long-term reliability concern for high-density PCBs operating in uncontrolled environments and is assessed through SIR testing at elevated temperature and humidity with applied DC bias per IPC-TM-650.

F

FR-4

The dominant PCB base material — a flame-retardant (FR) composite of woven fibreglass cloth impregnated with epoxy resin. FR-4 provides an excellent balance of mechanical strength, electrical insulation, thermal performance, flame resistance (UL 94 V-0), and cost. Standard FR-4 has a glass transition temperature (Tg) of 130-140 degC; high-Tg FR-4 variants (170-180 degC) are available for lead-free assembly and high-reliability applications.

Flying Probe Test

An electrical test method in which a small number of high-speed, programmable probes move across the PCB surface making sequential contact with test points to perform continuity and isolation testing. Flying probe testers require no custom fixture, making them economical for prototypes and small production runs, but are slower than bed-of-nails fixture testers for high-volume production.

Fine Pitch

A term describing SMD components with lead or ball spacing (pitch) smaller than approximately 0.5 mm. Fine-pitch devices (down to 0.3 mm for QFP and 0.4 mm for BGA) place stringent demands on solder-paste printing accuracy, stencil design, pad geometry, and reflow profile control, and require laser-cut or electroformed stencils for reliable paste release.

Fiducial

A small, precisely-placed circular copper pad with a contrasting background (typically solder-mask clearance around a bare copper pad) used as an optical alignment target by automated assembly equipment. Global fiducials (typically three near the corners of the board or panel) establish the overall coordinate reference; local fiducials near fine-pitch components correct for local placement errors.

First Article

The first production unit or sample produced using the intended production tooling, materials, and processes, subjected to comprehensive inspection and testing to verify that the manufacturing process is capable of meeting all design requirements. First-article inspection (FAI) in PCB fabrication typically includes dimensional verification, cross-section analysis, and electrical testing documented per IPC-6012 or AS9102.

Fixture

A custom mechanical assembly — typically a bed-of-nails — that holds a PCB and provides electrical contact to test points for in-circuit or functional testing. Test fixtures are designed for a specific PCB layout and are a significant non-recurring engineering (NRE) cost; the layout must include adequate test-point provision to make fixture-based testing feasible.

Flex PCB (FPC)

A printed circuit manufactured on a flexible polyimide or polyester substrate that can be bent, folded, or flexed during installation or operation. Flex PCBs replace wiring harnesses in applications where space, weight, or dynamic flexing are critical — smartphones, cameras, medical devices, and aerospace equipment being prime examples. Flex circuits can be single-sided, double-sided, or multilayer with or without stiffeners.

Flex-Rigid PCB

A hybrid PCB construction that combines rigid FR-4 sections (for component mounting and connector attachment) with flexible polyimide sections (for interconnection between rigid sections) into a single, monolithic board. Rigid-flex eliminates connectors and cables between sub-assemblies, improving reliability and reducing assembly cost in applications such as foldable electronics, avionics, and medical implants.

Flexural Strength

A mechanical property quantifying a laminate material's ability to resist deformation under bending load. Flexural strength, measured per IPC-TM-650, is important for thin PCBs, large-format boards that may sag, and applications subject to mechanical vibration or shock.

Flux

A chemical cleaning agent that removes oxides from metal surfaces during soldering, promotes wetting of the molten solder to the surfaces being joined, and protects the joint from re-oxidation during the soldering process. Flux chemistries range from rosin-based (R, RMA, RA) to water-soluble organic acid (OA) to no-clean formulations; the choice affects cleaning requirements, residue corrosivity, and long-term reliability.

Footprint (Land Pattern)

The specific arrangement and dimensions of copper pads on a PCB that match the terminals of a particular component package. The footprint includes pad sizes, pad shapes (rectangle, rounded rectangle, oblong), pad-to-pad spacing, and the courtyard outline that reserves space for the component body and placement tolerance. Footprint dimensions are derived from the component manufacturer's recommended land pattern, typically following IPC-7351 guidelines.

FR-408

A high-performance FR-4 variant with a lower dielectric constant (Dk ~3.7) and lower dissipation factor (Df ~0.009) than standard FR-4, designed for high-speed digital applications. FR-408 and its successors (FR-408HR) maintain the processability of conventional FR-4 while providing signal-integrity improvements that approach those of more expensive RF laminates.

Functional Test

A test methodology that exercises a fully assembled PCB under conditions simulating its intended operational environment — applying power, injecting input signals, and measuring outputs — to verify that the board performs its designed function. Functional test complements ICT and flying-probe testing by catching faults that electrical continuity testing cannot, such as incorrect component values, timing violations, and parametric failures.

Fusing

In PCB fabrication, a thermal process that bonds copper foil to the laminate substrate by passing the copper-clad panel through heated rollers under pressure. In assembly, fusing can also refer to the controlled melting of a solder-plated surface to create a uniform, flat coating.

Fab Drawing (Fabrication Drawing)

The master mechanical document that communicates all physical requirements for PCB fabrication, including board outline and dimensions, material specification, layer stackup, drill chart, surface finish, solder mask colour and type, silkscreen requirements, impedance-control specification, and applicable IPC performance class. The fab drawing, together with the Gerber/ODB++ data, forms the complete manufacturing data package.

Filled Via

A via whose barrel is filled — typically with conductive epoxy, non-conductive epoxy, or copper plating — to create a planar surface. Via filling is essential for via-in-pad designs where components are placed directly over vias; it prevents solder from wicking down into the via during reflow, which would starve the solder joint and potentially create a void.

Gold Finger

The gold-plated contact pads along the edge of a PCB edge connector, fabricated with hard electrolytic gold (0.75-1.5 um) over a nickel barrier (3-5 um) to provide wear resistance through hundreds or thousands of mating cycles. Gold fingers are the defining feature of plug-in PCB modules and must be manufactured with a lead-in bevel and precise dimensional control for reliable connector engagement.

Fiducial Mark

See Fiducial. The precision optical target used by automated assembly equipment to determine the position and orientation of a PCB panel. Fiducial marks are typically 1.0-3.0 mm diameter bare copper circles on a solder-mask-cleared background, and a minimum of two global fiducials (three preferred) per panel are required for accurate pick-and-place and AOI operation.

Four-Wire Kelvin Connection

A measurement technique that uses separate pairs of current-carrying and voltage-sensing conductors to eliminate the effect of lead resistance on low-resistance measurements. In PCB testing, Kelvin (4-wire) probing is used for precise measurement of very low resistances such as net continuity verification in high-current planes and for accurate milliohm-level measurements of contact resistance.

Fillet

The concave meniscus of solder that forms at the junction between a component lead or termination and the PCB pad, created by the surface tension of molten solder wetting both surfaces. The fillet's shape, height, and wetting angle are the primary visual indicators of solder-joint quality per IPC-A-610 criteria; a well-formed fillet provides both electrical continuity and mechanical reinforcement.

FR-1 / FR-2

Low-cost laminate materials based on phenolic resin with paper reinforcement (FR-2) or a mixed paper-glass composition (FR-1), used for simple single-sided consumer PCBs. These materials have significantly lower mechanical strength, poorer electrical properties, and lower temperature resistance than FR-4, but cost 50-70% less and are adequate for low-complexity, high-volume products such as remote controls and LED bulbs.

Flux Residue

The non-volatile components of flux that remain on a PCB assembly after soldering — rosin, resins, activators, and thickeners. No-clean flux residues are designed to be left on the board without causing corrosion or leakage currents; water-soluble (OA) flux residues are corrosive and must be completely removed by washing. Flux residue that bridges between conductors can reduce SIR and promote electrochemical migration.

Fused Lead

A solder-dipped or hot-solder-coated component lead that has been reheated after initial coating to create a uniform, smooth surface finish. Fused leads provide better solderability and insertion characteristics in through-hole components; the term is also used for solder-plated surface finishes that have been reflowed to improve planarity.

Flying Lead

A wire or flex-circuit tail that extends beyond the PCB outline to make connection to an off-board component or to provide a service loop for assembly and rework. Flying leads on rigid-flex PCBs are designed with strain-relief features to prevent fatigue at the flex-to-rigid transition, and their length, bend radius, and termination method are critical mechanical design parameters.

G

Gerber File

The universal vector-image format for PCB fabrication data, describing the copper layers, solder mask, silkscreen, solder paste, and other layers as a series of draw and flash commands. Gerber RS-274X (extended Gerber) embeds aperture definitions within the file; Gerber X2 adds metadata such as layer function, pad types, and net names. Every PCB fabricator in the world accepts Gerber format data.

Gerber X2

The second-generation Gerber format that adds embedded metadata to the traditional graphical description — including layer function (e.g., Top Copper, Bottom Solder Mask), pad/feature types, net names, and impedance-controlled trace identification. X2 metadata reduces CAM-operator interpretation errors and enables more automated DFM analysis compared to RS-274X.

Gold Flash

A very thin layer of gold (0.025-0.05 um) electroplated over nickel, used as a solderability preservative rather than a wear surface. Gold flash is not thick enough for edge-connector applications — see Hard Gold for those requirements. The term is sometimes used synonymously with immersion gold (ENIG) in casual conversation, though the processes are chemically distinct.

Immersion Gold

A gold layer deposited onto a nickel surface through a chemical displacement reaction in which gold atoms replace nickel atoms at the surface, forming a self-limiting thin layer (typically 0.05-0.125 um). Immersion gold does not require electrical current, producing a uniformly thin coating regardless of the board geometry. See ENIG.

Hard Gold

Electrolytic gold plating (0.5-5.0 um) containing cobalt or nickel alloying elements that increase hardness and wear resistance. Hard gold is plated over a nickel barrier layer and is the required finish for edge-connector fingers and other high-cycle-count contact surfaces. It is more expensive than ENIG and requires a plating bus to connect all contacts during the electroplating process.

Ground Plane

A continuous copper area (typically an entire layer) that serves as the 0V reference potential for all circuits on the board. The ground plane provides a low-impedance return path for signal currents, reduces EMI by minimising loop area, and acts as one side of controlled-impedance transmission lines. A well-designed ground plane is unbroken — no slots or gaps — in the path of high-speed return currents.

Grid Array

An IC package family — including BGA (Ball Grid Array), LGA (Land Grid Array), and PGA (Pin Grid Array) — in which connections are arranged in a two-dimensional matrix across the underside of the package rather than in peripheral rows. Grid-array packages maximise interconnect density per unit area and are the dominant format for high-pin-count devices.

Ground Fill

Copper polygons poured on signal layers and connected to ground potential to improve EMI performance, provide thermal spreading, and balance copper distribution for uniform plating and etching. Ground fill must be adequately stitched to the main ground plane with vias to prevent it from acting as an unintended antenna or resonator.

Guide Hole

A non-plated hole used for mechanical alignment — typically a tooling hole that engages with alignment pins in fabrication and assembly fixtures. Guide holes are usually located in the panel rails or in non-functional areas of the board and are specified with tight positional and diameter tolerances.

Gull-Wing Lead

A surface-mount component lead shape that extends outward from the package body and bends downward and outward at the foot, resembling a seagull in flight. Gull-wing leads are characteristic of QFP, SOIC, and SSOP packages and provide compliance that absorbs some of the CTE mismatch stress between the component and PCB during thermal cycling.

Tg (Glass Transition Temperature)

The temperature at which a polymer transitions from a rigid, glassy state to a softer, rubbery state. Below Tg, the material's CTE in the Z-axis is relatively low (~30-50 ppm/degC for FR-4); above Tg, Z-axis CTE increases dramatically (to 200-300 ppm/degC for standard FR-4). The laminate Tg must comfortably exceed all assembly and operating temperatures to maintain dimensional stability and plated-hole reliability. Standard FR-4 Tg is 130-140 degC; high-Tg materials are 170-180 degC.

Glass Weave

The woven glass fabric that provides mechanical reinforcement in PCB laminates. The weave pattern, thread count, and fibre-bundle dimensions create a microscopically non-uniform dielectric medium — signals routed parallel to glass bundles see a different effective Dk than signals routed at an angle — a phenomenon called the fibre-weave effect that becomes significant above ~5 Gbps and is mitigated by routing at an angle to the weave or using spread-glass laminates.

Glass-to-Resin Ratio

The proportion of glass fibre to epoxy resin in a PCB laminate, which affects the effective dielectric constant, mechanical strength, and drilling characteristics. Higher glass content increases mechanical rigidity and Dk but makes drilling more abrasive; standard FR-4 is approximately 40-60% glass by volume.

GND

The standard abbreviation for "ground" in schematic diagrams, PCB layouts, and test documentation. The GND net is the reference potential (0V) and current return path for all circuits; every GND pin and via must be connected to the main ground plane through a low-impedance path.

Golden Board

A fully assembled and tested PCB known to be fully functional and conforming to all specifications, used as the reference standard for calibrating and programming automated test equipment. AOI systems use golden-board image comparison; ICT and functional test systems use golden-board measurements to establish test limits. The integrity of the golden board is critical because any undetected defect will be coded as "acceptable" in the test system.

Gerber RS-274X

The extended Gerber format that embeds aperture definitions (shapes and sizes) within the data file, eliminating the separate aperture list file that earlier RS-274D required. RS-274X remains widely used and accepted, though Gerber X2 is gradually superseding it because of the metadata advantages.

Galvanic Corrosion

An electrochemical corrosion process that occurs when two dissimilar metals are in electrical contact in the presence of an electrolyte, causing preferential corrosion of the more anodic metal. In PCBs, galvanic corrosion is a concern at the interface between gold and nickel in ENIG finish (see Black Pad) and between copper and tin in solder joints exposed to moisture and electrical bias.

Glass Transition

The reversible physical change in an amorphous polymer (such as the epoxy resin in FR-4) from a hard, glassy state to a soft, rubbery state as temperature increases through the Tg. Below Tg, the polymer chains have limited mobility; above Tg, chain segments can move more freely, resulting in a dramatic increase in CTE and decrease in modulus. This transition is the fundamental phenomenon behind why Tg is the most critical thermal specification for PCB laminates.

Guard Trace / Guard Ring

A copper trace or ring connected to a low-impedance reference (typically ground) and placed adjacent to a sensitive high-impedance signal trace to intercept leakage currents and shield against capacitive coupling. Guard traces are a standard technique in precision analogue PCB design for protecting microampere- and nanoampere-level signals from surface leakage across the board.

Grid Pitch

The centre-to-centre spacing of the ball or land array on a BGA or LGA package. Common grid pitches include 1.27 mm, 1.0 mm, 0.8 mm, 0.65 mm, 0.5 mm, and 0.4 mm; finer grid pitches enable higher interconnect density but reduce the space available for escape routing and increase the fabrication challenge for the PCB, often forcing the use of via-in-pad, microvias, or reduced trace/space geometry under the BGA.

Ground Bounce

A transient voltage fluctuation on the ground reference of a digital IC caused by the simultaneous switching of multiple output drivers, which draws a sharp current pulse through the parasitic inductance of the ground path (bond wires, package leads, PCB traces, and vias). Ground bounce shifts the internal ground reference of the IC, potentially causing false switching in input receivers; it is mitigated by minimising ground-path inductance through short, wide connections, multiple ground vias, and adequate decoupling capacitance.

H

HASL (Hot Air Solder Leveling)

A surface finish process in which a bare PCB is dipped in molten solder and then passed between hot-air knives that blow excess solder off the pads and out of the holes, leaving a thin, uniform solder coating. HASL provides excellent solderability and shelf life; the traditional Sn63Pb37 HASL has been largely superseded by lead-free HASL (SnCu, SnAgCu alloys) to meet RoHS requirements. HASL's main limitation is poor planarity, making it unsuitable for fine-pitch (<0.5 mm) SMT.

Lead-Free HASL

A RoHS-compliant variant of HASL using lead-free solder alloys — typically SnCu0.7 or SnAgCu (SAC) — instead of traditional tin-lead. Lead-free HASL requires higher processing temperatures (260-270 degC vs. 230-250 degC for SnPb), which increases thermal stress on the PCB, but provides comparable solderability and planarity with reduced environmental impact.

HDI (High-Density Interconnect)

A PCB technology characterised by fine lines (<100 um), small vias (<150 um microvias), small capture pads, and high connection-pad density — typically greater than 20 pads/cm2. HDI boards use sequential lamination (build-up) processes with laser-drilled microvias to achieve routing densities impossible with conventional through-hole technology, and are the standard for smartphones, tablets, and advanced computing platforms.

Heavy Copper

PCBs fabricated with copper weights of 3 oz/ft2 (105 um) or greater — up to 20 oz/ft2 (700 um) for extreme high-current applications. Heavy-copper PCBs are used in power supplies, motor drives, welding equipment, and power distribution where standard 1-2 oz copper cannot carry the required current without excessive temperature rise.

High Tg

Laminate materials with a glass transition temperature of 170 degC or higher, specified for lead-free assembly (where peak reflow temperatures reach 245-260 degC) and for high-reliability applications involving elevated operating temperatures. High-Tg FR-4 materials reduce Z-axis expansion, improving plated-through-hole reliability during thermal cycling.

High-Speed PCB

A PCB designed to carry signals with rise times fast enough that transmission-line effects — reflections, crosstalk, dispersion, and loss — become significant and must be managed through controlled impedance, careful stackup design, and appropriate material selection. The threshold is not a specific frequency but rather the ratio of signal rise time to the propagation delay along the interconnect.

Hole Wall Quality

A qualitative and quantitative assessment of the drilled hole's interior surface, including surface roughness, the presence of resin smear, glass-fibre protrusion, and copper adhesion. Poor hole-wall quality leads to plating voids, barrel cracks, and reduced long-term reliability; it is evaluated by cross-section analysis per IPC-6012 criteria.

Hole Size Tolerance

The permissible variation in a finished hole's diameter from the specified nominal dimension. Typical hole-size tolerances are +/-0.075 mm for plated holes and +/-0.05 mm for non-plated holes, though tighter tolerances are achievable with premium processes. Hole size directly affects component fit and the quantity of solder in a through-hole joint.

Hybrid PCB

A PCB that incorporates two or more different laminate material types in the same stackup — for example, combining standard FR-4 layers for power and low-speed signals with high-frequency PTFE-based layers for RF circuitry. Hybrid constructions optimise cost and performance but require careful process control to manage the different CTE, lamination temperature, and resin-flow characteristics of the disparate materials.

Halogen-Free

Laminate materials manufactured without brominated flame retardants or other halogen-containing compounds, meeting the IEC 61249-2-21 standard of less than 900 ppm chlorine, 900 ppm bromine, and 1500 ppm total halogens. Halogen-free laminates are driven by environmental regulations and corporate sustainability commitments, particularly in consumer electronics destined for the European market.

Heat Sink

A thermally conductive structure — typically aluminium or copper with fins or pins — attached to a heat-generating component to increase the surface area for convection cooling. In PCB design, thermal management may involve discrete heat sinks, copper coin inserts in the PCB, metal-core substrates, or arrays of thermal vias that conduct heat from a component pad to a large copper area or an external heat sink.

HiPot Test (High Potential Test)

A safety test that applies a high voltage (typically 2x rated voltage + 1000V for mains-connected equipment) between conductors that should be galvanically isolated — such as between primary and secondary sides of a transformer — to verify the integrity of the insulation. HiPot testing is a regulatory requirement for safety-certified products and must be specified on the fabrication drawing when inter-layer or intra-layer voltage withstand is critical.

Hot Bar Soldering

A thermode-based soldering process in which a heated bar (thermode) is pressed onto a flex circuit tail or connector to simultaneously reflow all connections, commonly used for attaching flex PCBs to rigid boards or displays. Hot-bar soldering requires specific pad geometry, pre-tinning, and precise control of temperature, pressure, and dwell time.

Hybrid Stackup

A PCB layer stackup that mixes different material types — for example, combining high-speed/low-loss laminates on outer layers for RF signal routing with lower-cost FR-4 cores for inner power and digital layers. Hybrid stackups optimise the cost-performance trade-off but require careful material selection to match CTE and lamination characteristics and avoid processing-induced warp.

Hydroscopic / Hygroscopic

Describes a material's tendency to absorb moisture from the ambient environment. PCB laminates, particularly FR-4, are hygroscopic; absorbed moisture vaporises explosively during soldering temperatures, causing delamination (popcorning) or blistering. Moisture-sensitive components (MSL-rated) and bare PCBs must be stored in low-humidity conditions and may require baking before assembly if the exposure limit is exceeded.

Half-Hole / Castellated Hole

A plated through-hole that has been cut in half along its axis, typically at the edge of a PCB or along a breakaway tab, creating a semi-circular solderable pad on the board edge. Castellated holes are used to create edge-connection points for PCB modules that are soldered onto a host board, such as Bluetooth modules, Wi-Fi modules, and small daughterboards.

HASL Planarity

The degree to which the solder-coated surface of a HASL-finished pad deviates from a flat plane. HASL planarity is inherently inferior to flat finishes such as ENIG, ENEPIG, OSP, and immersion silver/tin because the liquid solder forms a dome upon solidification. Poor planarity can cause coplanarity issues for fine-pitch components and is the primary reason HASL is not recommended for pitches below 0.5 mm.

HASL Thickness

The thickness of the solder coating on a HASL-finished pad, typically 1-25 um as measured from the copper surface. Minimum HASL thickness on the pad surface must ensure complete copper coverage; on plated hole walls, a minimum of 3 um is typically specified to ensure a solderable inner surface.

Hole Plugging

The process of filling a plated through-hole or via with a material — typically conductive or non-conductive epoxy — to create a solder-mask-covered, planar surface over the hole. Hole plugging prevents solder migration into the hole during reflow (which would starve an adjacent solder joint) and enables via-in-pad designs with components placed directly over filled, capped vias.

HASL vs ENIG

A frequent surface-finish comparison: HASL provides excellent solderability at the lowest cost but with poor planarity, making it unsuitable for fine-pitch SMT; ENIG provides exceptional planarity, long shelf life, and wire-bonding compatibility at higher cost and with a risk of black-pad defect if process control is inadequate. The choice between them is typically driven by the finest component pitch on the board, the required shelf life, and whether RoHS compliance mandates lead-free HASL.

Hole Density

The number of drilled holes per unit area of a PCB, typically expressed in holes per square decimetre (holes/dm2) or per square inch. High hole density increases drilling time and cost, creates more opportunities for drill-bit breakage, and can complicate electroplating current distribution; it is a key factor in PCB fabrication cost estimation.

Hipot (Dielectric Withstand)

See HiPot Test. A high-voltage test applied between electrically isolated conductors on a PCB to verify the integrity of the insulation and ensure that no breakdown occurs at the specified test voltage. The test voltage, ramp rate, dwell time, and permitted leakage current are specified based on the working voltage and the applicable safety standard.

Heat-Sink Slug

A thick copper or aluminium coin — typically 0.5-3.0 mm thick — embedded or press-fit into a PCB directly beneath a high-power component to provide a low-thermal-resistance path from the component to the opposite side of the board or to an external heat sink. Heat-sink slugs offer far better through-thickness thermal conductivity than thermal vias alone and are used in power-converter, LED-lighting, and RF-power-amplifier PCBs.

Halogen Content

The amount of halogen elements — principally chlorine and bromine (from flame retardants) — present in a PCB laminate, restricted by the IEC 61249-2-21 standard to less than 900 ppm each and 1500 ppm total for halogen-free designation. Halogen content is measured by combustion ion chromatography and is reported on the laminate manufacturer's certificate of analysis as part of the material declaration for RoHS and WEEE compliance.

I

IPC

The trade association (formerly the Institute of Printed Circuits, now IPC — Association Connecting Electronics Industries) that develops and publishes the globally recognised standards for PCB design, fabrication, assembly, and acceptance. Key IPC standards include IPC-6012 (rigid PCB qualification), IPC-A-610 (acceptability of electronic assemblies), J-STD-001 (soldering requirements), IPC-2221 (generic design standard), and IPC-7351 (land pattern standard).

Impedance

The total opposition a circuit presents to alternating current, comprising both resistance (real part) and reactance (imaginary part). In PCB transmission-line design, characteristic impedance (Z0) is the impedance seen by a signal propagating along an infinitely long or perfectly terminated line, determined by the cross-sectional geometry of the trace and the dielectric properties of the surrounding materials.

Impedance Control

The practice of designing and fabricating PCB transmission lines to achieve a specified characteristic impedance within a defined tolerance. Impedance control requires the PCB designer to calculate trace width and spacing for the chosen stackup, and the fabricator to adjust the artwork for etch factor and to hold dielectric thickness and Dk within tolerance. Impedance test coupons on every panel verify conformance.

Inner Layer

Any conductive layer of a multilayer PCB that is not the top or bottom outer layer. Inner layers are etched on core laminates before the multilayer stack is assembled, undergo black/brown oxide treatment for adhesion, and are permanently sealed within the finished board. Inner layers are typically used for signal routing, power distribution, and ground reference planes.

Inner Layer Clearance

The distance between the edge of an inner-layer copper feature (trace, pad, or plane) and the nearest plated through-hole that is not connected to that feature. This clearance — typically 0.2-0.5 mm — ensures that the drill does not inadvertently short the hole to the inner-layer copper and that the anti-pad around the hole provides adequate voltage isolation.

IST (Interconnect Stress Test)

An accelerated reliability test that cycles a dedicated test coupon through repeated heating (by passing current through a chain of plated through-holes) and cooling to induce fatigue failure. IST measures the number of cycles before a specified resistance increase occurs and is used to qualify laminate materials, plating processes, and board designs for high-reliability applications.

Immersion Silver

A surface finish in which a thin layer of silver (0.15-0.5 um) is deposited onto copper pads through a direct displacement reaction. Immersion silver provides excellent planarity for fine-pitch SMT, good solderability, and wire-bonding compatibility at lower cost than ENIG. Its main limitation is susceptibility to tarnish (silver sulphide formation) in sulphur-rich environments, which is mitigated by anti-tarnish additives in the plating bath.

Immersion Tin

A surface finish in which a thin layer of pure tin (0.8-1.2 um) is deposited onto copper pads through a chemical displacement reaction. Immersion tin provides a very flat surface suitable for fine-pitch applications and is easily soldered, but it has a shorter shelf life than ENIG and can develop tin whiskers under certain conditions. Immersion tin also forms a copper-tin intermetallic that can degrade solderability if the tin layer is too thin or aged.

ICT (In-Circuit Test)

A production test methodology that uses a bed-of-nails fixture to make electrical contact with every net on an assembled PCB, then individually tests each component — resistors, capacitors, inductors, diodes, transistors, and simple ICs — for correct value, orientation, and presence. ICT provides high fault coverage for manufacturing defects but requires dedicated test points on every net and substantial fixture NRE cost.

Insertion Loss

The reduction in signal power caused by inserting a device — such as a PCB trace, connector, or passive component — into a transmission path, expressed in decibels (dB). In PCB design, insertion loss is the sum of conductor loss (skin effect and surface roughness) and dielectric loss (dissipation factor of the laminate), and it is the primary signal-integrity constraint for long, high-speed serial links.

Insulation Resistance (IR)

The DC resistance measured between two conductors separated by an insulator — the higher the insulation resistance, the better the isolation. IPC-6012 requires a minimum insulation resistance of 100 Mohm for rigid PCBs (typically tested at 500V DC after temperature/humidity conditioning). Low insulation resistance indicates contamination, moisture absorption, or CAF growth.

IATF 16949

The international quality management system standard for automotive production, incorporating ISO 9001 with additional automotive-specific requirements. PCB and PCBA suppliers to the automotive industry are expected to hold IATF 16949 certification, which mandates advanced product quality planning (APQP), production part approval process (PPAP), failure mode and effects analysis (FMEA), and statistical process control (SPC).

ISO 9001

The international standard for quality management systems (QMS), specifying requirements for documented processes, management responsibility, resource management, product realisation, and measurement/analysis/improvement. Most professional PCB fabricators and assemblers hold ISO 9001 certification as a baseline demonstration of process control and continuous improvement capability.

Inner-Layer Registration

The positional accuracy with which inner-layer patterns align to one another and to the drill pattern after lamination. Registration tolerance is a function of artwork accuracy, material dimensional stability during lamination, and tooling-hole precision. Typical inner-layer registration tolerance is +/-0.075 mm; tighter registration enables smaller annular rings and higher routing density.

Isola

A leading global manufacturer of copper-clad laminates and prepreg materials for PCB fabrication. Isola's product families — including FR-408HR, IS410, IS415, I-Speed, I-Tera MT40, Astra MT77, and Tachyon 100G — span the range from standard FR-4 processing to ultra-low-loss materials for 112 Gbps PAM-4 signalling, and are widely specified by name in PCB fabrication drawings.

IMS (Insulated Metal Substrate)

A PCB construction in which the copper circuit layer is bonded to a metal base plate (typically aluminium) with a thin, thermally conductive but electrically insulating dielectric layer in between. IMS boards (also called metal-core PCBs or MCPCBs) provide superior heat dissipation for high-power LEDs, power converters, and motor drives, with effective thermal conductivities of 1-10 W/mK compared to ~0.3 W/mK for standard FR-4.

Ion Migration

A failure mechanism in which metal ions migrate across an insulating surface under the influence of DC voltage and moisture, forming dendrites that eventually create a short circuit. Silver is particularly susceptible to ion migration; tin, copper, and lead are also affected. Proper cleaning, conformal coating, and maintaining adequate conductor spacing mitigate ion-migration risk.

Impedance Coupon

A dedicated test structure on the PCB panel containing transmission lines of the same geometry and material as the controlled-impedance traces on the production boards. The fabricator measures the characteristic impedance of these coupons using a TDR (Time Domain Reflectometer) and reports the results as evidence that the impedance specification has been met.

Isotropic Etching

Etching that proceeds at the same rate in all directions — vertically and laterally. Wet chemical etching in PCB fabrication is inherently isotropic, meaning that as copper is etched vertically downward, it also etches laterally under the resist (undercut). Semi-additive processes and plasma etching enable more anisotropic (directional) etching, which is necessary for sub-25 um trace/space geometries.

Intermetallic Compound (IMC)

The layer of metal alloy — typically Cu6Sn5 or Cu3Sn for tin-based solders on copper — that forms at the interface between the solder and the base metal during soldering, representing the actual metallurgical bond. A thin, continuous IMC layer is essential for a reliable solder joint; excessive IMC growth (through prolonged heating or high-temperature ageing) consumes the base metal and embrittles the interface, leading to premature joint failure.

IP (Ingress Protection)

An international standard (IEC 60529) rating that classifies the degree of protection an enclosure provides against solid objects and liquids. PCB assemblies intended for harsh environments are often sealed in IP67 or IP68 enclosures; the conformal coating on the PCB itself contributes to moisture and particulate protection but does not directly achieve an IP rating without the enclosure.

Ionic Contamination

The presence of ionisable residues — flux activators, plating salts, handling residues, or environmental contamination — on a PCB surface that can dissolve in moisture and create a conductive electrolyte, promoting electrochemical migration and reducing SIR. Ionic contamination is measured by resistivity of solvent extract (ROSE) testing per IPC-TM-650 and is specified as a maximum equivalent NaCl contamination level, typically <1.56 ug/cm2 for high-reliability assemblies.

Inner-Layer Imaging

The photolithographic process of transferring the circuit pattern onto the dry-film resist on an inner-layer core, comprising lamination of the resist, exposure through the artwork or by LDI, development to remove unexposed resist, etching of the exposed copper, and stripping of the remaining resist. Inner-layer imaging is performed in a cleanroom with tight control of temperature, humidity, and UV exposure energy to achieve consistent feature dimensions across the panel.

I-Beam Effect

A descriptive term for the cross-sectional geometry of a PCB trace after pattern plating, where the copper builds up in a mushroom or I-beam shape — wider at the top than at the base due to plating-current distribution effects. The I-beam shape must be considered when calculating trace resistance and impedance, as the effective cross-sectional area differs from the simple width x thickness assumption of an ideal rectangular trace profile.

J

J-STD-001

The joint IPC/EIA standard that prescribes the materials, methods, and verification criteria for producing high-quality soldered electrical and electronic assemblies. J-STD-001 defines acceptance criteria for solder joints in all three IPC classes and is the foundational process standard referenced by virtually every PCB assembly contract in the industry.

Jumper Wire

A short insulated wire added to a PCB, usually on the solder side, to create or repair an electrical connection that does not exist in the etched circuit pattern. On single-sided boards without plated through-holes, jumper wires are a standard DFM technique for crossing traces; in assembled boards, jumper wires are used for rework and modification.

J-Lead

A surface-mount component lead shape that bends downward and then curls inward under the package body, forming a J-shaped profile. J-leads are used on SOJ, PLCC, and some QFP packages, and provide a more compact footprint than gull-wing leads by placing the solder joint partially under the package body — though this also makes inspection more difficult.

JTAG / Boundary Scan

See Boundary Scan. The IEEE 1149.1 standard for a test access port and boundary-scan architecture embedded in ICs, widely used for interconnect testing, device programming, and debugging. The term JTAG derives from the Joint Test Action Group that originated the standard.

Jack

The female half of a connector pair — an electrical connector that receives a plug. In PCB contexts, jacks are typically panel-mounted or PCB-mounted connectors into which a mating plug (male connector) is inserted. Common examples include barrel-style DC power jacks, RJ45 Ethernet jacks, and 3.5 mm audio jacks.

JEDEC

The Joint Electron Device Engineering Council (now JEDEC Solid State Technology Association), the semiconductor engineering standards body that publishes widely referenced standards for component packaging, moisture sensitivity classification (J-STD-020), solder-joint reliability testing (JESD22), and many other specifications directly relevant to PCB assembly and reliability engineering.

Jig

A mechanical fixture used to hold a PCB or panel in a specific position during manufacturing or assembly operations — for example, a wave-solder pallet that holds the PCB at the correct height above the solder wave while masking areas that must not be soldered. Jigs and fixtures are custom-made for each PCB design and are part of the NRE cost for assembly set-up.

K

Kelvin Connection / 4-Wire Measurement

A precision resistance-measurement technique that uses separate pairs of conductors for sourcing current and sensing voltage, eliminating the voltage drop in the test leads and contact resistance from the measurement. In PCB testing, Kelvin probing is essential for accurately measuring very low resistances — such as trace resistance in high-current circuits or the contact resistance of connectors — where lead and contact resistance would otherwise swamp the measurement. See Four-Wire Kelvin Connection.

Kapton

A brand name (DuPont) for polyimide film, the most widely used substrate material for flexible PCBs. Kapton offers excellent thermal stability (continuous operating temperature up to 400 degC), chemical resistance, and dielectric strength, and is the standard base material for flex circuits in demanding aerospace, military, and medical applications.

Known Good Board (KGB)

A PCB assembly that has been comprehensively tested and verified as fully functional and compliant with all specifications, used as a reference for calibrating test systems and as a comparison standard for troubleshooting. Similar to a Golden Board, the KGB serves as the benchmark against which production boards are measured.

KiCad

A free, open-source PCB design software suite that includes schematic capture, PCB layout with a push-and-shove router, 3D viewer, and Gerber generation. KiCad has matured into a professional-grade tool capable of designing boards with up to 32 copper layers, and its open file format and zero licensing cost make it increasingly popular in startups, academia, and the open-source hardware community.

Keyhole

A PCB hole shape consisting of a circular hole tangent to a rectangular slot, resembling a keyhole in plan view, used to accommodate a component or fastener with a specific orientation requirement. Keyholes are produced by CNC routing or by drilling overlapping holes and are defined in the mechanical layer or fabrication drawing.

Keep-Out Zone

A defined area on a PCB layer where no components, traces, vias, or copper of a specified type may be placed. Keep-out zones are used to reserve clearance around mounting holes, connectors, heat sinks, and high-voltage areas, and for creating isolation barriers between different sections of a mixed-signal board.

Key Slot

A milled slot in a PCB that engages with a corresponding key or tab in an enclosure, connector, or mating assembly to ensure correct orientation and prevent mis-insertion. Key slots are dimensioned on the mechanical layer and are produced by CNC routing operations defined in the rout file.

Kiln Drying

A forced-air drying process using a programmable oven to remove absorbed moisture from bare PCBs before assembly, particularly important for boards that have exceeded their moisture exposure limits or that use hygroscopic laminates. Typical kiln-drying parameters are 105-125 degC for 4-8 hours depending on board thickness and moisture level; inadequate drying leads to delamination and blistering during reflow.

Kitting

The logistics process of gathering all components specified in the BOM — from multiple suppliers and in the correct quantities — and organising them into a single kit for delivery to the SMT line. Kitting accuracy is critical: a missing or incorrect component halts production; kitting services are offered by distributors and by full-service PCBA providers as part of turnkey assembly.

Kerf

The width of material removed by a cutting tool — the slot cut by a router bit or the groove cut by a V-scoring blade. In PCB fabrication, the kerf width of the routing tool (typically the router-bit diameter, 1.0-2.4 mm) must be accounted for when calculating panel utilisation and when designing internal cut-outs; the rout path must be offset by half the kerf width to achieve the intended board outline.

L

Laminate

The base insulating material of a PCB, consisting of reinforcement (typically woven glass fabric) impregnated with a cured resin system (typically epoxy) with copper foil bonded to one or both surfaces. The laminate provides the mechanical, thermal, and electrical foundation for the PCB; its properties — Tg, CTE, Dk, Df, moisture absorption — directly determine the board's performance envelope.

Laser Drilling

A non-mechanical hole-forming process that uses focused UV (typically CO2 or Nd:YAG) laser energy to ablate material and create small-diameter holes — typically 50-150 um — for microvias in HDI PCBs. Laser drilling achieves much smaller hole diameters than mechanical drilling, does not suffer from drill-bit wear or breakage, and can drill blind vias that stop precisely on an internal copper pad.

Layer Count

The total number of conductive copper layers in a multilayer PCB. Layer counts are always even because PCBs are built symmetrically; typical designs range from 2 to 32 layers, with 4, 6, 8, and 12-layer boards being the most common multilayer configurations. Higher layer counts provide more routing channels, dedicated power/ground planes, and better signal integrity — at correspondingly higher cost.

Layer Stackup

The ordered arrangement of all copper layers, dielectric cores, and prepreg sheets in a multilayer PCB, specifying the material type, thickness, and copper weight for each layer. The stackup is the single most important PCB design decision: it determines impedance, crosstalk, routing density, thermal performance, and fabrication cost, and must be designed jointly by the PCB designer and fabricator.

Lead-Free

Compliance with the restriction of lead (and other hazardous substances) in electronic products, principally driven by the EU RoHS Directive (2002/95/EC, recast 2011/65/EU). Lead-free PCB assembly requires solder alloys (typically SAC305), component terminations, and surface finishes that contain no intentionally added lead, and higher soldering temperatures (peak 245-260 degC vs. 215-230 degC for SnPb).

Legend / Silkscreen

The printed layer of epoxy-ink text and symbols on a PCB outer surface that identifies component outlines, reference designators, polarity marks, pin-1 indicators, company logos, date codes, and regulatory markings. The legend is typically applied by screen printing (for low resolution) or by direct legend printing (DLP) using inkjet technology (for high resolution), and is available in white, black, yellow, and other colours.

LPI (Liquid Photo-Imageable) Solder Mask

The most widely used type of solder mask, applied as a liquid coating that is dried, exposed to UV light through a photomask, developed to remove unexposed areas, and thermally cured. LPI solder mask provides high resolution (down to ~75 um dam width), good adhesion, and chemical resistance, and is available in green, red, blue, black, white, and other colours.

LGA (Land Grid Array)

A surface-mount IC package in which the connection to the PCB is made through flat metallic lands (pads) on the underside of the package rather than through solder balls. LGA packages use solder paste printed on the PCB pads to form the connection during reflow, and are common for memory packages, power-management ICs, and some high-speed digital devices where the absence of balls reduces inductance and package height.

Line Width

The width of a copper trace on a PCB, typically specified as the width at the base of the trace (the interface with the laminate). Minimum line width — usually 75-100 um (3-4 mil) for standard PCBs, down to 25 um (1 mil) or less for advanced HDI — is a key measure of a fabricator's capability and directly affects routing density and current-carrying capacity.

Line Spacing

The minimum distance between adjacent copper features — traces, pads, or plane edges — on the same layer. Line spacing determines the maximum voltage that can be sustained without dielectric breakdown or creepage and is typically 75-100 um for standard PCBs with finer spacing down to 25 um for advanced HDI. Together with line width, spacing defines the "track/gap" or "L/S" (line/space) capability.

Lot Number

A unique alphanumeric identifier assigned to a batch of PCBs or components manufactured or processed together under the same conditions, enabling traceability from raw materials through fabrication, assembly, and field deployment. Lot traceability is a mandatory requirement for automotive (IATF 16949), medical (ISO 13485), and aerospace (AS9100) supply chains.

LDI (Laser Direct Imaging)

A photolithographic process in which the circuit pattern is written directly onto the photoresist by a computer-controlled UV laser beam, eliminating the need for a physical photomask (artwork). LDI provides superior registration accuracy compared to traditional contact/tooling-pin exposure systems and allows pattern scaling to compensate for laminate dimensional changes, enabling finer features — typically down to 25-50 um line/space — and tighter layer-to-layer registration.

Land Pattern

See Footprint. The arrangement of copper pads on a PCB designed to match the terminals of a specific component package. Land patterns follow dimensional recommendations in IPC-7351, which specifies three density levels: Level A (maximum — for wave/reflow, largest pads), Level B (median — for reflow, nominal pads), and Level C (minimum — for hand-soldered protos, smallest pads).

Laminate Void

A localised absence of resin within a laminate layer, appearing as an air or gas pocket visible in cross-section. Laminate voids are manufacturing defects caused by inadequate resin flow during lamination, trapped volatiles, or damaged prepreg; they reduce dielectric strength and can provide pathways for CAF growth. IPC-6012 defines maximum allowable void sizes.

Lead Pitch

The centre-to-centre distance between adjacent leads on an IC package. Lead pitch is the primary determinant of PCB manufacturing complexity: pitches of 0.8-1.0 mm are standard and easily manufactured; pitches of 0.5 mm require tighter process control; pitches of 0.4 mm and below (ultra-fine pitch) demand laser-cut stencils, step stencils, and advanced solder-paste inspection.

Leapfrog Testing

A dynamic electrical test strategy in which the flying-probe test program skips some test points on each pass and covers them on subsequent passes, reducing the total distance the probes must travel and thereby decreasing test cycle time. Leapfrog algorithms optimise probe movement as a travelling-salesman problem and can significantly improve throughput for high-node-count boards.

Lifted Pad

A defect in which a copper pad partially or fully separates from the laminate substrate, typically caused by excessive heat during hand soldering, mechanical stress during rework, or poor copper-to-laminate adhesion. Lifted pads are a common failure during prototyping and rework; the risk is reduced by using adequate pad-to-trace fillets, proper soldering-iron temperature control, and Z-axis-cushioned laminate materials.

Low-Flow Prepreg

Prepreg formulations with restricted resin flow characteristics, used in rigid-flex and other specialised stackups where excessive resin flow during lamination could contaminate flex areas, plug small holes, or create thickness variations. Low-flow prepregs maintain sufficient adhesion while limiting the distance the resin can spread beyond the laminate edge.

Laser Ablation

The process of removing material by irradiating it with a pulsed laser beam that vaporises or decomposes the target material. In PCB fabrication, laser ablation is used to create microvias in HDI boards, to selectively remove solder mask for fine-pitch pads, and to pattern ultra-fine traces in semi-additive processes. The ablation rate and quality depend on laser wavelength, pulse energy, and the absorption characteristics of the target material.

Layer-to-Layer Registration

The positional accuracy with which features on one layer of a multilayer PCB align with corresponding features on another layer. Proper registration is critical for annular ring integrity, controlled-impedance accuracy, and the alignment of BGA pads on outer layers with fanout vias on inner layers. Registration accuracy is verified by X-ray inspection of target patterns on each layer.

Laser Ablation Threshold

The minimum laser energy density (fluence, in J/cm2) required to remove material from a surface by ablation. In PCB microvia drilling, the laser fluence must exceed the ablation threshold of the copper or dielectric while remaining below the damage threshold of the underlying copper pad that serves as the via bottom. The ablation threshold varies by material and laser wavelength, making laser parameter optimisation specific to each material set.

Loss Tangent

See Df (Dissipation Factor). The tangent of the loss angle of a dielectric material, representing the ratio of energy dissipated as heat to energy stored per cycle of an applied alternating electric field. Loss tangent is a primary material parameter for high-frequency PCB design; lower values (tan d < 0.005) are essential for minimising signal attenuation in multi-gigabit serial links and RF transmission lines.

Laser Sintering

An additive manufacturing process that uses a laser to selectively fuse powdered material — metal, ceramic, or polymer — layer by layer to build a three-dimensional structure. In PCB prototyping, laser sintering of conductive inks or pastes can produce functional circuit traces on various substrates without etching, though the conductivity and resolution are typically inferior to conventional etched copper traces.

Lifted Component / Tombstoning

See Tombstoning. A solder defect in which a chip component lifts off one pad during reflow due to unbalanced wetting forces. Prevention strategies include symmetric pad geometries, equal copper-connection widths on both pads (to balance thermal mass), and optimised reflow profiles that minimise the temperature difference across the component during the liquidus phase.

Lead Forming

The mechanical bending of through-hole component leads to match the PCB hole spacing and to provide the correct standoff height between the component body and the board surface. Lead forming is performed with dedicated tooling for production volumes, ensuring consistent bend radii that do not damage the lead-to-body seal or create stress concentrations; for prototyping, leads are formed with needle-nose pliers and measured against a lead-forming template.

M

Microvia

A small via — typically 0.15 mm diameter or smaller — formed by laser drilling rather than mechanical drilling, connecting two adjacent layers of an HDI PCB. Microvias have a 1:1 maximum aspect ratio (depth/diameter) and are the fundamental building block of HDI technology; stacked microvias provide layer-to-layer transitions in build-up constructions, while staggered microvias reduce process complexity at the cost of routing real estate.

Microsection

A carefully prepared cross-sectional sample of a PCB mounted in epoxy, ground, and polished to reveal the internal structure for microscopic examination. Microsectioning is the gold-standard quality-assurance technique for evaluating copper plating thickness in holes, intermetallic compound formation at solder joints, inner-layer registration, laminate integrity, and any of dozens of internal features invisible from the surface.

Microstrip

A controlled-impedance transmission-line configuration in which the signal trace runs on an outer layer with a single reference plane (ground or power) on the next inner layer below, separated by a dielectric. Microstrip traces have the signal partially in air (solder mask) and partially in the laminate, producing an effective dielectric constant between that of the two media and typically requiring wider traces than stripline for the same target impedance.

Multilayer PCB

A PCB with three or more conductive copper layers, consisting of etched cores and prepreg sheets laminated together under heat and pressure to form a single monolithic board. Multilayer construction enables dedicated power and ground planes (improving signal integrity and PDN performance) and dramatically increases routing density compared to single- or double-sided boards, at the cost of higher fabrication complexity and lead time.

Solder Mask

The thin polymer coating applied to the outer surfaces of a PCB over the copper traces and planes, leaving only the pads and other features to be soldered exposed. Solder mask prevents solder bridges between adjacent pads during assembly, protects the copper traces from oxidation and mechanical damage, and provides the board's characteristic colour — most commonly green, but also available in red, blue, black, white, yellow, and purple. See LPI.

Metal Core PCB (MCPCB)

A PCB construction in which the copper circuit layer is bonded to a metal base plate — typically aluminium for cost-performance balance or copper for maximum thermal conductivity — with a thin, thermally conductive dielectric layer between them. MCPCBs provide 5-30x the through-plane thermal conductivity of FR-4 and are the standard substrate for high-power LED lighting, automotive power modules, and motor-drive circuits. See IMS.

Megtron 6

A high-performance laminate material from Panasonic with a low dielectric constant (Dk ~3.6) and very low dissipation factor (Df ~0.002 at 1 GHz), designed for high-speed digital applications up to 25 Gbps and beyond. Megtron 6 is widely specified in advanced networking, data-centre, and telecommunications equipment as an alternative to more expensive PTFE-based RF laminates, and is processable using standard FR-4 fabrication techniques.

Mil-Spec (Military Specification)

A performance and testing standard published by the U.S. Department of Defense, applicable to PCBs and assemblies destined for military, defence, and aerospace applications. Key PCB-related Mil-Specs include MIL-PRF-31032 (printed circuit board performance specification) and MIL-PRF-55110 (general specification for rigid PCBs). Mil-Spec qualification represents the highest tier of PCB manufacturing rigour and is required for many defence contracts.

Minimum Conductor Width

The narrowest copper trace width that a PCB fabricator can reliably produce at high yield. Minimum conductor width, together with minimum spacing, defines a fabricator's "technology class" and is typically 75-100 um (3-4 mil) for mainstream suppliers, 50 um (2 mil) for advanced fabricators, and 25 um (1 mil) or less for HDI-specialist facilities.

Minimum Hole Size

The smallest-diameter hole a PCB fabricator can reliably drill, plate, and finish. Minimum mechanical drill size is typically 0.15-0.20 mm; smaller holes can be produced by laser drilling. The minimum hole size directly constrains via density, BGA escape routing (since vias must fit between BGA pads), and component selection (through-hole parts require specific hole diameters).

Motherboard

The main PCB in a computer, server, or complex electronic system that hosts the central processor, memory, chipset, and expansion connectors, and interconnects all sub-systems through printed-circuit traces. Motherboards are among the most complex PCBs manufactured — modern server motherboards may contain 20-30 layers, multiple HDI build-up regions, hundreds of BGAs, and thousands of controlled-impedance differential pairs.

Mouse Bite

A row of small, closely-spaced holes along a breakaway tab that perforates the tab so it can be cleanly broken after assembly to separate individual PCBs from the panel. The name derives from the resemblance of the remaining edge — a series of small bumps and indentations — to the pattern of a mouse bite. Mouse bites reduce the force required for depanelisation but leave a rough edge that must be accounted for in the board outline tolerance.

Mechanical Drill

A solid carbide drill bit rotated at high speed (typically 80,000-200,000 RPM) to create holes in a PCB panel by physically cutting through the laminate and copper. Mechanical drilling is limited to hole diameters above approximately 0.15 mm due to the fragility of smaller bits and the precision of the spindle/chuck system; smaller features require laser drilling.

Metalization (Metallisation)

The collective processes that deposit copper onto the hole walls of a PCB to create the plated through-hole barrel — starting with electroless copper deposition (providing the initial conductive layer), followed by electrolytic copper plating (building the barrel to full thickness). The term also encompasses the chemical preparation steps — desmear, microetch, catalyst — that precede copper deposition.

Mask Expansion

The intentional enlargement of the solder-mask opening relative to the copper pad it clears, specified as a radial offset. Positive mask expansion (opening larger than the pad) ensures that the entire pad is exposed for soldering even with mask registration tolerances; negative mask expansion (opening smaller than the pad, also called mask-defined pad) uses the mask opening to precisely define the solderable area and is sometimes used for fine-pitch BGAs to prevent bridging.

MSL (Moisture Sensitivity Level)

A JEDEC-standard classification (J-STD-020) that rates the susceptibility of non-hermetic SMD packages to moisture-induced damage during reflow soldering. MSL ratings range from 1 (unlimited floor life at <=30 degC/85% RH) to 6 (mandatory bake before use). Components with higher MSL ratings must be stored in moisture-barrier bags with desiccant and must be assembled within a specified exposure window after opening or else baked to remove absorbed moisture and prevent popcorning.

Mask Dam

The narrow strip of solder mask between two adjacent pads that prevents solder bridging during reflow. The minimum mask dam width — typically 75-100 um for green LPI solder mask — is a key DFM constraint; if the pad-to-pad gap is too small to allow an adequate dam, the solder-mask opening may be merged into a single window covering both pads, increasing the risk of bridging.

Manual Soldering

Hand soldering using a temperature-controlled soldering iron, performed by a skilled operator for prototyping, rework, and low-volume production of through-hole and simple SMD components. Manual soldering quality depends on iron temperature, tip geometry, dwell time, and operator technique; IPC-J-STD-001 defines the acceptability criteria.

Mixed Assembly

A PCB assembly that contains both surface-mount and through-hole components on the same board, requiring a combination of SMT processes (solder paste printing, pick-and-place, reflow) and through-hole processes (insertion, wave soldering, or selective soldering). Mixed-assembly boards are common when legacy through-hole connectors, transformers, or high-power components cannot be sourced in SMD format.

Metallurgical Bond

The atomic-level connection formed when molten solder wets a metal surface and dissolves a small amount of the base metal, creating an intermetallic compound (IMC) layer at the interface. A proper metallurgical bond is essential for reliable solder-joint mechanical strength and electrical conductivity; a cold joint or disturbed joint lacks this bond and will fail under thermal cycling or mechanical load.

Mask Relief

The clearance between the edge of a solder-mask opening and the edge of the copper pad it exposes (see Mask Expansion). Proper mask relief ensures that the entire pad is available for soldering even with mask registration tolerances, while adequate mask dam width between adjacent openings prevents solder bridging.

Marking Permanency

The ability of legend ink, labels, or laser-etched markings on a PCB to remain legible after exposure to soldering temperatures, cleaning chemicals, and the expected service environment. Marking permanency is verified by rub testing, solvent exposure, and thermal cycling, and is particularly important for serial numbers, date codes, and regulatory marks that must remain readable throughout the product's service life.

Mesh (Stencil Mesh)

In the context of stencil design, a mesh or grid pattern of multiple small apertures used instead of a single large opening for paste deposition on large thermal pads. The mesh reduces the total paste volume, provides escape channels for volatiles during reflow (reducing voiding), and prevents the squeegee from dipping into a single large aperture — all critical for achieving consistent, low-void solder joints on QFN and similar packages.

Multi-Level HDI

An HDI PCB construction containing microvias at two or more levels — for example, a 2+N+2 build-up with two sequential lamination steps on each side of the core, each adding one microvia level and one copper layer. Multi-level HDI enables extreme routing densities for advanced smartphones, high-end FPGAs, and complex wearable devices, but at significant cost premium due to the additional lamination, drilling, and plating cycles required.

Micro-Etch

A light chemical etch — typically using a persulphate or peroxide-sulphuric acid solution — that removes a very thin layer of copper (1-3 um) to create a micro-roughened surface texture. Micro-etching is performed before dry-film lamination (to improve resist adhesion), before oxide treatment (to prepare the copper surface), and before solder-mask application (to ensure mask adhesion to the copper traces).

N

Netlist

A structured list of all electrical connections (nets) in a PCB design, specifying which component pins are connected to which other pins. The netlist is the bridge between the schematic and the PCB layout — the layout tool uses it to verify that the routed copper traces correctly implement all intended connections. In bare-board electrical testing, the netlist extracted from the Gerber data (the "reference netlist") is compared against the physical board's connectivity to detect opens and shorts.

NPTH (Non-Plated Through-Hole)

A hole drilled in a PCB that is not plated with copper on its interior walls. NPTHs are used for mechanical mounting (screw holes, standoff holes), for tooling/fixturing alignment, and for connector shells that do not require electrical connection. The drill file distinguishes NPTHs from PTHs (plated through-holes), and the fabricator masks NPTHs during the electroplating process or drills them after plating.

Non-Wetting

A soldering defect in which the molten solder does not adhere to the pad or component termination surface, leaving the surface partially or fully exposed. Non-wetting is caused by surface contamination, oxidised pads, insufficient flux activity, or a deteriorated surface finish, and produces a solder joint with poor mechanical and electrical integrity. IPC-A-610 classifies non-wetting as a defect for all three performance classes.

NEMA Grade

A classification system established by the National Electrical Manufacturers Association for laminate materials, designating them by code — FR-4 (Flame Retardant, Grade 4), G-10 (glass/epoxy without flame retardant), FR-2 (paper/phenolic, flame retardant), CEM-1, CEM-3, etc. NEMA grades define the reinforcement type, resin system, and flame-retardant properties, providing a common shorthand for material specification.

Nickel Barrier

A layer of nickel (typically 3-7 um) plated between copper and gold in PCB surface finishes to prevent copper diffusion into the gold and to prevent gold-copper intermetallic formation that would embrittle the solder joint. The nickel barrier is a critical reliability feature of ENIG, ENEPIG, and hard-gold finishes; without it, copper migrates rapidly through gold at soldering temperatures.

Node

In circuit theory and PCB design, a node is any point in a circuit where two or more components connect — every net is a node, and every pin connected to that net is on the same node. In netlist terms, a node (or net) has a unique name and a list of all connected component pins; electrical testing verifies that every pin on a given node has continuity to every other pin on that node and isolation from all other nodes.

NRE (Non-Recurring Engineering)

One-time costs associated with setting up a new PCB or assembly project — tooling for test fixtures, stencil fabrication, programming of pick-and-place machines, CAM engineering, and first-article preparation. NRE charges are quoted separately from per-unit production costs and are amortised over the production volume; they can range from negligible (for simple 2-layer boards with standard specifications) to substantial (for complex HDI with custom stackups and multiple test fixtures).

Negative Etching

An inner-layer fabrication approach in which the photoresist defines the spaces between conductors (rather than the conductors themselves), and the etching step removes all copper not protected by resist. In negative-etching, the copper remaining after etching forms the ground plane or power plane with the anti-pads, thermal reliefs, and clearance areas already "cut out." This contrasts with positive etching, where the resist protects the desired traces and pads.

Nail Heading

A drilling defect in which the copper foil at the entry side of a drilled hole deforms into a nail-head shape — wider than the hole diameter — due to a dull drill bit or excessive feed rate. Nail-heading creates an uneven surface that can compromise solder-mask coverage and pad definition, and is a rejectable condition per IPC-6012 when it exceeds specified limits.

Nominal

The specified design value for a dimension or parameter — the target value around which tolerances are defined. For example, a trace may have a nominal width of 150 um with a tolerance of +/-25 um, meaning the acceptable range is 125-175 um. The nominal value is the design intent; tolerances define the permissible manufacturing variation.

Neutral Bake

A controlled heating process performed on bare PCBs before assembly to remove absorbed moisture, conducted at a temperature (typically 105-125 degC) and duration (4-24 hours depending on thickness) that is high enough to drive out water but low enough to avoid excessive oxidation of the surface finish. Neutral bake is required when PCBs have exceeded their moisture exposure limit, particularly for boards stored in uncontrolled environments or scheduled for multiple reflow cycles.

No-Clean Flux

A flux formulation designed to leave minimal, non-corrosive, non-conductive residue after soldering, eliminating the need for post-solder cleaning. No-clean fluxes dominate modern SMT assembly because they remove a process step (cleaning), but their residues can interfere with ICT probe contact, conformal-coating adhesion, and high-frequency circuit performance; in these cases, the "no-clean" label notwithstanding, cleaning may still be required.

O

OSP (Organic Solderability Preservative)

See ENTEK/OSP. A thin, transparent organic coating — typically a benzimidazole or benzotriazole compound — chemically bonded to the copper surface to prevent oxidation and preserve solderability during storage between fabrication and assembly. During soldering, the OSP is displaced by the flux and dissolves into the solder, leaving a clean copper surface for wetting. OSP is the lowest-cost surface finish and provides excellent planarity for fine-pitch SMT, but has limited shelf life and typically only survives 1-2 reflow cycles.

Open Circuit

An unintended break in the electrical continuity of a net — a trace that does not connect, a plated hole that is cracked or unplated, or a solder joint that has failed to form — resulting in an electrical discontinuity where continuity is required. Opens are detected during bare-board electrical test and are one of the two fundamental PCB defect categories (along with short circuits).

Outer Layer

The top (component-side) or bottom (solder-side) copper layer of a PCB — the layers visible on the surface of the finished board, covered by solder mask except where pads and other features are exposed. Outer layers carry the final surface finish (ENIG, HASL, OSP, etc.), receive component placement and soldering, and are subject to more stringent cosmetic and dimensional requirements than inner layers.

Over-etch

Excessive copper removal during the etching process that reduces trace width below the design target, potentially compromising current-carrying capacity and controlled-impedance accuracy. Over-etch results from excessively long dwell time in the etchant, overly aggressive etchant chemistry, or inadequate resist protection, and is monitored by measuring trace width on test coupons.

Oxidation

The chemical reaction of a metal — particularly copper — with oxygen to form an oxide layer. Copper oxide is non-solderable, so bare copper surfaces must be protected by surface finishes (ENIG, HASL, OSP, immersion silver/tin); flux is designed to remove light oxidation during soldering. Black oxide and brown oxide are intentionally grown oxide layers used to enhance prepreg adhesion to inner-layer copper.

ODB++

An intelligent, hierarchical data format for transferring PCB design data to fabrication, assembly, and test. Unlike the flat Gerber format, ODB++ includes netlist information, component placement data, material stackup, and BOM data in a single structured database, reducing the risk of data misalignment between the various files of a traditional Gerber-based data package. ODB++ is a proprietary format (Mentor Graphics/Siemens) but is widely supported by CAM systems.

Outgassing

The release of trapped gas or volatile compounds from a material when heated, of particular concern in vacuum or space applications where released gases can condense on optical surfaces or contaminate sensitive instruments. PCB materials for space applications are tested for outgassing per ASTM E595 to ensure total mass loss (TML) and collected volatile condensable material (CVCM) are within acceptable limits.

Overage

Extra PCBs produced beyond the ordered quantity to compensate for yield loss during fabrication and assembly, typically 2-10% depending on complexity. Overage ensures the customer receives at least the ordered quantity even if some boards are lost to handling damage, test failures, or process defects; it is a standard provision in PCB procurement contracts.

Ohm

The SI unit of electrical resistance (symbol: capital omega), defined as the resistance between two points of a conductor when a constant potential difference of 1 volt, applied between those points, produces a current of 1 ampere. In PCB design, resistance values are calculated for traces (to determine voltage drop), for plane shapes (for PDN analysis), and for insulation between nets (to verify adequate isolation).

One-Sided PCB (Single-Sided PCB)

A PCB with copper circuitry on only one side of the insulating substrate, with no plated through-holes. Single-sided boards are the simplest and lowest-cost PCB construction, used in high-volume consumer products like LED lighting, power supplies, and simple control boards where all components can be mounted on one side and routing density is modest.

Oval Pad

A pad with an elongated (oval or oblong) shape, used for through-hole components where the pad elongation provides extra solder fillet area in the direction of wave-solder travel, or where it accommodates a degree of positional tolerance in lead forming. Oval pads are also used in BGA land patterns for the outer rows where elongation towards the package exterior aids inspection.

Overhang

In PCB etching, the projection of the resist or tin-lead plating layer beyond the edge of the underlying copper trace, caused by lateral etching (undercut) of the copper beneath the protective layer. Excessive overhang can break off and create conductive debris that causes shorts; it is controlled by optimising the etch process for a high etch factor.

Open (in ICT)

In the context of in-circuit testing, an "open" is a fault condition where a component terminal has no electrical connection to its expected net — typically caused by a missing solder joint, a cracked trace, or a bent component lead that did not enter the PCB hole. ICT detects opens by measuring the resistance between the component pin and every other node on the same net through the bed-of-nails fixture.

OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer)

In the PCB supply chain, the OEM is the company that designs the electronic product and contracts the PCB fabrication and assembly to specialist manufacturers. The OEM provides the design data (Gerber files, BOM, assembly drawings), specifies the quality requirements and acceptance criteria, and bears the ultimate responsibility for the product's performance and compliance in the market.

Organic Acid (OA) Flux

A water-soluble flux based on organic acids (such as citric, adipic, or glutamic acid) that provides strong oxide-removal activity for difficult-to-solder surfaces. OA fluxes leave corrosive residues that must be completely removed by water washing immediately after soldering; they are used when maximum soldering performance is required and when the assembly design permits thorough cleaning and drying.

P

PCB (Printed Circuit Board)

A laminated structure consisting of alternating layers of insulating substrate (typically FR-4 fibreglass/epoxy) and conductive copper traces that mechanically support and electrically connect electronic components. PCBs are the foundational interconnect technology for virtually all electronic products, with the global industry producing hundreds of millions of square metres annually across technologies from simple single-sided boards to complex 30-layer HDI with embedded passives.

PCBA (Printed Circuit Board Assembly)

A PCB that has been populated with electronic components through soldering processes — SMT reflow, wave soldering, selective soldering, or manual soldering — to create a functional electronic circuit. The term distinguishes the assembled, component-populated board from the bare (unpopulated) PCB.

Pad

The exposed copper area on a PCB — defined by an opening in the solder mask — to which a component terminal is soldered. Pads come in two fundamental types: surface-mount pads (for SMD components, soldered directly to the pad on the same layer) and through-hole pads (annular rings around a plated hole into which a component lead is inserted and soldered). Pad design — size, shape, and relationship to the solder-mask opening — directly affects assembly yield and solder-joint reliability.

Panel

The large-format laminate sheet (typically 457 x 610 mm or 533 x 610 mm) from which multiple PCBs are fabricated in a single batch. Individual PCB designs are stepped and repeated ("nested") into the panel area with border rails for handling, tooling holes for alignment, and test coupons for quality verification. Panel utilisation — the percentage of the panel area occupied by actual PCBs — is a key cost driver.

Panelization (Panelisation)

The process of arranging multiple copies of a PCB design (or multiple different designs) onto a single fabrication or assembly panel together with tooling holes, fiducials, and breakaway tabs or V-score lines. Proper panelisation maximises material utilisation, enables efficient SMT assembly, and controls board-handling throughout the production process.

Paste Stencil

A thin metal sheet — typically stainless steel, 100-150 um thick — with laser-cut or electroformed apertures corresponding to every SMD pad on a PCB, through which solder paste is printed onto the pads before component placement. Stencil design — aperture size relative to pad size, aperture shape, wall smoothness, and stencil thickness — is the single most critical factor in achieving consistent, high-yield solder-paste deposition.

Pattern Plating

An outer-layer fabrication process in which the dry-film resist is imaged with the reverse of the desired circuit pattern (plating resist on areas that will become clearances), and copper is electroplated onto the exposed traces and pads, followed by tin or tin-lead plating as an etch resist. After resist stripping, the background copper is etched away, leaving the plated-up circuit pattern. Pattern plating produces superior trace definition compared to panel plating (in which the entire panel is plated before imaging).

Peel Strength

A measure of the adhesive bond strength between copper foil and the laminate substrate, quantified as the force (in N/mm or lb/in) required to peel a standard-width strip of copper from the laminate at a specified angle. Minimum peel strength is typically 1.05 N/mm (6 lb/in) for 1 oz copper on FR-4; inadequate peel strength can lead to lifted pads and traces during soldering and rework.

PTH (Pin-Through-Hole / Plated Through-Hole)

A hole drilled through the entire PCB thickness, with copper electroplated onto its interior walls to create an electrical connection between the layers intersected by the hole. PTHs serve as component-lead mounting holes and as vias for inter-layer connections. The term also refers to through-hole components (as opposed to SMD), and the soldering process for those components.

Pitch

The centre-to-centre distance between adjacent conductors, pads, or component leads. In IC packages, pitch typically refers to the lead-to-lead spacing; pitches of 0.8-1.27 mm are standard, 0.5 mm is fine-pitch, and 0.3-0.4 mm is ultra-fine pitch. In PCB routing, pitch refers to the trace centre-to-centre spacing, determined by the line-width plus line-spacing specifications.

Plasma Etching

A dry etching process that uses a reactive plasma (ionised gas, typically oxygen, CF4, or argon) to remove material — used in PCB fabrication for desmear of drilled holes (removing epoxy residue), for surface activation before electroless copper deposition, and for removing thin organic layers such as residual dry-film resist. Plasma processes are anisotropic and produce less undercut than wet chemical etching.

Plated Through-Hole

See PTH. The copper-lined interior of a drilled hole that provides an electrical connection between layers. The plated barrel must have a minimum copper thickness (typically 20-25 um for Class 2, 25 um for Class 3) and must cover the full hole circumference from pad to pad without voids, cracks, or discontinuities.

Polyimide

A high-temperature polymer used as the base substrate for flexible PCBs (see Kapton) and as a specialised laminate material for high-temperature rigid PCBs. Polyimide laminates offer a very high Tg (>250 degC), excellent thermal stability, and good chemical resistance. They are significantly more expensive than FR-4 and are specified for applications involving sustained elevated temperatures, such as downhole drilling instrumentation and aerospace engine-control modules.

PPM (Parts Per Million)

A dimensionless unit expressing the number of defective units per million units produced, used as a process-quality metric in PCB manufacturing. A fabricator reporting 50 PPM for a particular defect type means that 50 out of every 1,000,000 opportunities exhibited that defect. World-class PCB manufacturing operates at single-digit to low-double-digit PPM levels for critical defects.

Prepreg (Pre-impregnated Bonding Sheet)

A sheet of glass fabric pre-impregnated with partially cured (B-stage) epoxy resin, used as the bonding and insulating layer between cores in multilayer PCB construction. During lamination, heat and pressure cause the prepreg resin to melt, flow, and fully cure, bonding adjacent cores together into a single monolithic structure. Prepreg thickness, resin content, and flow characteristics are selected to achieve the target layer-to-layer dielectric thickness for impedance control.

Press-Fit

A solderless interconnection technology in which a compliant (eye-of-needle) pin is pressed into a plated through-hole, creating a gas-tight electrical connection through elastic deformation of the pin within the hole. Press-fit connectors and terminals eliminate the thermal stress of soldering, can be reworked, and are widely used in backplane, automotive, and high-reliability applications.

Probe (Test Probe)

A spring-loaded electrical contact (pogo pin) used in flying-probe testers and bed-of-nails test fixtures to make temporary electrical contact with test points on a PCB. Test probes come in various tip styles (crown, spear, flat, serrated) and spring forces appropriate for different pad surfaces and test requirements; crown-tip probes are preferred for penetrating flux residues and oxides to achieve reliable contact.

Reflow Profile

The time-temperature curve that a PCB assembly follows as it passes through a reflow oven, characterised by ramp rate, soak time and temperature, time above liquidus (TAL), and peak temperature. The profile must deliver sufficient heat to fully melt the solder, activate the flux, and form reliable intermetallic bonds without exceeding the thermal limits of any component. Reflow profiling is product-specific and is verified with a thermocouple-instrumented board.

Pad Lifting

See Lifted Pad. A defect in which the copper pad partially or fully detaches from the laminate substrate, usually caused by excessive heat, mechanical stress, or poor copper adhesion. Pad lifting during rework or hand soldering is a common cause of PCB scrap.

Panel Routing

The CNC routing operation that cuts individual PCBs free from the surrounding panel frame, following the board outline defined in the rout file. Panel routing uses high-speed carbide router bits (typically 1.0-2.4 mm diameter) and may leave breakaway tabs at programmed intervals to keep the PCBs attached to the panel frame until after assembly.

Photoplotter

A precision machine that writes the photolithographic artwork onto photographic film or directly onto photoresist by steering a focused light beam across the image area under computer control. Modern photoplotters use laser light sources and can produce features as small as 15-25 um; they have been largely replaced by laser direct imaging (LDI) systems that write directly onto the PCB panel, eliminating the film intermediate step.

Photo Resist

A light-sensitive polymer material — either a dry film laminated onto the copper surface or a liquid coating applied by roller or spray — that polymerises (cross-links) when exposed to UV light and becomes resistant to the developing and etching chemistries. Photoresist is the enabling material for transferring the circuit image from the artwork or LDI data onto the copper layer.

Peelable Mask

A temporary solder mask applied by screen printing to areas that must be protected during assembly — typically gold edge-connector fingers, test points, or switch contacts — and peeled off by hand after soldering is complete. Peelable mask prevents solder from wetting the protected areas during wave soldering and is an alternative to taping for protecting gold fingers.

Plating Void

A localised absence or thin spot in the copper plating of a through-hole barrel, appearing as a gap in the copper when examined in cross-section. Plating voids reduce the current-carrying capacity of the hole, concentrate thermal-cycle stress, and can crack open during thermal cycling. IPC-6012 defines void limits by size and position; voids in the knee of the hole (where the barrel meets the pad) are particularly critical.

Pick-and-Place

The automated SMT assembly process in which a high-speed machine picks components from feeders (tape-and-reel, tray, or tube) using vacuum nozzles and places them at their programmed X-Y coordinates and rotation angles on the solder-paste-printed PCB. Modern pick-and-place machines achieve placement rates of 40,000-100,000 components per hour with placement accuracy of +/-25-50 um, and use vision systems to centre each component before placement.

PDN (Power Distribution Network)

The complete electrical network — comprising voltage regulator modules, power and ground planes, decoupling capacitors, and the PCB traces and vias connecting them — that delivers power from the source to every active device on the board. A well-designed PDN maintains a stable supply voltage across all operating frequencies by presenting a low impedance from DC to beyond the maximum clock frequency; PDN analysis is an essential step in high-speed digital PCB design.

Plot File

A historical term for Gerber photoplot data (still used colloquially), referring to the vector data that drove a photoplotter to expose film. In modern usage, "Gerber plot" or simply "plots" is synonymous with Gerber files — the layer-by-layer image data used to fabricate a PCB.

Pin-Through-Hole (PTH) Component

An electronic component with wire leads designed to be inserted through holes in the PCB and soldered to annular pads, as distinguished from surface-mount devices. PTH components include DIP ICs, axial and radial leaded passives, connectors, transformers, and high-power semiconductors, and require wave soldering, selective soldering, or manual soldering rather than reflow.

Preheating

The initial stage of a reflow or wave-soldering thermal profile in which the PCB assembly is gradually heated to a soak temperature (typically 150-180 degC for lead-free processes) to activate flux, evaporate solvents from the solder paste, and minimise thermal shock to components. Proper preheating is critical for preventing solder-ball formation, component cracking, and board warpage during subsequent exposure to peak soldering temperatures.

Post-Reflow Inspection

The visual and automated inspection of PCB assemblies immediately after reflow soldering to detect assembly defects — solder bridges, insufficient solder, tombstoned components, misaligned parts, and solder balls — while the board is still in process and can be economically reworked. AOI systems perform the bulk of post-reflow inspection, supplemented by human visual inspection for hard-to-automate defect categories.

Panel Plating

An outer-layer fabrication process in which the entire panel is electroplated with copper to the full required thickness before the circuit pattern is imaged, after which the unwanted background copper is etched away. Panel plating is simpler than pattern plating but results in greater undercut during etching (because more copper must be removed) and is less suitable for fine-line designs where tight trace-width control is critical.

Q

QFP (Quad Flat Package)

A surface-mount IC package with gull-wing leads extending from all four sides of a flat rectangular or square body. QFP packages range from 32 to over 300 pins with pitches from 1.0 mm down to 0.3 mm (ultra-fine-pitch QFP). The leads provide compliance that absorbs CTE mismatch stress, and the exposed lead geometry allows visual inspection of every solder joint — advantages that have sustained QFP usage despite the higher density offered by BGA packages.

QFN (Quad Flat No-Lead)

A surface-mount IC package with peripheral metal pads on the underside of the package body rather than protruding leads, providing a near-chip-scale footprint with low inductance and excellent thermal performance via the exposed thermal pad on the package underside. QFN packages are challenging for assembly because the solder joints under the package are not visually inspectable, and the large centre thermal pad requires carefully designed stencil apertures and via patterns for proper solder-joint and thermal-interface formation.

QTA (Quick Turn Assembly)

An accelerated PCB assembly service in which a supplier commits to assembling a specified quantity of boards in a compressed timeframe — typically 24-72 hours from receipt of materials — using express processes and prioritised scheduling. QTA services are essential for prototyping, urgent engineering builds, and time-critical manufacturing ramp-up, and typically command a premium over standard lead times.

Quality Plan

A documented, project-specific plan defining the quality requirements, inspection criteria, test methods, sampling plans, and acceptance standards that apply to a PCB or PCBA production order. The quality plan references the applicable IPC class, identifies critical-to-quality features, specifies any customer-specific requirements beyond IPC standards, and defines how non-conformances will be dispositioned.

Quad Flat Package

See QFP. Also used more broadly for any flat-pack IC with leads on four sides, including QFN (no-lead), QFP (gull-wing), and LQFP/TQFP (low-profile and thin variants).

QTA vs Prototype

Quick Turn Assembly and prototyping are related but distinct services: QTA emphasises speed (typically 24-72 hours from kit receipt to shipped assemblies) and may use production tooling and processes; prototyping emphasises flexibility (accommodating design changes, partial BOMs, hand-placement of critical parts) and may use simplified processes that differ from the eventual production line. Some suppliers offer both services on a single production line with graded speed options.

R

RoHS (Restriction of Hazardous Substances)

The European Union directive (2011/65/EU, RoHS 2, and 2015/863, RoHS 3) that restricts the use of lead, mercury, cadmium, hexavalent chromium, PBBs, PBDEs, and four phthalates (DEHP, BBP, DBP, DIBP) in electrical and electronic equipment. For PCBs and assemblies, RoHS compliance means lead-free solders and surface finishes, halogen-free laminates where applicable, and documented material declarations for every substance in the finished product.

Registration

The positional alignment accuracy between layers of a multilayer PCB — between the drill pattern and the pads, between outer-layer features and inner-layer features, and between the solder-mask openings and the copper pads they cover. Registration is maintained through tooling holes and fiducials throughout the fabrication process; misregistration causes annular ring loss, solder-mask-on-pad defects, and impedance variability.

Reflow Soldering

The predominant SMT soldering process in which solder paste (a suspension of solder-powder spheres in flux) is printed onto PCB pads, components are placed onto the paste, and the entire assembly is heated through a controlled temperature profile in a reflow oven, causing the solder powder to melt, wet the surfaces, and form metallurgical bonds. Reflow soldering produces all the SMD solder joints in a single thermal cycle and is capable of soldering components with pitches down to 0.3 mm.

Rigid PCB

A standard, non-flexible printed circuit board fabricated on a rigid laminate substrate such as FR-4. Rigid PCBs constitute the vast majority of all PCBs produced and provide the mechanical structure, electrical interconnection, and thermal management for components in everything from consumer electronics to industrial controls to aerospace systems.

Rigid-Flex PCB

See Flex-Rigid PCB. A hybrid construction combining rigid FR-4 sections for component mounting with flexible polyimide sections for interconnection, fabricated as a single monolithic board. Rigid-flex is increasingly adopted in aerospace, military, medical implant, and high-end consumer applications where space, weight, and reliability considerations justify the higher fabrication cost.

Rogers Material

A family of high-performance laminate materials from Rogers Corporation, based on PTFE (Teflon), ceramic-filled PTFE, or hydrocarbon-ceramic composites, engineered for RF, microwave, and high-speed digital applications. Rogers materials — including RO4000, RO3000, RT/duroid, and RO4835 — offer very low dielectric loss (Df 0.001-0.004) and tightly controlled Dk (~2.2-10.2) and are the standard choice for antenna, radar, satellite, and 5G base-station PCBs.

Routing / Profile Routing

The CNC machining operation that cuts the finished board outline from the panel using a high-speed carbide router bit. Routing is the final mechanical operation in PCB fabrication and produces the board edges, internal cut-outs, slots, and any non-circular features specified in the mechanical layer or rout file.

Return Loss

A measure of signal reflection at an impedance discontinuity, expressed in dB as the ratio of reflected power to incident power. High return loss (more negative dB) means less reflection and better impedance matching. In PCB design, return loss is degraded by via stubs, connector transitions, trace-width changes, and poorly matched terminations, and is a critical parameter for RF and high-speed serial links.

RF PCB (Radio Frequency PCB)

A PCB designed to carry signals at radio frequencies (typically 100 MHz and above, extending into the mmWave range at 30-100+ GHz), requiring specialist low-loss laminate materials (Rogers, Taconic, Isola Astra), precise controlled-impedance structures, and careful layout to minimise parasitic effects, radiation, and coupling. RF PCBs are foundational to wireless communications, radar, satellite systems, and 5G infrastructure.

Reflow Oven

A conveyorised heating system — typically using convection (hot gas), infrared (IR), or vapour phase (condensation) heating — with multiple temperature zones through which PCB assemblies pass to execute the reflow soldering profile. Modern reflow ovens have 8-12 independently controlled zones and may use nitrogen atmosphere to reduce oxidation and improve wetting, especially for lead-free solders.

Resist

A protective coating — either dry-film photopolymer or liquid photo-imageable material — that shields selected areas of copper from etching or plating chemistries. In PCB fabrication, both etch resists (protecting circuit traces during etching) and plating resists (exposing selected areas for electroplating) are used. Solder mask is also a type of resist applied to the finished board.

Resin

The organic polymer component of a PCB laminate — typically epoxy in FR-4, polyimide in flex circuits, or PTFE in RF laminates — that encapsulates the glass reinforcement and bonds the layers together. The resin system determines the laminate's Tg, CTE, dielectric properties, moisture absorption, and chemical resistance, and is the primary determinant of material cost and processability.

RCC (Resin-Coated Copper)

A thin copper foil coated with a layer of partially cured resin, used as an alternative to conventional prepreg plus copper foil in HDI build-up constructions. RCC provides finer dielectric-thickness control than prepreg and is used in high-layer-count HDI and IC-substrate fabrication where very thin dielectrics are required.

Rework

The process of removing and replacing a defective component on an assembled PCB, using specialist rework equipment — hot-air nozzles, IR heaters, or soldering irons — to heat the solder joints, remove the component, clean the pads, and solder a replacement. Rework is a skilled manual operation that risks lifting pads, damaging adjacent components, or thermally stressing the board, and is subject to the same acceptance criteria as production soldering per IPC-7711/7721.

Registration Mark

A dedicated target — typically a crosshair, circle, or other simple pattern on each layer — used by the fabricator to align artwork and verify layer-to-layer registration. Registration marks are placed outside the board area, usually in the panel rails, and are read by automated optical alignment systems on exposure, lamination, and drilling equipment.

Radial Lead

A through-hole component lead configuration in which two or more wire leads emerge from the same face (typically the bottom) of a cylindrical or rectangular component body, as seen in many capacitors, inductors, and power transistors. Radial-leaded components stand upright on the PCB and consume less board area than axial-leaded components with the same body size, but require the leads to be formed (bent) to match the hole spacing.

Resin Smear

See Epoxy Smear. A thin layer of resin mechanically smeared across inner-layer copper surfaces during drilling, which must be chemically removed (desmeared) to allow the plated copper to bond directly to the inner-layer copper pad.

Reverse Engineering

The process of reconstructing design data — schematic, netlist, and PCB layout — from a physical PCB by measuring and analysing the board, typically through a combination of X-ray imaging, layer-by-layer deconstruction, and electrical probing. Reverse engineering of PCBs may be performed for obsolescence management, competitive analysis (subject to legal restrictions), or forensic failure analysis, and requires specialist equipment and skills.

Reliability Testing

A suite of accelerated-stress tests designed to evaluate the long-term durability of a PCB or assembly under conditions more severe than the expected service environment. Key reliability tests include thermal cycling (e.g., -40 to +125 degC, 500-1000 cycles), thermal shock, highly accelerated life testing (HALT), IST (interconnect stress testing), and vibration testing — each targeting specific failure mechanisms such as plated-hole fatigue, solder-joint creep, or CAF growth.

Resin Content

The percentage by weight of resin in a prepreg or laminate material, with the balance being glass reinforcement. Prepregs are available in various resin-content levels — low (40-50%), medium (50-60%), and high (>60%) — which determine the amount of resin available to flow and fill gaps during lamination. Higher resin content provides better gap-filling capability but results in a thicker dielectric layer after pressing, affecting impedance calculations.

Rise Time

The time required for a digital signal to transition from its low logic level to its high logic level (typically measured from 10% to 90% of the voltage swing). Signal rise time, rather than clock frequency, determines whether transmission-line effects are significant: when the rise time is comparable to or shorter than the round-trip propagation delay of the interconnect, reflections must be managed through controlled-impedance design and proper termination.

Routing Density

A measure of the number of traces that can be routed per unit area or per layer of a PCB, determined by the minimum line width, minimum spacing, via size, and component pitch. High routing density — achievable with HDI technologies and fine-line processing — enables more functionality in a given board area but increases fabrication cost and places tighter demands on registration, etching, and plating uniformity.

Round-Robin Testing

A quality assurance and capability-assessment methodology in which the same set of test samples is measured by multiple laboratories, operators, or machines to evaluate measurement consistency and identify sources of variation. In PCB manufacturing, round-robin testing of impedance coupons, microsections, and solderability tests ensures that the fabricator's measurement systems are accurate and that results correlate with independent reference laboratories.

Rinsing

A critical cleaning step between chemical processes in PCB fabrication — after etching, after plating, after developing, after stripping — in which deionised water removes residual chemistry from the board surface and holes. Inadequate rinsing leaves ionic contamination that can cause corrosion, poor solderability, or reduced insulation resistance in the finished board; rinse-water quality is monitored by resistivity measurement.

RoHS vs REACH

Two complementary EU regulations affecting PCB materials: RoHS restricts specific hazardous substances (lead, mercury, cadmium, etc.) in electrical and electronic equipment; REACH (Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals) regulates chemicals more broadly across all products. A PCB assembly that is RoHS-compliant may still contain substances of very high concern (SVHCs) that require declaration under REACH, particularly in adhesives, flame retardants, and plating additives.

S

Solder Paste

A viscous suspension of microscopic solder-alloy spheres (typically 15-45 um diameter for Type 3-5 powders) dispersed in a flux medium, printed through a stencil onto PCB pads to provide both the solder and flux for SMT reflow soldering. Solder-paste selection — alloy composition, powder particle size (Type), and flux activity — directly affects printability, slump resistance, tack time, and solder-joint voiding, and must be matched to the stencil design and reflow profile.

SMT (Surface Mount Technology)

The assembly technology in which components are mounted directly onto the surface of a PCB rather than inserted into holes, enabling smaller components, higher component density, double-sided assembly, and fully automated production. SMT is the dominant assembly technology for modern electronics; a typical smartphone PCB contains over 1,000 SMD components assembled at placement rates exceeding 50,000 components per hour.

SMD (Surface Mount Device)

An electronic component designed to be mounted directly onto the surface of a PCB — as opposed to through-hole components with wire leads inserted into holes. SMDs encompass resistors, capacitors, inductors, diodes, transistors, ICs, connectors, and electromechanical parts, in package sizes from 0201 (0.6 x 0.3 mm) to large BGAs exceeding 50 x 50 mm.

Single-Sided PCB

See One-Sided PCB. A PCB with a single copper conductor layer on one side of the insulating substrate, typically with no plated through-holes — the most basic and lowest-cost PCB construction used for high-volume, low-complexity products.

Silkscreen

See Legend. The ink layer printed on the outer surface of a PCB, typically white on a green solder-mask background, that carries component outlines, reference designators, polarity indicators, pin-1 marks, date codes, UL marks, and other human-readable information. The silkscreen is applied by screen printing or direct legend printing (DLP) and must not encroach onto solderable pads.

Slot

A non-circular hole — rectangular, oval, or any elongated shape — milled or routed into a PCB to accommodate a component or mechanical feature that cannot fit into a round hole. Slots are defined in the mechanical layer and produced by CNC routing operations; plated slots require careful process control to ensure copper coverage on the slot walls.

Solder Bridge

An unintended connection of solder between two adjacent pads or conductors, forming an electrical short circuit. Solder bridges are the most common SMT assembly defect and are caused by excessive solder-paste deposition, inadequate solder-mask dam width, component misalignment, or insufficient reflow-profile control. AOI systems detect solder bridges as part of standard post-reflow inspection.

Solder Ball

In BGA packaging, the small sphere of solder alloy (typically 0.25-0.76 mm diameter) that forms each interconnection point. In SMT assembly, a solder ball is also an undesired small sphere of solder that splatters onto the board during reflow and may cause a short circuit if it lodges between adjacent conductors. IPC-A-610 limits the number and location of stray solder balls.

Solder Joint

The metallurgical connection formed between a component terminal and a PCB pad after the solder has wetted both surfaces, flowed to form a fillet, and solidified. The quality of every solder joint in an assembly — as evaluated by shape, wetting angle, fillet height, surface texture, and void content per IPC-A-610 — determines the electrical and mechanical reliability of the finished product.

Standoff

The vertical distance between the underside of a component body and the PCB surface, determined by the solder-joint height for SMD components and by lead-forming or spacer elements for through-hole components. Adequate standoff enables cleaning under the component, facilitates visual inspection, and provides stress relief during thermal cycling; insufficient standoff can trap flux residues and promote corrosion.

Stencil

See Paste Stencil. A precision metal foil with apertures corresponding to every SMD pad on a PCB, through which solder paste is deposited onto the pads by a squeegee-driven printing process. Stencil thickness, aperture size and shape, and sidewall smoothness are the primary factors influencing paste-transfer efficiency and the consistency of the printed solder-paste volume.

Stripline

A controlled-impedance transmission-line configuration in which a signal trace is embedded between two reference planes (typically ground above and below) within the PCB stackup. Stripline offers superior shielding compared to microstrip — the signal is completely enclosed, reducing EMI and crosstalk — but requires narrower traces for the same impedance (because the dielectric is entirely within the laminate, with a higher effective Dk than microstrip).

Substrate

The insulating base material of a PCB — the fibreglass/epoxy composite in FR-4, the polyimide film in flex circuits, or the ceramic in hybrid circuits — that provides the mechanical foundation and electrical isolation for the conductive copper layers. The substrate's thermal, mechanical, and dielectric properties are fundamental to the PCB's performance and reliability.

Surface Finish

The metallic or organic coating applied to the exposed copper pads of a bare PCB to protect the copper from oxidation, preserve solderability during storage, and provide a solderable or wire-bondable surface during assembly. Common surface finishes — each with distinct cost, planarity, shelf-life, and reliability trade-offs — include HASL (SnPb and lead-free), ENIG, ENEPIG, immersion silver, immersion tin, OSP, and hard gold.

SIR (Surface Insulation Resistance)

The electrical resistance measured between two conductors across the surface of a PCB under controlled temperature and humidity conditions, used to assess the cleanliness and reliability of the board after assembly. Low SIR indicates ionic contamination (flux residue, plating salts, handling contamination) that can support electrochemical migration and dendritic growth under DC bias. IPC-J-STD-001 and IPC-TM-650 define SIR test procedures and pass/fail criteria.

Silver Migration

A specific type of electrochemical migration in which silver ions dissolve from a silver-bearing conductor or finish, migrate through absorbed moisture under DC electric field, and electrodeposit as metallic silver dendrites that can short-circuit adjacent conductors. Silver migration is a known reliability concern for immersion-silver surface finishes and silver-epoxy die-attach materials in high-humidity, high-DC-bias environments.

Splay

The fan-out of glass fibres at the exit side of a drilled hole, where individual filaments protrude into the hole barrel due to incomplete cutting by the drill bit. Excessive splay creates a rough hole wall that is difficult to plate uniformly and can trap plating-solution residues, leading to long-term reliability issues. Splay is controlled by optimising drill-bit geometry, spindle speed, and feed rate.

Sputtering

A physical vapour deposition (PVD) process in which atoms are ejected from a solid target material by bombardment with energetic ions and deposited onto the substrate. In PCB and IC-substrate fabrication, sputtering is used to deposit a thin, highly adherent seed layer of copper or other metal onto a surface prior to electrolytic plating, particularly for semi-additive processes where a very thin, uniform seed layer is required.

Stencil Thickness

The thickness of the metal foil from which a solder-paste stencil is fabricated, typically 0.1-0.15 mm (4-6 mil) for standard SMT, with thicker stencils (up to 0.2 mm) for through-hole solder-paste application (pin-in-paste) or step stencils. Stencil thickness is the primary determinant of solder-paste deposit volume: thicker stencils deposit more paste, which is beneficial for larger components and through-hole fill, but increase the risk of bridging on fine-pitch pads.

Step Stencil

A solder-paste stencil with two or more thickness regions — typically a thicker section for through-hole or large-component paste requirements and a thinner section for fine-pitch components — created by etching or coining the stencil foil to reduce thickness in defined areas. Step stencils enable a single printing operation to deposit different paste volumes for components with widely differing requirements, eliminating the need for multiple stencils or post-print dispensing.

Storage Condition

The prescribed environmental conditions — temperature, humidity, and duration — under which PCBs, components, and materials must be stored to prevent degradation. Bare PCBs are typically stored at 20-25 degC and <50% RH with sealed packaging; moisture-sensitive components (MSL-rated) require sealed moisture-barrier bags with desiccant and humidity indicator cards, with strict floor-life tracking after opening; solder paste requires refrigerated storage (0-10 degC) and controlled warm-up before use.

Soldering Iron

A handheld tool with a heated metal tip used for manual soldering of through-hole and SMD components. Modern soldering irons feature closed-loop temperature control (typically 300-400 degC), interchangeable tips in a wide variety of geometries optimised for different joint types, and rapid thermal recovery; proper tip selection, temperature setting, and dwell time are essential for producing acceptable solder joints without damaging the PCB or component.

Short Circuit

An unintended low-resistance electrical connection between two nets that should be electrically isolated, typically caused by a solder bridge, an unetched copper sliver, a metal particle, or internal CAF growth. Shorts are detected during bare-board electrical test and in-circuit test, and are one of the two fundamental PCB defect categories (along with open circuits).

Signal Integrity (SI)

The discipline of ensuring that electrical signals propagate through PCB interconnects with sufficient fidelity that the receiver can correctly interpret the transmitted data. SI analysis addresses reflections (from impedance mismatches), crosstalk (from electromagnetic coupling between adjacent traces), attenuation (from conductor and dielectric losses), inter-symbol interference, and timing skew — all of which intensify as data rates increase and voltage margins shrink.

Signal Layer

A copper layer in the PCB stackup primarily intended for routing signal traces, as opposed to plane layers dedicated to power or ground distribution. Signal layers are typically adjacent to reference plane layers so that transmission-line structures (microstrip or stripline) can be formed with predictable impedance.

Stacked Via

A vertical arrangement of microvias — one directly above another — spanning three or more layers in an HDI PCB, formed by plating a microvia, then drilling and plating the next microvia directly on top of the previous one in sequential build-up steps. Stacked microvias provide the highest interconnect density but require precise registration and plating control; staggered microvias are an alternative that is easier to manufacture but consumes more routing area.

Staggered Via

A via arrangement in HDI boards where microvias connecting adjacent layer pairs are offset from each other rather than stacked directly. Staggered vias are simpler to manufacture than stacked vias but consume additional routing area on each layer for the transition, and the horizontal interconnect between staggered vias may increase inductance and degrade signal integrity at very high frequencies.

Selective Soldering

An automated through-hole soldering process in which a miniature solder fountain or nozzle applies molten solder to specific through-hole component locations on an otherwise SMD-populated board, avoiding areas already populated with SMD components. Selective soldering is an alternative to wave soldering for mixed-assembly boards where only a few through-hole components need to be soldered, and it eliminates the need for pallets or wave-solder masking.

Snap-Off

The method of separating individual PCBs from a V-scored panel by flexing the panel along the score lines until the boards fracture apart. Snap-off depanelisation is fast and requires no tooling, but it induces mechanical stress in the PCB and components; sensitive or heavy boards are typically routed with breakaway tabs and separated by a depanelisation router or blade fixture to minimise stress.

Stackup Impedance Calculator

A software tool — either built into EDA packages or available as a standalone utility — that computes the characteristic impedance of microstrip, stripline, and differential-pair transmission lines given the dielectric thickness, Dk, copper weight, and trace geometry. Fabricators provide their own stackup calculators with parameters calibrated to their specific material sets and processes, and the designer's calculated trace width must be reconciled with the fabricator's recommended width.

Solderability Testing

A test method per J-STD-003 and IPC-TM-650 that evaluates a PCB pad's ability to be wetted by molten solder after specified ageing conditions (typically steam ageing or elevated-temperature exposure). The test uses a solder dip-and-look method (assessing wetting coverage) or a wetting-balance instrument (measuring wetting force vs. time), and is performed on test coupons or sacrificial boards from each production lot.

Shear Strength

The mechanical force required to cause a solder joint to fail in a shearing mode (force applied parallel to the plane of the joint), measured in Newtons or grams-force. Ball shear testing (BGA), lead shear, and component shear testing are used to qualify solder-joint integrity and to evaluate the effects of thermal ageing, multiple reflow cycles, and material selection on joint strength.

Squeegee

The blade — typically metal (stainless steel) or polyurethane — that is drawn across a solder-paste stencil during printing to force paste into the apertures and shear off excess paste flush with the stencil surface. Metal squeegees provide consistent paste volume over long print runs; polyurethane squeegees conform to slight board irregularities. Squeegee pressure, speed, and angle directly affect paste-transfer efficiency and print quality.

Spacing (Clearance)

The minimum distance between adjacent copper features on a PCB layer (see Line Spacing). Conductor spacing is the complement to line width: together they define a fabricator's patterning capability. Adequate spacing is essential for preventing dielectric breakdown at operating voltage, for suppressing crosstalk between high-speed traces, and for providing manufacturing margin against etching variation and particulate defects.

Solder Mask Clearance

See Mask Expansion. The radial distance between the edge of the solder-mask opening and the edge of the copper pad it defines. Adequate solder-mask clearance ensures that no mask encroaches on the pad even with worst-case mask registration error; typical clearance values are 0.05-0.1 mm per side, resulting in a mask opening that is 0.1-0.2 mm larger than the copper pad.

T

Tg (Glass Transition Temperature)

The temperature range at which a PCB laminate transitions from a rigid, glassy state to a soft, rubbery state. This is the most critical thermal parameter for laminate selection: the Z-axis CTE increases 3-5x above Tg, driving plated-hole fatigue, and the material's mechanical modulus drops sharply. For lead-free assembly (peak reflow ~245-260 degC), a minimum Tg of 170 degC is recommended; for high-reliability applications, Tg > 170 degC is essential. Measured by DSC (Differential Scanning Calorimetry) or TMA (Thermomechanical Analysis) per IPC-TM-650.

Td (Decomposition Temperature)

The temperature at which a laminate material experiences 5% weight loss due to chemical decomposition (pyrolysis), measured by TGA (Thermogravimetric Analysis) per IPC-TM-650. Td indicates the absolute thermal limit of the material — the temperature beyond which irreversible chemical degradation occurs. Typical Td values are 310-350 degC for standard FR-4 and 350-390 degC for high-reliability laminates.

T260 / T288

The time (in minutes) that a laminate material can withstand exposure to 260 degC or 288 degC before experiencing delamination, measured by TMA. T260 and T288 are direct indicators of a laminate's ability to survive lead-free reflow and rework cycles without internal damage. Typical T260 values are >30 minutes for standard FR-4 and >60 minutes for high-reliability materials; T288 values are correspondingly shorter.

Through-Hole

A component-mounting technology in which component leads are inserted through plated holes in the PCB and soldered to the annular pads on the opposite side. Through-hole technology provides stronger mechanical attachment than SMT and is still used for connectors, transformers, electrolytic capacitors, and high-power components that are subject to mechanical stress or that are not available in surface-mount packages.

TDR (Time Domain Reflectometer)

An instrument that launches a fast-rise-time electrical pulse into a transmission line and analyses the reflections to determine the characteristic impedance along the line and to locate impedance discontinuities. In PCB manufacturing, TDR is the standard method for verifying the impedance of test coupons against the specified value, with measurement accuracy typically within +/-1-2 ohms.

Gold Tab / Edge Connector Tab

See Gold Finger and Edge Connector. The gold-plated contact pad on a PCB edge connector, fabricated with hard electrolytic gold over a nickel barrier for wear resistance through repeated mating cycles.

Tack Time

The period after solder-paste printing during which the paste retains sufficient tackiness (stickiness) to hold components in place before reflow. Tack time is determined by the flux formulation and ambient conditions (temperature and humidity); it is typically 4-24 hours for modern no-clean solder pastes. If tack time is exceeded, components may shift before reflow, causing misalignment and open or bridged solder joints.

Tenting

The practice of covering a via with solder mask — whether the mask bridges across a via opening (tented via) or fully fills and caps it (plugged and capped via) — to prevent solder from flowing into the via during wave soldering, to protect the via from contamination, and to prevent accidental shorting from test probes or neighbouring components. Via tenting is a standard DFM technique for vias not intended as test points.

Thermal Relief

A PCB design feature — typically four or more narrow traces connecting a pad to a large copper plane — that provides electrical connection while thermally isolating the pad from the plane. Thermal reliefs reduce the heat sinking effect of the plane during soldering, ensuring that the pad reaches soldering temperature quickly and evenly. Without thermal reliefs, the plane would conduct heat away so rapidly that the solder might not melt properly — a defect known as a cold joint.

Thermal Pad

A large exposed copper pad on the underside of a component package (typically a QFN, DFN, or power package) or on the PCB, designed to conduct heat from the component into the PCB for dissipation. The thermal pad on the PCB typically incorporates an array of thermal vias that conduct heat to a copper plane or an external heatsink, and the stencil design for the thermal pad uses a grid of smaller apertures rather than a single large opening to prevent excessive solder volume and voiding.

Thermal Conductivity

A material property (units: W/mK) that quantifies the ability of a material to conduct heat. FR-4 has very low thermal conductivity (~0.3 W/mK through the thickness), which is why thermal vias and metal-core boards are used when significant heat must be dissipated. Alumina ceramic substrates offer ~24-30 W/mK; aluminium MCPCB dielectrics offer ~1-10 W/mK; copper (the metal) offers ~390 W/mK — several orders of magnitude better than any PCB laminate.

Thieving

The addition of non-functional copper patterns — typically small dots, squares, or grids — to areas of a PCB layer that would otherwise be bare laminate, to balance the copper density across the panel. Thieving promotes uniform electroplating current density, more consistent etching, and better laminate dimensional stability during pressing. The thieving patterns are electrically isolated from active circuitry.

Tin Whisker

A single-crystal filament of tin that spontaneously grows from a pure tin surface — such as an immersion-tin or electroplated-tin finish — over periods of weeks to years, potentially creating a short circuit between adjacent conductors. Tin whiskers are a well-documented reliability risk for pure-tin finishes; mitigation strategies include the addition of a small percentage of lead (historically, now restricted by RoHS), annealing, conformal coating, or using alternative finishes such as ENIG or immersion silver.

Tooling Hole

A non-plated hole — typically 2.0-4.0 mm diameter — located in the panel rails or at defined positions on the PCB, used to align the board on fabrication and assembly tooling pins. Tooling holes are the primary mechanical reference for all processes from drilling through assembly and test, and their positional accuracy directly affects the registration precision of every subsequent operation.

Trace

A continuous path of copper on a PCB layer that forms an electrical connection between two or more points (pads, vias, or component pins). The trace's width, thickness, and length determine its resistance, current-carrying capacity, and — in conjunction with the adjacent dielectric and reference plane — its characteristic impedance for high-speed signals.

Trace Width

The nominal width of a copper trace, typically measured at the base of the trace (at the copper-laminate interface). Trace width, together with copper thickness (weight), determines the trace's DC resistance and current-carrying capacity, while trace width relative to dielectric thickness determines characteristic impedance. Minimum trace width is a key measure of a fabricator's capability.

Type 4 PCB

An IPC-6012 classification for HDI PCBs with blind and/or buried vias and plated microvias. Type 4 encompasses the highest-complexity printed board constructions using sequential lamination with multiple levels of stacked or staggered microvias. Type 4 qualification requires demonstrated capability for fine-line etching, laser drilling, and microvia plating reliability per IPC-6016.

Thermocouple

A temperature sensor consisting of two dissimilar metal wires joined at a measurement junction, producing a voltage proportional to the temperature difference between the junction and a reference point. Thermocouples are attached to a PCB assembly at multiple locations to instrument (profile) the reflow oven and verify that every region of the board reaches the required temperatures without exceeding component limits.

Tab Routing

A panelisation method in which the individual PCB outline is routed (cut) almost completely, leaving only small breakaway tabs connecting the board to the panel frame. Tab routing produces cleaner board edges than V-scoring and allows non-rectangular board outlines, but is slower and requires more complex toolpaths. The tabs are typically perforated with mouse bites to facilitate clean depanelisation.

Test Coupon

See the C-section definition. A purpose-designed test structure on the fabrication panel that is used for destructive and non-destructive evaluation of manufacturing quality — including microsectioning, solderability testing, peel-strength measurement, and impedance verification — without sacrificing a production PCB.

Thermal Conductivity of PCB Laminate

The through-thickness thermal conductivity of standard FR-4 (~0.25-0.35 W/mK) is extremely poor compared to metals, making the PCB a significant thermal barrier between components and ambient cooling. Thermal vias — arrays of plated through-holes filled or unfilled — create copper paths through the laminate thickness that reduce the effective thermal resistance by one to two orders of magnitude, enabling conduction of heat from component thermal pads to internal or external copper planes.

Thin Core

A laminate core with a thickness of 0.1 mm (4 mil) or less, used in HDI stackups to create thin dielectrics for controlled impedance and to reduce total board thickness. Thin cores are fragile and require careful handling to prevent cracking during inner-layer processing; they are typically processed on automated handling systems or using carrier frames.

Tombstoning (Manhattan Effect)

A solder defect in which a small two-terminal SMD component (typically a chip resistor or capacitor) stands up on one end during reflow, resembling a tombstone. It is caused by unbalanced wetting forces when one end of the component reflows before the other — the earlier-melting end's solder surface tension pulls the component vertical. Tombstoning is prevented by symmetrical pad design, uniform heating across the component, and optimised stencil-aperture geometry to balance paste volumes on both ends.

Trace Resistance Calculator

A design utility — available in EDA tools and online — that computes the DC resistance of a PCB trace given its length, width, and copper weight, using the resistivity of copper and accounting for temperature. The calculator output determines whether the trace's voltage drop is acceptable for the load current and whether the trace can carry the required current without exceeding the maximum allowed temperature rise per IPC-2152 guidelines.

Tolerance Stack-Up

The cumulative effect of individual dimensional tolerances — for etch factor, laminate thickness, registration, drill position, and material Dk — on a critical PCB parameter such as impedance or annular ring. A proper tolerance stack-up analysis uses worst-case or statistical (RSS) methods to verify that the specified tolerances combine to yield an acceptable variation in the critical parameter, and is an essential part of DFM review for controlled-impedance and high-reliability designs.

Tg-less Materials

Laminate materials — such as PTFE-based RF substrates and certain polyimide formulations — that do not exhibit a distinct glass transition temperature within the range of PCB processing and operating temperatures. Tg-less materials offer excellent dimensional stability across the full temperature range and are favoured for applications involving wide temperature excursions, such as downhole drilling, space, and under-hood automotive electronics.

U

UL (Underwriters Laboratories)

A global safety certification organisation that tests and certifies products for compliance with safety standards. For PCB laminates, the UL 94 standard classifies materials by flammability — V-0 being the most stringent (self-extinguishing within 10 seconds, no flaming drips) and a universal requirement for PCBs in products sold commercially. Fabricators are certified to apply the UL mark to boards built with UL-recognised materials per UL 796 (the PCB standard).

Underwriter Laboratories

See UL. The formal name of the organisation. Every PCB fabricator's UL file number (e.g., Huaxing E354321) is marked on compliant boards and allows the customer to verify that the boards were produced in a UL-certified facility using UL-recognised materials.

UV Cure

A polymerisation process in which ultraviolet light initiates and drives the cross-linking reaction that transforms a liquid or B-stage coating into a fully cured solid film. In PCB manufacturing, UV curing is used for solder mask (both as a primary cure mechanism for UV-cure masks and as a tack-cure step before thermal final-cure for LPI masks), legend inks, and conformal coatings.

Ultrasound

High-frequency acoustic energy (typically 20-40 kHz) used in PCB cleaning (ultrasonic cleaning), in wire bonding (ultrasonic bonding where the ultrasonic vibration scrubs the bond surfaces to promote interdiffusion), and in non-destructive testing (acoustic microscopy for detecting delamination, voids, and cracks within components and assemblies).

Underfill

A liquid epoxy encapsulant that is dispensed along one or two edges of a flip-chip or BGA component after soldering and drawn under the component by capillary action, then cured to form a solid layer that mechanically reinforces the solder joints. Underfill distributes the CTE-mismatch stress across the full component area rather than concentrating it in the solder joints, dramatically improving thermal-cycle reliability for large die and packages.

Unsupported Hole

A drilled hole in a PCB that has no copper plating on its walls — effectively a synonym for NPTH (Non-Plated Through-Hole), used for mechanical mounting and alignment purposes. The term emphasises that the hole wall lacks the structural reinforcement that copper plating provides to a PTH.

UL 94 V-0

The most stringent flammability classification in the UL 94 standard for plastic materials, requiring that a vertically oriented test specimen self-extinguishes within 10 seconds after flame removal and produces no flaming drips. V-0 is the universal minimum requirement for PCB laminates in commercial products; the "FR" in FR-4 stands for "Flame Retardant" and denotes compliance with UL 94 V-0.

UL Marking

The marking applied to a PCB — typically on the silkscreen layer — that identifies the fabricator's UL file number, the laminate material designation, and the flammability rating. The UL marking provides traceability to the certified manufacturing facility and materials, and its presence is a contractual requirement for PCBs destined for UL-listed end products.

Ultrasonic Cleaning

A PCB cleaning method that uses high-frequency sound waves (typically 20-40 kHz) to create cavitation bubbles in a cleaning solution; the implosion of these bubbles at the board surface provides mechanical scrubbing that dislodges flux residues, solder balls, and particulate contamination from under low-standoff components and inside plated through-holes. Ultrasonic cleaning is effective for densely populated assemblies but can damage sensitive MEMS devices and some crystal oscillators; compatibility must be verified before use.

V

Via

A plated through-hole (or laser-drilled microvia) whose primary purpose is to electrically connect traces on different layers of a multilayer PCB, rather than to serve as a component-lead mounting hole. Vias come in three fundamental types: through vias (connecting from top to bottom), blind vias (connecting an outer layer to one or more inner layers), and buried vias (connecting two or more inner layers without reaching the surface).

Via-In-Pad

A design technique in which a via is placed directly within the land area of a surface-mount pad, typically for BGA escape routing where there is no room for a dog-bone fanout via between pads. Via-in-pad requires the via to be filled (with conductive or non-conductive epoxy) and planarised, then plated over (capped) to create a flat, solderable pad surface. Without filling and capping, solder would wick down into the via during reflow, starving the solder joint.

Via Fill

The process of filling the barrel of a via with a material — typically conductive epoxy (for thermal and electrical continuity), non-conductive epoxy (for mechanical support under a pad), or solid copper (by extended electroplating) — to create a planar surface. Via fill is mandatory for via-in-pad applications and is also used to eliminate entrapped air or chemistry in the via barrel that could outgas or corrode over time.

Via Tenting

See Tenting. The covering of a via opening with solder mask to prevent solder ingress, contamination, and accidental shorting. Via tenting is achieved by bridging the solder-mask opening completely over the via pad, and is standard practice for all vias that do not serve as test points or that are not in pad.

V-Score / V-Groove

A method of panelising PCBs by cutting a V-shaped groove approximately one-third of the way through the board thickness from both the top and bottom along the boundaries between individual boards. After assembly, the boards are separated by snapping along the score line. V-scoring is efficient for rectangular board arrays but cannot be used for non-straight outlines, and the residual edge after snapping is rougher than a routed edge.

V-Cut

See V-Score. The actual cutting operation that produces the V-shaped grooves for panel depanelisation. V-cut depth is critical — too deep and the panel may break prematurely during handling; too shallow and the depanelisation force will be excessive and may damage components or the board.

Vacuum Lamination

A multilayer PCB pressing process performed in a vacuum chamber to eliminate air and volatiles that would otherwise be trapped between layers and cause voids or delamination. Vacuum lamination provides superior void-free bonding compared to atmospheric pressing and is standard for high-layer-count, high-reliability, and RF/microwave PCBs where any laminate void could compromise performance or reliability.

Void

An empty space or cavity within a material or interface where material should be present — laminate voids (air pockets within the dielectric), plating voids (gaps in the copper barrel), and solder-joint voids (gas bubbles trapped in the solder fillet or BGA ball). Voids reduce mechanical strength, thermal conductivity, and in some cases electrical continuity; acceptance criteria for each type are defined in the relevant IPC standards.

Vapour Phase (Condensation) Soldering

A reflow soldering process in which the PCB assembly is immersed in a saturated vapour of a high-boiling-point perfluorinated fluid (typically Galden, boiling at ~230-240 degC). The vapour condenses on the cooler PCB surfaces, transferring its latent heat of vaporisation and heating the board rapidly and uniformly to the fluid's boiling point. Vapour-phase soldering provides inherently precise peak-temperature control and oxygen-free atmosphere, making it suitable for complex assemblies with large thermal mass variations, though it is slower and more expensive than convection reflow.

Varistor (Variable Resistor / VDR)

A voltage-dependent resistor — typically a metal-oxide varistor (MOV) — whose resistance decreases sharply above a threshold voltage, used on PCBs for transient voltage suppression (surge protection). Varistors are placed across power inputs and signal lines to clamp voltage spikes and protect downstream circuitry, and their relatively high parasitic capacitance must be accounted for in high-speed signal-path designs.

VSWR (Voltage Standing Wave Ratio)

A measure of impedance matching on a transmission line, defined as the ratio of the maximum to minimum voltage along the line due to standing waves formed by reflections from an impedance discontinuity. A VSWR of 1.0:1 represents perfect matching (no reflection); values above 2.0:1 indicate significant mismatch. VSWR is widely used as an RF PCB acceptance parameter for antenna feed lines, filters, and connector transitions.

Visual Inspection

The human examination of a PCB or assembly — with the unaided eye or with magnification (typically 4-10x) — to detect visible defects such as solder bridges, insufficient solder, misaligned components, damaged traces, solder-mask defects, and contamination. Visual inspection is the first and most universal quality gate in both bare-board and assembly manufacturing, and IPC-A-610 and IPC-6012 define the acceptance criteria. AOI augments but does not fully replace skilled human visual inspection for detecting subtle or unusual defects.

Via Cap

The layer of copper plated over the top of a filled via to create a planar, solderable surface that is indistinguishable from a standard SMD pad. Via capping is the final step in the via-in-pad process after filling and planarising; the cap must be pinhole-free and adequately thick for the intended soldering or probing operations.

Voltage Drop

The reduction in voltage along a PCB trace or plane caused by the trace's DC resistance (V = I x R). For high-current circuits — power distribution, motor drives, LED arrays — the voltage drop in PCB traces must be calculated and kept within acceptable limits (typically <2-5% of the supply voltage) by selecting adequate trace widths and copper weights. Excessive voltage drop causes functional problems (reduced voltage at the load) and generates Joule heating that raises the trace temperature.

Via Aspect Ratio

The ratio of the PCB thickness (or the dielectric thickness between the layers being connected) to the diameter of a via. For through vias, a 1.6 mm thick board with a 0.2 mm via has an aspect ratio of 8:1; for microvias, the aspect ratio is typically 1:1 or less. Higher aspect ratios challenge the ability to deposit uniform copper on the via walls and are limited by the fabricator's plating capability — typically 10:1 for standard processes and up to 15:1 for advanced processes.

W

Wave Soldering

A high-speed through-hole soldering process in which the underside of a component-populated PCB is passed over a pumped wave (or dual waves) of molten solder, simultaneously soldering all through-hole component leads on the bottom side of the board. Wave soldering is used for through-hole assembly and for mixed-technology boards where SMD components on the bottom side are glued in place before wave exposure. The wave profile, conveyor speed, preheat, and flux application are critical parameters for achieving complete hole fill without bridging.

Warpage

Out-of-plane deformation of a PCB caused by residual stress from the lamination process, asymmetric copper distribution, or CTE mismatch between materials. Warpage during reflow can cause open solder joints (as the board curves away from the component leads) and is limited by IPC-6012 to 0.75% of the diagonal dimension for SMT boards. Balanced stackups — symmetric copper distribution and material placement about the board centreline — are the primary design measure for warpage control.

Wire Bonding

A microelectronic interconnection process in which fine gold, aluminium, or copper wire (typically 15-75 um diameter) is ultrasonically or thermosonically bonded between a semiconductor die pad and a substrate or package lead. The PCB pads for wire bonding must have a suitable surface finish — typically ENIG or ENEPIG — and the bonding surface must be free of contamination and oxidation to achieve reliable bond strength.

Wicking

In PCB fabrication, the capillary migration of a liquid — typically solder, flux, or plating solution — along the glass fibres at the wall of a drilled hole, caused by poor resin-to-glass bonding. Wicking of plating solution into the laminate can cause internal short circuits and CAF; in assembly, solder wicking up a component lead away from the pad indicates poor joint formation. In a positive sense, the capillary action of molten solder up through a plated hole (hole fill) is essential for reliable through-hole solder joints.

Wetting

The ability of molten solder to spread across, adhere to, and form a metallurgical bond with a metal surface. Good wetting — characterised by a low contact angle (<30 degrees), a smooth, concave fillet, and complete coverage of the pad or lead — is essential for reliable solder joints. Wetting is measured by the contact angle and spread area in solderability testing per J-STD-003; non-wetting and de-wetting are both defect conditions.

WIP (Work In Progress)

Partially completed PCBs or assemblies at an intermediate stage of the manufacturing process — after drilling but before plating, after etching but before solder-mask application, after placement but before reflow, etc. WIP inventory levels are a key operations metric: excessive WIP indicates bottlenecks or quality hold-ups, while insufficient WIP risks starving downstream operations.

Working Side

The primary side of a PCB — typically the top or component side — that is the main focus of a particular operation. In assembly, the working side is the side currently being populated or soldered; in design, the working side is the active layer being routed or edited. The term distinguishes the side of current interest from the opposite (secondary) side.

Woven Glass

The reinforcement fabric in PCB laminates, produced by weaving fine glass-fibre yarns into a regular crisscross pattern. Common glass-weave styles include 106, 1080, 2116, and 7628, which differ in yarn-bundle size, thread count, and thickness. The weave pattern creates the fibre-weave effect — a microscopically non-uniform dielectric medium that affects high-speed signal propagation — and its thickness and resin content determine the laminate's mechanical and electrical properties.

Weave Exposure

A condition in which the glass-fibre reinforcement of a laminate becomes visible at the surface of a PCB, typically due to resin recession during processing, aggressive scrubbing, or ablation. Exposed glass fibres can wick moisture and plating solutions and can create micro-creepage paths for high-voltage circuits. Weave exposure beyond minor cosmetic levels is a rejectable defect for high-reliability applications.

Wire Harness

An assembly of discrete wires, cables, and connectors bundled and routed to interconnect electrical sub-systems. Wire harnesses are an alternative to flex PCBs and rigid-flex PCBs for board-to-board and system-level interconnection; they are more flexible and reworkable but heavier, bulkier, and more labour-intensive to assemble. Harnesses are increasingly replaced by flex circuits in volume applications where space and weight are priorities.

WEEE Directive

The EU Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment Directive (2012/19/EU) that mandates the collection, treatment, recycling, and environmentally sound disposal of electronic waste. PCB manufacturers and assemblers must ensure that their products and processes comply with WEEE requirements for marking, take-back, and recyclability, often documented through material declarations and lifecycle-analysis data.

Water Wash

A post-soldering cleaning process that uses deionised water — sometimes with saponifiers or surfactants — to remove flux residues from PCB assemblies. Water-wash cleaning is used with water-soluble (OA) fluxes that leave corrosive residues and must be completely removed, and is performed in inline or batch washers with spray, immersion, and drying stages. Ionic cleanliness after washing is verified by resistivity-of-solvent-extract (ROSE) testing per IPC-TM-650.

Wetting Balance

A precision laboratory instrument that measures the wetting force between a molten solder bath and a test specimen (PCB coupon or component lead) as a function of time. The wetting-balance curve — force vs. time — quantifies solderability objectively: a rapid rise to a high equilibrium force indicates excellent wetting; a slow rise or low force indicates marginal solderability. Wetting-balance testing per J-STD-003 is the gold-standard method for evaluating surface finishes and component solderability.

Working Voltage

The maximum continuous voltage that a PCB is designed to sustain between any two conductors, used to determine the required conductor spacing (clearance and creepage) per IPC-2221 or the applicable safety standard. Working voltage may be DC, AC RMS, or a combination; designs with voltages above approximately 60V DC or 30V AC RMS enter the realm where safety-critical spacing rules apply and may require specific laminate CTI ratings and HiPot testing.

Wave-Solder Pallet

A custom fixture — typically machined from a high-temperature composite material — that holds a PCB during wave soldering, masking areas that must not contact the solder wave (previously reflowed SMD components, gold edge connectors, non-solderable surfaces) while exposing only the through-hole component leads to be soldered. Pallets are a significant NRE cost but are essential for protecting SMD components on the bottom side of mixed-technology assemblies during wave soldering.

X

X-Ray Inspection

A non-destructive inspection technique that uses X-rays to visualise features hidden from optical view — BGA and QFN solder joints, via-in-pad fill quality, inner-layer registration, and internal laminate voids. X-ray systems for PCB inspection range from 2D transmission systems (for production-line BGA solder-joint assessment) to 3D computed tomography (CT) systems (for detailed failure analysis and advanced package inspection). X-ray inspection is the only method for evaluating solder joints under area-array packages without destructive cross-sectioning.

X-Y Coordinate

A location on a PCB defined by its horizontal (X) and vertical (Y) distances from a specified datum point, forming the fundamental positional reference system for all PCB fabrication and assembly operations. Every hole, pad, component, and test point has a unique X-Y coordinate; the pick-and-place machine uses X-Y coordinates from the centroid (placement) file to position each component, and the CNC drilling and routing machines use X-Y coordinates from the Excellon and rout files to position their tools.

Xenon Flash

A high-intensity pulsed light source used in some AOI systems and in photonic curing processes for printed electronics. Xenon flash lamps produce broad-spectrum visible and UV light in microsecond-duration pulses that can cure conductive inks without overheating the substrate, enabling PCB-like circuits to be fabricated on low-temperature materials such as PET and paper.

Y

Yield

The percentage of PCBs or assemblies produced that meet all acceptance criteria — in other words, the ratio of good units to total units produced. First-pass yield (FPY) measures the percentage of units that pass inspection and test on the first attempt (without rework); cumulative yield accounts for units recovered through rework. Yield is the most fundamental manufacturing-performance metric: higher yield directly translates to lower unit cost, shorter delivery, and more predictable production planning.

Yield Rate

The percentage of conforming units at a specific process step or across a sequence of steps. In PCB fabrication, key yield rates include inner-layer yield (after develop/etch/strip), lamination yield, drill yield, outer-layer yield, and final electrical-test yield. Tracking yield at each operation identifies process weaknesses and guides continuous-improvement efforts; world-class fabricators achieve overall yields above 98% for standard-technology PCBs.

Young's Modulus

A mechanical material property (also called the elastic modulus or tensile modulus, units: GPa or psi) that quantifies a material's stiffness — the ratio of stress to strain in the elastic (recoverable) deformation region. For PCB laminates, Young's modulus values of 17-25 GPa (along the glass fibres) determine the board's rigidity and its resistance to bending under load; the modulus is anisotropic in woven-glass laminates, being higher in the warp and fill (weave) directions than at 45 degrees to the weave.

Z

Z-Axis Expansion

The increase in thickness of a PCB laminate with increasing temperature, driven by the coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE) in the direction perpendicular to the board surface (the Z-axis). Z-axis expansion is the primary stressor on plated through-hole barrels during soldering and thermal cycling: the laminate expands much more than the copper plating (~50-70 ppm/degC vs. ~17 ppm/degC above Tg for standard FR-4), placing the copper barrel in tension, which cumulatively fatigues and eventually cracks the plating. High-Tg laminates reduce Z-axis expansion and are essential for lead-free assembly and high-reliability applications.

Zero Defect

A quality philosophy and operational target — particularly associated with automotive (IATF 16949) and aerospace (AS9100) supply chains — in which the goal is to have zero non-conforming units delivered to the customer. Achieving zero defects requires robust process control, statistical process monitoring, thorough inspection and test coverage, and a culture of continuous improvement. In PCB manufacturing, zero-defect programmes focus on preventing defects at source (through process FMEA and control plans) rather than screening them out at final inspection.

Zinc Whisker

A single-crystal filament of zinc that can spontaneously grow from zinc-electroplated surfaces — historically a concern for electroplated zinc finishes on steel floor panels in data centres, where zinc whiskers could become airborne and short-circuit electronic equipment. In PCB contexts, zinc whiskers are relevant primarily for zinc-electroplated enclosures and fasteners used near or in contact with PCBs, particularly in high-reliability applications where any conductive filament could cause a short circuit. Tin whiskers are a more directly PCB-relevant phenomenon (see Tin Whisker).

Z-Axis Milling / Controlled Depth Drilling

A precision machining process that cuts or drills to a specific depth within the PCB thickness rather than through the entire board, used for creating blind cavities, counterbores, and controlled-depth vias. Z-axis milling requires precise Z-height calibration of the CNC machine and is used in advanced rigid-flex and cavity-board constructions where components are embedded within the PCB.
