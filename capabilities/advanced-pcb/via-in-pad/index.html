The Density Problem

What Via-in-Pad Is — and Why It Enables HDI

Traditional PCB routing requires a via, a trace, and a solder pad — three separate features consuming board real estate. On a 0.5mm-pitch BGA with 1,000+ balls, the breakout consumes every available layer and still constrains routing. Via-in-pad eliminates the third feature entirely: the via is placed inside the solder pad, filled with epoxy, planarized flat, and plated over. The pad surface becomes indistinguishable from a standard SMD pad — solderable, flat, and fully compatible with automated SMT assembly.

Process & Applications

The Via-in-Pad Manufacturing Sequence

Via-in-pad adds four steps to the standard PCB fabrication flow: drill the via, fill with epoxy (vacuum-assisted to eliminate voids), planarize the surface (mechanical scrubbing or fly-cut to remove excess fill), and electroplate a copper cap over the filled via. The result is a pad surface that is indistinguishable from a standard SMD land — flat to within 5 µm, fully solderable, and compatible with all standard surface finishes.

  • BGA Breakout — 0.5mm, 0.65mm, and 0.8mm pitch BGAs. Eliminate dog-bone fanout and reduce layer count by 2-4 layers on high-pin-count devices.
  • QFN & LGA Landing Pads — Place thermal vias directly in the exposed pad for maximum heat transfer. Conductive fill preferred for power QFN packages.
  • RF & Microwave Boards — Shortest possible ground return path. Via-in-pad under RF IC ground paddles minimizes parasitic inductance.
  • Medical & Aerospace HDI — Class 3 HDI with via-in-pad for implantable devices, avionics processors, and mission-critical FPGA boards.
  • Wearable & IoT Miniaturization — Maximum component density on 4-8 layer rigid-flex with via-in-pad enabling single-sided placement on ultra-compact designs.
