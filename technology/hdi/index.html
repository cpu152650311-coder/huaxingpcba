HDI (High-Density Interconnect) is not a single technology — it's a family of PCB fabrication techniques that use laser-drilled microvias, sequential lamination, and fine-pitch routing to achieve interconnect densities impossible with conventional mechanical drilling. The defining difference: laser microvias (typically 75-100µm diameter) replace mechanically drilled vias (minimum 150-200µm), enabling pad sizes down to 200µm and via-to-via spacing down to 350µm.

At Huaxing PCBA, we manufacture HDI boards from 1+N+1 (one microvia layer on each side) to 3+N+3 (three sequential microvia layers). Our production capability spans smartphone main boards with 12-layer any-layer HDI, wearable rigid-flex with 75µm stacked microvias, and IoT sensor modules with 0.3mm total thickness. HDI is not a niche — it's now the default architecture for any design with a 0.5mm pitch or finer BGA.

HDI Architecture Levels Explained

HDI is classified by the number of sequential lamination cycles. Each cycle adds one microvia layer on each side of the core. The notation "M+N+M" means M microvia layers, N conventional core layers, M microvia layers on the opposite side. Understanding this architecture is essential for cost-optimized design — each additional microvia layer adds approximately 15-25% to the bare board cost.

Stacked vs. Staggered Microvias: The Critical Decision

When routing from layer 1 to layer 3 through a microvia stack, you have two choices: stack the vias directly on top of each other (stacked) or offset them (staggered). This single decision impacts reliability, cost, and routing density — and it's one of the most common points of confusion in HDI design.

When to Choose HDI: Cost vs. Density Analysis

HDI is more expensive per square centimeter than conventional through-hole PCB — but it can reduce total layer count, shrink board area, and eliminate the need for expensive high-layer-count conventional boards. The economic break-even depends on your specific design constraints.

Use 1+N+1 HDI When

BGA pitch ≤0.5mm or board area is constrained

A 0.5mm BGA cannot be escaped with conventional through-hole vias (minimum annular ring 125µm + minimum hole 200µm = 450µm pad, leaving 50µm for routing — impossible). 1+N+1 HDI with 75µm laser vias and 200µm pads solves this. Added cost vs. 4-layer conventional: ~20-30%. Typical applications: MCU+BLE modules, compact sensor boards.

Use 2+N+2 When

0.4mm BGA or double-sided component placement

0.4mm BGA with >400 pins requires two levels of microvia fan-out. Via-in-pad with copper-filled and planarized microvias becomes mandatory — no escape routing possible on outer layers alone. Added cost vs. 1+N+1: ~25-40%. Applications: smartphone application processors, FPGA modules, ADAS camera processors.

Stay Conventional When

BGA pitch ≥0.8mm, board area is not critical

If your finest BGA is 0.8mm pitch and your board has room for dog-bone fan-out, conventional through-hole PCB with 0.2mm mechanical drills will be 40-60% cheaper than equivalent HDI. Don't pay for HDI if you don't need it. The density argument only matters when conventional routing fails — test it in your CAD tool first.
