PCB Stackup Design:
Signal Integrity Starts Here.

The right stackup is the single most consequential design decision you make. Layer count, material selection, dielectric thickness, copper weight, and plane assignment determine impedance control, crosstalk, power delivery, and manufacturability — before you route a single trace.

Home / Technology / Stackup

The PCB stackup is the laminated sequence of copper signal and plane layers, separated by dielectric materials (prepreg and core), that forms the physical substrate of your circuit. Every electrical property that matters — characteristic impedance, insertion loss, crosstalk, power distribution network (PDN) impedance, and intra-pair skew — is fundamentally determined by the stackup geometry: layer order, dielectric thickness, copper weight, and material Dk/Df.

Our standard dielectric options range from 0.05mm prepreg (for tight coupling in power-ground pairs) to 1.5mm core (for structural rigidity in high-layer-count backplanes), with copper weights from 0.5oz (18µm) for fine-pitch signal layers to 2oz (70µm) for power planes and high-current traces. Stackup Design Principles

A well-designed stackup is not a random sequence of layers — it follows fundamental electromagnetic and mechanical principles that have been validated across decades of high-speed digital design. These four principles are non-negotiable for any design above 100MHz or with controlled-impedance requirements.

1. Signal-Ground Adjacency

Every signal layer must be adjacent to a continuous reference plane (ground preferred, power acceptable if properly decoupled). This ensures a well-defined return path directly beneath each trace, minimizing loop inductance and controlling characteristic impedance. When a signal changes reference planes, the return current must find a path through stitching capacitors or vias — if it cannot, the signal radiates and creates EMI. A common stackup mistake: routing high-speed signals on layer 3 of a 4-layer board where layer 2 is power (not ground) and layer 4 is ground — the return path on layer 2 is interrupted by plane splits, forcing return currents to take long detours. Rule: every high-speed signal layer must have an adjacent, uninterrupted ground plane.

2. Power-Ground Plane Pairs

Place power and ground planes adjacent with a thin dielectric (typically 0.1mm–0.2mm prepreg) to create planar capacitance. This inter-plane capacitance acts as a high-frequency decoupling capacitor with virtually zero inductance — it is the most effective PDN element you can design. The capacitance per square inch is C = ε₀ · εᵣ · A / d. With FR-4 (εᵣ ≈ 4.2) and 0.1mm dielectric, you get approximately 370pF/cm². At 1GHz, the impedance of this planar capacitance is approximately 0.43Ω/cm² — orders of magnitude lower than any discrete capacitor after accounting for mounting inductance. Rule: every power plane must be tightly coupled to a ground plane with ≤0.2mm dielectric.

3. Symmetric Construction for Warpage Control

Asymmetric stackups — where copper distribution, dielectric thickness, or material type differs between the top and bottom halves — create internal stresses during lamination and thermal cycling. These stresses manifest as board warpage (bow and twist), which causes tombstoning during SMT reflow, open joints on BGA corners, and reliability failures under thermal cycling. A symmetric stackup balances copper percentage, prepreg/core thickness, and material type about the geometric centerline. Rule: the stackup cross-section must be mirror-symmetric about the center of the board. For odd-layer-count boards (e.g., 3-layer), warpage is inherent — always prefer even layer counts.

4. Layer Count Optimization

Adding layers reduces routing density per layer and improves signal integrity (more room for ground fills, wider trace spacing), but increases cost non-linearly. Each additional layer pair adds approximately 15-25% to the bare board cost due to additional lamination cycles, material, and drilling complexity. The optimal layer count minimizes total cost while meeting these constraints: (a) every signal layer has an adjacent reference plane, (b) impedance targets are achievable with manufacturable trace widths (typically ≥75µm for standard etch), (c) routing density per layer is ≤50% to leave room for ground copper pour, and (d) via aspect ratio remains ≤10:1 for reliable plating. Rule: start with the minimum layer count that satisfies signal integrity and routability, not the maximum your budget allows.

Layer Count Decision Guide

Choosing the right number of layers is often the first stackup decision you face. The following guide maps application complexity to recommended layer count ranges. These are rules of thumb — the final answer comes from a proper routing study and impedance calculation in your CAD tool.

2–4 Layers

Simple Digital & Power Electronics

Best for designs with no high-speed signals (>50MHz), no controlled impedance, and BGA pitch ≥0.8mm. Typical applications: power supplies, LED drivers, basic MCU boards (Arduino-class), relay control, sensor interfaces. 4-layer stackup (signal-ground-power-signal) is the sweet spot — it provides a solid reference plane and PDN for very little cost premium over 2-layer. Avoid 4-layer on complex mixed-signal designs where one ground plane must be shared between analog and digital return currents.

6–10 Layers

Mixed-Signal & Mid-Complexity Digital

Required when you have DDR memory (DDR3/DDR4 with controlled 50Ω single-ended and 100Ω differential impedance), multiple power rails needing dedicated planes, or analog/digital partitioning. 6-layer (signal-ground-signal-signal-power-signal) is the minimum for DDR routing. 8-layer with 4 signal + 4 plane layers is the workhorse configuration for mid-range FPGA and industrial control. At 10 layers, you can dedicate entire layers to high-speed buses with adjacent ground planes on both sides (stripline routing).

12–20 Layers

High-Density Digital & Advanced FPGA

Necessary for large pin-count FPGAs (>800 pins), DDR4/DDR5 with 4+ byte lanes, 10Gbps+ SerDes links requiring stripline routing with precise impedance control, and mixed RF/digital designs. 12-layer provides 6 signal layers and 6 plane layers — enough for separate analog and digital ground planes, multiple power islands, and high-speed differential pairs on dedicated stripline layers. 16-layer is common for server motherboards and network switch line cards. 20-layer addresses the most demanding FPGA/ASIC designs with 20+ power rails.

20–32 Layers

Telecom Backplanes & Aerospace Systems

Reserved for the highest-density, highest-reliability applications: telecom core backplanes carrying hundreds of differential pairs across a passive interconnect, automated test equipment (ATE) with thousands of relay-switched channels, military avionics with redundant power planes and shielded secure channels, and particle physics data acquisition with picosecond timing distribution. These stackups require advanced materials (Megtron 6, Rogers), back-drilling to remove via stubs on high-speed layers, and aspect ratios approaching 10:1 for through-hole vias. Each additional layer pair above 20 adds approximately 10-15% cost premium.
